# Regulation of organic reactions by controlled microenvironments using designer condensates

## Abstract

This ERC Proof of Concept project addresses the critical need for sustainable drug synthesis by employing biomolecular condensates as tunable, organic solvent-free reaction systems. Our innovation lies in leveraging liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS) to create biocompatible microenvironments that enhance reaction efficiency, offering a versatile alternative to traditional organic solvent-based methods. The project's primary objectives include: (1) designing and characterizing peptide-based condensates with controlled architecture and material properties; (2) optimizing these condensates for specific drug synthesis reactions; and (3) demonstrating the scalability and applicability of the platform for a variety of chemical and enzymatic transformations. Our methodology focuses on precise control over reactant recruitment and catalytic activity within the condensates, guided by the hypothesis that the chemical composition of the peptide sequence dictates the condensate's ability to spatially regulate hydrophobic organic molecules and their chemical transformations. Expected outcomes include the development of a robust, scalable platform for green drug synthesis, validated through the synthesis of several model drugs. The project's impact extends to reducing environmental harm associated with pharmaceutical manufacturing, enhancing reaction efficiency, and promoting the adoption of sustainable practices in the chemical industry. The commercial viability of this technology stems from its potential to revolutionize drug synthesis, offering a cost-effective, environmentally friendly alternative to conventional methods, with applications spanning the pharmaceutical, biotechnology, and fine chemical sectors. This research aligns with the ERC Proof of Concept Grant by translating our prior discoveries in peptide condensate design into a commercially promising technology for sustainable chemical synthesis.

## Section 1a: The idea – Breakthrough Innovation potential

### 1a.i. Brief description of the idea to be taken to proof of concept

The core idea is to leverage biomolecular condensates, specifically peptide-based liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS) systems, as tunable microreactors for sustainable drug and macromolecule-drug conjugate synthesis in an organic solvent-free aqueous environment. This approach addresses the critical need for environmentally friendly practices in pharmaceutical manufacturing, offering a green alternative to traditional organic solvent-based methods. The innovation lies in the design and application of these condensates to spatially regulate hydrophobic organic molecules and control their chemical transformations, thereby enhancing reaction efficiency and conversion rates. The underlying technology builds upon the principles of micellar catalysis, but with enhanced versatility and tunability achieved through precise control over the peptide sequence, condensate architecture, and material properties. This allows for the creation of dynamic, biocompatible microenvironments tailored to specific chemical and enzymatic reactions.

The technical description involves the design of peptide sequences that undergo LLPS to form condensates in aqueous solutions. These peptides are engineered to incorporate specific functional groups or catalytic moieties that facilitate the desired chemical reactions within the condensate. The partitioning of reactants into the condensate is controlled by their hydrophobicity and affinity for the peptide building blocks. The reaction rate and conversion are modulated by the chemical composition of the peptide sequence, which in turn influences the condensate architecture and material properties. Characterization of the condensates involves a combination of techniques, including microscopy, spectroscopy, and scattering methods, to determine their size, shape, composition, and internal structure. Reaction kinetics are monitored using standard analytical techniques to assess the efficiency and selectivity of the reactions within the condensates. The novelty of this approach stems from the use of biomolecular condensates as dynamic, tunable microreactors for organic synthesis. Unlike traditional micellar catalysis, which relies on fixed micelle structures, our condensates offer a high degree of flexibility in terms of chemical composition, architecture, and material properties. This allows for precise control over the reaction environment and enables the optimization of reaction conditions for specific transformations. Furthermore, the use of peptides as building blocks ensures biocompatibility and sustainability, making this approach particularly attractive for pharmaceutical applications.

The potential applications of this technology are broad, encompassing the synthesis of a wide range of drugs and macromolecule-drug conjugates. By eliminating the need for organic solvents, this approach reduces waste generation and environmental harm, aligning with the principles of green chemistry. The enhanced reaction efficiency and conversion rates offered by the condensates can lead to significant cost savings and improved productivity. Moreover, the biocompatibility of the peptide-based systems makes them suitable for the synthesis of drugs and conjugates intended for in vivo applications. The scientific basis for this innovation lies in the principles of LLPS, micellar catalysis, and supramolecular chemistry. LLPS is a phenomenon in which a homogeneous solution separates into two or more distinct phases, one of which is enriched in a specific set of molecules. This process is driven by weak, non-covalent interactions, such as hydrophobic interactions, electrostatic interactions, and hydrogen bonding. Micellar catalysis is a technique in which organic reactions are carried out in the presence of micelles, which are aggregates of amphiphilic molecules in aqueous solution. The micelles provide a hydrophobic microenvironment that can enhance the reaction rate and selectivity. Supramolecular chemistry is the study of molecules that interact with each other through non-covalent interactions to form larger, more complex structures. By combining these principles, we can design peptide-based condensates that act as dynamic, tunable microreactors for organic synthesis.
