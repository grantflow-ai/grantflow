# Regulation of organic reactions by controlled microenvironments using designer condensates

## Abstract

This Proof of Concept project addresses the environmental challenges of traditional drug synthesis by employing biomolecular condensates as tunable, organic solvent-free reaction systems. The project aims to develop sustainable, biocompatible microenvironments that enhance reaction efficiency, focusing on antibody-drug conjugate (ADC) click reactions as model systems. Our central hypothesis posits that condensate composition dictates hydrophobic molecule spatial regulation, influencing reaction kinetics and conversion. We will optimize reactant partitioning, catalytic activity, and reaction selectivity within liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS)-based microreactors, eliminating reliance on organic solvents. Key methodologies include fluorescence correlation spectroscopy (FCS), confocal microscopy, NMR spectroscopy, HPLC, and mass spectrometry to characterize reactant and product encapsulation, reaction kinetics, and system sustainability. Expected outcomes include optimized conditions for ADC synthesis, quantitative analysis of reaction kinetics and conversion, and assessment of system sustainability via E-factor metrics and solvent usage. The project's impact lies in providing a green chemistry alternative to traditional methods, reducing environmental impact, and enhancing reaction efficiency. Successful commercialization will revolutionize drug synthesis, offering a scalable, versatile, and biocompatible platform for pharmaceutical manufacturing. This ERC Proof of Concept grant will facilitate the translation of our innovative research into commercially viable technology, addressing a critical need for sustainable practices in the pharmaceutical industry.

This innovative approach builds upon the principles of micellar catalysis, enabling organic reactions in solvent-free aqueous media, while offering enhanced versatility and tunability in chemical composition and reaction control through condensates.

The underlying technology involves the design and engineering of peptide-based condensates that can selectively recruit and compartmentalize reactants, facilitating chemical transformations within these confined microenvironments. The novelty lies in the ability to spatially regulate the partitioning of hydrophobic organic molecules and their chemical transformation, i.e., reaction rate and conversion, which is determined by the chemical composition of the condensate building block, i.e., peptide sequence. This, in turn, controls the condensate architecture and materials properties. The technical specifications include precise control over peptide sequence, charge, and hydrophobicity to tailor the condensate properties for specific reactions.

To explore the innovation potential of the ERC-funded research, a multi-faceted approach integrating experimental design, data analysis, and rigorous validation methods will be employed. This approach is structured around the objectives outlined previously, with a focus on demonstrating the feasibility and scalability of biomolecular condensates as dynamic, tunable, organic solvent-free reaction systems for drug and ADC synthesis. The experimental design is centered on the CuAAC click reaction as a model system for ADC synthesis. The initial phase, detailed in Objective 1, involves the development and optimization of reaction conditions within LLPS-based microreactors. This will be achieved through a series of experiments designed to systematically evaluate the impact of condensate composition on reactant partitioning, catalytic activity, and reaction selectivity. Specifically, two click reaction models will be employed, with reaction parameters optimized for aqueous environments. These parameters include, but are not limited to, catalyst concentration (0.1-10 mol%), reactant ratios (1:1 to 1:10 alkyne:azide), and reaction time (1-24 hours). The optimization process will involve varying these parameters in a controlled manner and monitoring the reaction progress using analytical techniques such as HPLC and mass spectrometry. Condensate formation will be optimized concurrently, focusing on the relationship between peptide sequence design and condensate properties. Peptide composition will be modified by varying charged residues (lysine, glutamic acid), hydrophobic domains (alanine, valine), and linker sequences (PEG, alkyl chains). The resulting condensates will be characterized using fluorescence correlation spectroscopy (FCS), confocal microscopy, and dynamic light scattering (DLS) to assess their size (10-1000 nm), stability (assessed by monitoring size and morphology over 24 hours), and morphology (spherical, elongated, irregular).

Objective 2 focuses on characterizing reactant and product encapsulation within condensates. The experimental design involves systematic variations in peptide sequence, charge, and hydrophobicity to assess their impact on phase separation and molecular partitioning. Encapsulation efficiency will be quantified using FCS, confocal microscopy, and mass spectrometry. Parameters affecting the selective retention of products versus reactants, including salt concentration and temperature, will be assessed to inform condensate design for optimized reaction kinetics. Specifically, salt concentrations ranging from 0-500 mM (NaCl or MgCl2) and temperatures from 4-50°C will be tested. The pH will also be varied from 5-9 to determine its effect on encapsulation efficiency.

Objective 3 is dedicated to characterizing CuAAC reaction kinetics and thermodynamics within peptide-based condensates. Reaction rates and activation energies will be quantified using NMR spectroscopy (25-75°C), mass spectrometry, and HPLC. The influence of condensate composition will be evaluated by varying peptide sequence, charge, and hydrophobicity. Reactant and product partitioning between dense and dilute phases will be determined to understand spatial organization effects on reaction efficiency. Microscopy analysis will be conducted to elucidate product localization in the two phases, providing visual confirmation of the reaction kinetics. The experimental design includes control reactions in standard organic solvents (DMSO, DMF) to assess the impact of the condensate environment on reaction kinetics.

Objective 4 focuses on analyzing system sustainability and scalability. Condensate stability and phase separation behavior will be assessed across scales using dynamic light scattering and microscopy over multiple reaction cycles. Energy efficiency will be compared to traditional methods through E-factor calculations and energy consumption (MJ/kg product).
