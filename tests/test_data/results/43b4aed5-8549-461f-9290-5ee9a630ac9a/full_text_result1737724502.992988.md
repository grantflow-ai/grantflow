# Project Summary

## General Audience Abstract
This melanoma research project focuses on addressing the critical challenge of brain metastases, which significantly reduce survival rates and quality of life for patients with advanced melanoma. By leveraging cutting-edge single-cell technologies and artificial intelligence, we aim to understand the complex interactions within the brain's tumor microenvironment. Our approach involves identifying key factors that suppress the immune system's ability to fight cancer, particularly the role of regulatory macrophages, and developing novel immunotherapies that can reverse this suppression. The project will design immunocytokines that target these cells, effectively transforming the tumor environment from immune-resistant to responsive. This translational science initiative will not only improve the treatment options but also facilitate the early detection and diagnosis of melanoma brain metastases, ultimately aiming to extend the life expectancy and enhance the quality of life for individuals affected by this devastating condition. The outcomes of this research hold significant promise for translating laboratory findings into effective clinical applications.

## Technical Abstract
This project hypothesizes that advanced single-cell technologies, including ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq coupled with AI-driven analysis, will facilitate a detailed understanding of the melanoma brain metastases (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME). Specifically, we aim to identify cytokines capable of remodeling the TME towards anti-tumor activity. This knowledge will inform the design of novel immunocytokines, combining the most promising cytokines with our anti-TREM2 antibody to modulate both myeloid and T and NK cell compartments. Furthermore, we hypothesize that our single-cell and AI analysis will pinpoint biomarkers, enabling the design of masking strategies to increase the specificity of the immunocytokine for the BM TME. Our methodology will involve developing orthotopic immunocompetent murine models for melanoma BMs, coupled with analysis of patient clinical samples. Single-cell atlases of immune, stromal, and tumor cells from human and murine BMs will be generated. We will use novel technologies for cell temporal tracking (ZMAN-seq, differentiation flows), cell-cell interactions (PIC-seq), and AI analysis of spatial transcriptomics (Stereo-seq). Cytokines identified in Aim 1 will be screened in vitro and in vivo using cytokine-secreting clones of brain tropic melanoma cell lines and in tumor spheroids co-cultured with immune cells, varying cytokine combinations. The most effective combinations will be used in vivo, using intracardial injections of B16 clones expressing the shortlisted cytokines, with tumor response monitored by MRI. The culmination of this methodology will be the design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines, incorporating potent cytokines into antibody-cytokine fusion proteins targeting tumor antigens highly expressed in melanoma BMs.

## Rationale and Fit with MRA Portfolio
This project directly addresses several key priorities within the MRA research portfolio, specifically focusing on improving outcomes for melanoma brain metastases (MBM), a significant area of unmet need. MBM occurs in up to 50% of advanced melanoma patients and is associated with a dismal prognosis and limited treatment options, despite recent advances in immunotherapy. Our research aims to transform the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) of MBM into an immunologically "hot" environment, thereby enhancing the efficacy of immunotherapies. This aligns with MRA's translational goals of developing novel therapeutic strategies that can be rapidly translated into clinical practice. By employing state-of-the-art single-cell technologies, including ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq, coupled with advanced AI analysis, we will gain unprecedented insights into the complex interactions within the MBM TME. This research has the potential to identify critical biomarkers and design highly specific tumor-targeting immunocytokines, addressing the current limitations of systemic immunotherapies which often fail in the brain due to the blood brain barrier (BBB) and immunosuppressive environment. The potential clinical impact of this project is substantial. By developing novel immunocytokine therapies, we aim to significantly improve survival rates and quality of life for patients with MBM. Our approach of simultaneously modulating different immune cell compartments offers a promising avenue to overcome treatment resistance. This innovative approach, if successful, has the potential to provide a first-in-class therapeutic modality that can be implemented in clinical trials, bringing substantial progress toward improving the care and prognosis of melanoma patients.

## Research Plan

### Research Objectives

#### Objective 1: Developing BM TME models with holistic, multimodal AI-driven analysis

The central hypothesis of Objective 1 posits that a comprehensive, multimodal analysis of the BM TME, leveraging temporal, spatial, and cell-cell interaction data, will reveal novel targets for immunotherapy. This objective is designed to fulfill several key goals: first, to develop a detailed understanding of immune cell dynamics in the BM TME; second, to identify critical cell-cell interactions that drive immunosuppression; and third, to map the spatial organization of the TME to uncover potential therapeutic access barriers. These goals align with SMART criteria by being Specific (focused on the BM TME), Measurable (through single-cell transcriptomics and spatial analysis), Achievable (using established and novel technologies), Relevant (to the development of novel immunotherapies), and Time-bound (within the project timeline). To achieve these goals, multiple tasks are defined. Task 1 will focus on the temporal understanding of immune activity in the BM TME by leveraging Zman-seq, a technology developed by the team, which introduces temporal information into single-cell RNA-seq data. This will allow us to resolve the differentiation trajectories of immune cells as they infiltrate the BM, revealing the molecular mechanisms behind their functional changes over time. Task 2 focuses on the characterization of cell-cell interactions in the BM TME using PIC-seq, a technology developed by the Giladi lab for identifying physically interacting cells. This task will provide a detailed understanding of the cross talk between different immune and tumor cell populations and identify key receptor-ligand pairs that drive immunosuppression. Task 3 will employ spatial transcriptomics using Stereo-seq to map the spatial distribution of immune and tumor cells within the BM TME, determining the proximity of different cell types to each other and to the tumor microenvironment. This data will be crucial for understanding how spatial organization affects immune responses and could identify locations inaccessible to certain therapies. These tasks share common methodologies, specifically scRNA-seq, which will be applied to all three tasks, and computational analysis to integrate and interpret the data, and there are variations for specific task needs such as Zman-seq for task 1, PIC-seq for task 2, and Stereo-seq for task 3. Equipment needed includes standard molecular biology equipment for cell culture, RNA extraction, sequencing, and advanced imaging equipment to support spatial transcriptomics. The expected results include: 1) a time-resolved map of immune cell differentiation within the BM TME; 2) a detailed network of cell-cell interactions, including the identification of key receptor-ligand pairs; and 3) a high-resolution spatial map of the cellular organization of the BM TME. These results will be directly linked to hypothesis validation by providing the data necessary to validate the temporal, spatial and cell-cell interactions driving immunosuppression in the TME. The successful completion of these tasks will provide critical targets for the development of novel immunotherapies. Objective 1 is critically dependent on the successful generation of murine BM models and collection of human patient samples, as outlined in the project proposal. The data generated in this objective will inform the cytokine screening in Objective 2 and the design of immunocytokines in Objective 3. A deeper understanding of the TME should enhance the effectiveness of the immunotherapies being developed in later stages of the project. This research will build upon single-cell analysis of in-vitro and in-vivo systems, such as the TREM2 knockout model, to generate a holistic picture of the BM TME.

##### Preliminary Results

Our preliminary data have demonstrated the feasibility of modulating macrophage activity using anti-TREM2 antibodies and cytokines. We have shown that combining an anti-TREM2 antibody with cytokines can induce a strong pro-inflammatory response in macrophages in vitro, suggesting a promising avenue for therapeutic intervention. Furthermore, we have successfully produced preliminary TREM2 immunocytokine model molecules, including an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein, demonstrating our capacity to engineer targeted immunotherapeutics. We have also developed and validated a masking strategy for the cytokine arm using blocking moieties that are responsive to TAM-specific proteases, showcasing our ability to create highly specific therapeutics that can be activated in the tumor microenvironment. The development of these immunocytokines and the masking strategy was validated in vivo.

In addition, we have leveraged Zman-seq, an in-vivo single-cell technology developed in our lab, to resolve immune cell state transitions in the tumor microenvironment. Using Zman-seq in a murine glioblastoma model, we have observed that cytotoxic NK cells rapidly transition to a dysfunctional state within 24 hours of tumor infiltration, characterized by upregulation of TGF-β signaling. We also found that infiltrating monocytes differentiate into immunosuppressive macrophages over 36-48 hours, marked by increased expression of Trem2, Il18bp, and Arg1. Importantly, our studies showed that treatment with anti-TREM2 antagonistic antibody redirected the monocyte-to-macrophage trajectory towards pro-inflammatory macrophages. These results highlight the dynamic nature of the TME and the potential of TREM2 as a therapeutic target. Zman-seq has also revealed a direct correlation between tumor exposure time and Trem2 expression, an immunosuppressive signaling hub for macrophages. Blocking TREM2 with an antagonistic antibody modified TAMs and evoked anti-tumor immunity.

These preliminary findings align with the objectives of this proposal by providing the methodology to study the TME and demonstrating the feasibility of targeting TREM2. The temporal resolution afforded by Zman-seq has allowed for the identification of critical time-dependent events in immune cell differentiation, which will guide the development of more effective immunotherapies. The technical challenges that remain include identifying additional cytokines that synergize with anti-TREM2, optimizing the immunocytokine design, and validating the efficacy of these immunotherapeutics in relevant preclinical models. The next steps will be to apply Zman-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq to the BM TME. These will determine the dynamic cellular interactions and spatial relationships in the brain metastases microenvironment, and identify effective immunotherapeutic strategies.

##### Risks and Alternatives

The primary risk for Task 1, employing Zman-seq, is the potential for low cell recovery or insufficient temporal resolution to capture rapid immune cell state transitions. Mitigation strategies include optimizing cell labeling protocols, increasing sequencing depth, and employing computational methods to extrapolate short time-scale dynamics. An alternative approach would be to use single-cell ATAC-seq to complement transcriptomic data. Task 2, utilizing PIC-seq, faces the risk of challenges in distinguishing specific cell-cell interactions from background signals or transient contacts. To mitigate this, we will implement stringent quality controls for cell isolation and crosslinking, and use higher sequencing depth. As an alternative, we can also utilize spatial transcriptomics to infer cell-cell interaction. Task 3, applying Stereo-seq, is at risk of low spatial resolution or limitations in the field of view, which may hinder the analysis of large or heterogeneous tissue regions. Mitigations include adjusting DNB spot density, optimizing tissue processing, and employing image analysis to enhance resolution. Alternatively, we can use multiplexed immunohistochemistry (IHC) to complement the spatial transcriptomic data. All tasks share a resource dependency on sequencing capacity and computational infrastructure. Failure to secure these resources would significantly impact the project timeline. To address this we will secure access to core facilities. The failure to generate robust murine models or obtain sufficient human samples would also impact the project progress, requiring the use of alternative datasets.

#### Objective 2: Preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models

This objective focuses on the preclinical evaluation of cytokines identified through Aim 1, utilizing orthotopic immunocompetent brain metastasis (BM) models. The central hypothesis is that specific cytokines, when introduced into the BM tumor microenvironment (TME), will shift the immune landscape from an immunosuppressive to an antitumor state, promoting tumor regression. The aim is to identify cytokines that, either alone or in combination, demonstrate the greatest therapeutic potential against melanoma brain metastases.

Specifically, Task 2.1 involves the screening of cytokines in the BM TME. Melanoma cell lines with brain tropism will be engineered to inducibly express a panel of candidate cytokines. This approach leverages the Amit group's expertise in genetic manipulation of tumor cells. These engineered cells will be used to generate tumor spheroids, which will then be co-cultured with immune cells. The addition of doxycycline will enable precise control of cytokine expression, allowing testing of various cytokine combinations. This in vitro screening phase will assess the impact of the cytokines on immune cell activation and tumor cell viability. The Weiss group will conduct in vivo validation studies based on the in vitro screening results.

Task 2.2 is focused on in-vitro validation. Cytokine combinations that demonstrate promising results in the initial screen will undergo rigorous validation in vitro. This will include detailed analysis of immune cell activation markers, cytokine secretion profiles, and tumor cell killing assays. These experiments will aim to identify the most effective cytokines and cytokine combinations for further in vivo testing. A key component will be the integration of single-cell analysis, which will provide a granular view of how cytokine treatment alters cellular composition and function within the tumor spheroids. The in vitro results from task 1.4 will be used to prioritize the cytokines for in vivo screening.

Task 2.3 is focused on in-vivo validation of selected cytokines. B16 melanoma clones expressing the most promising cytokines, or combinations, will be used to generate melanoma BMs via intracardial injections, a technique established by the Weiss group. Cytokine expression will be activated with doxycycline, enabling temporal control of cytokine release in the in vivo setting. Tumor response will be evaluated through MRI and survival measurements. The primary outcome will be the ability of the identified cytokines to suppress tumor growth and improve overall survival. This task will build upon the scRNA-seq data gathered in task 1.5 to provide a comprehensive analysis of the in vivo impact of cytokine modulation on the BM TME.

Dependencies include the outputs of Objective 1, specifically the selection of cytokines with high potential for antitumor activity in melanoma BMs identified in Tasks 1.1, 1.2, and 1.3, as well as the results from tasks 1.4 and 1.5 which provide a deeper understanding of immune cell states and trajectories. This objective will lay the groundwork for the development of targeted immunocytokines in Aim 3 by identifying the most effective cytokine candidates for tumor-specific delivery. Successful completion will lead to a data-driven selection of 3-5 cytokines for further development.

#### Objective 3: Design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines

This objective focuses on the design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines, building upon the insights gained from prior objectives. The central hypothesis is that by creating immunocytokines that target specific tumor antigens highly expressed in melanoma brain metastases (BMs) and deliver potent cytokines, we can shift the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) to an anti-tumor state. This will be achieved through the construction of antibody-cytokine fusion proteins that can effectively target melanoma BMs. The design process will leverage advanced technologies in protein design, including the selection of optimal peptide linkers, blocking moieties, and tumor-associated-macrophage (TAM)-specific cleavage sites, with computational optimization for structural stability.

The primary goal is to identify the most effective immunocytokine constructs for subsequent IND enabling studies. Task 3.1 focuses on designing fusion proteins and cleavage sites. This task involves the selection of the most suitable peptide linkers, blocking moieties, TAM-specific cleavage sites, and the computational optimization of the protein structure and stability of the top 3-5 mAb-cytokine combinations identified in Objective 2. This is directly dependent on the output of the previous objective. Task 3.2 involves the production of the designed fusion proteins. Once the constructs are designed and optimized, they will be produced using our established protocols for protein expression and purification. This ensures a reliable supply of high-quality immunocytokines for in vitro validation. Task 3.3 will focus on the in-vitro validation of the immunocytokines. The in vitro studies will evaluate the efficacy of the immunocytokines to activate immune cells. This step will also evaluate the specificity of the immunocytokines for the tumor cells, and their potential off target effects. We will use a combination of cellular assays, flow cytometry and imaging to quantify the immune activation and the tumor cell killing.

The expected results include a set of well-characterized immunocytokine constructs with optimized binding affinity, specificity, and stability. We expect to identify constructs that show a significant increase in anti-tumor activity in in-vitro assays, compared to the control. The results will directly inform the selection of candidates for in vivo studies, and will validate the hypothesis that the engineered immunocytokines can modulate the immune response in the BM TME. This objective is directly dependent on the completion of Objective 2, particularly on the selection of the optimal mAb-cytokine combinations, and on the design and manufacture of the immunocytokine constructs.

###### Task 3.3: In-vitro validation of immunocytokines

The in-vitro validation of immunocytokines, as detailed in Task 3, will employ a multi-faceted approach to rigorously assess their binding affinity and functional impact on immune cell interactions. The fusion proteins generated in Task 3.2 will serve as the core reagents for these studies. Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR) will be utilized to quantify the binding kinetics of the immunocytokines to their respective target receptors, providing precise measurements of association and dissociation rates. Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assays (ELISA) will complement SPR by providing quantitative assessment of immunocytokine binding to target molecules in solution, enabling measurement of binding avidity and specificity. These assays will use purified receptor proteins and cell lysates to mimic the cellular environment. Cell-based binding assays will further validate the binding by analyzing immunocytokine interactions with target receptors expressed on the surface of human cells. These assays will use a range of cell lines, including both tumor cells and immune cell lines. Reporter gene assays will be implemented to evaluate the functional consequences of receptor activation upon immunocytokine binding. These will use cells engineered with reporter genes controlled by signaling pathways of interest, allowing for quantifiable assessment of target engagement and downstream signaling. Furthermore, we will investigate the effect of the immunocytokines on immune cell interactions by co-culturing human monocyte-derived macrophages (huMDMs), natural killer (NK) cells, or T cells. To assess the efficacy of the fusion proteins in inducing cytotoxic NK and T cell activity, co-cultures of huMDMs, NK, or T cells with tumor cells will be treated with the various mAb-cytokine chimeras. The cytotoxic activity of NK and T cells will be assessed using standard assays such as flow cytometry for expression of activation markers like CD107a and granzyme B, and cytotoxicity assays such as calcein-AM or LDH release assays. The data generated will allow us to correlate binding affinities with observed changes in immune cell activation and cytotoxic function. These assays will be performed using multiple concentrations of the fusion proteins and different incubation times to determine the optimal conditions for each fusion protein. The data will be analyzed by non-linear regression using GraphPad prism or similar software.

##### Risks and Alternatives

The primary risk associated with this objective is the potential failure to identify immunocytokine constructs with the desired binding affinity, specificity, and stability. This could stem from suboptimal selection of peptide linkers, blocking moieties, or TAM-specific cleavage sites. Furthermore, the computational optimization process may not fully predict the in vitro behavior of the fusion proteins, resulting in constructs that are unstable or lack the desired activity. A medium risk is that the chosen mAb-cytokine combinations may not demonstrate the anticipated synergistic effects, or may be limited by off-target effects, limiting the therapeutic potential of the immunocytokine. The reliance on the successful completion of Objective 2 poses a significant dependency risk; failure in selecting optimal mAb-cytokine combinations would directly hinder the success of this objective (High risk). The availability of high-quality protein expression and purification protocols for the fusion proteins is also a resource dependency (Medium risk). To mitigate the technical risks, we will employ a combinatorial design approach for the fusion proteins, testing multiple variants of linkers, blocking moieties and cleavage sites. We will implement rigorous quality control measures during protein production and purification. To address the dependency on the output of Objective 2, we will adopt a phased approach, allowing for iterative refinement and adjustments based on preliminary results. If the initial mAb-cytokine combinations do not yield the desired results, we will explore alternative combinations identified through the previous objectives. As an alternative, we could also focus on a few lead mAb-cytokine combinations, instead of a large set.
