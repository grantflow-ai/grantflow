The working hypothesis is that the brain metastases tumor microenvironment (BM TME) creates a highly immunosuppressive milieu that impairs anti-tumor immune responses. The general goals of this objective are to utilize advanced single-cell technologies to comprehensively characterize the immune landscape within the BM TME and to identify novel therapeutic targets, specifically cytokines and antibodies, that can modulate immune activity towards a more effective anti-tumor response. To achieve this, we will employ several cutting-edge technologies. We will use Zman-seq, which combines fluorescent temporal tracking of cells with scRNA-seq, to profile the dynamic changes in immune cell states over time as they infiltrate the BM TME. We will also employ PIC-seq to study cell-cell interactions within the TME and spatial transcriptomics to investigate the spatial distribution of immune cells. By integrating these technologies, we expect to identify specific molecular pathways, transcriptional programs, and receptor-ligand interactions that drive the immunosuppressive state of the BM TME. Furthermore, these methods will help us to identify how the interactions between immune cells and the tumor cells influence the TME. The expected results include a comprehensive characterization of the immune cell state transitions and differentiation pathways, identification of key immunosuppressive molecules such as checkpoints and secreted factors and cytokines, and delineation of temporal and spatial dynamics of immune cells within the BM. This data-driven approach will allow us to propose a list of cytokines with potentially high antitumor activity and targets for antibodies to counteract the immune suppression in the BM TME, ultimately contributing to the development of novel immunotherapeutic strategies.

##### Preliminary Results

Our previous research has demonstrated the synergistic potential of combining an anti-TREM2 antibody with cytokines to induce robust pro-inflammatory macrophage activity in vitro, thus supporting the feasibility of our approach to modulate the immune response within the tumor microenvironment. We have successfully generated and tested preliminary TREM2 immunocytokine model molecules, including an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein, and developed a masking strategy for the cytokine arm using TAM-specific protease-responsive blocking moieties, which have been tested in vivo. Furthermore, utilizing Zman-seq, we have previously investigated the temporal dynamics of immune cell differentiation within a glioblastoma (GBM) model. These studies revealed that, in control conditions, infiltrating monocytes differentiate into immunosuppressive TAMs over a 36-48 hour period, while NK cells transition to a dysfunctional state within 24 hours. Critically, blocking TREM2 signaling redirected monocyte differentiation towards a pro-inflammatory TAM phenotype, highlighting TREM2 as a key regulator of myeloid cell fate within the tumor microenvironment. These observations were supported by single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data showing that aTREM2 treatment resulted in a distinct TAM population compared to control, with downregulation of immunosuppressive genes such as Arg1, Pirb, Il18bp, Vegfa and Cd274, and upregulation of pro-inflammatory genes including Ccl3, Ccl4, Cd81 and Cd83. The roots of both the control and aTREM2 trajectories were similar, consisting of infiltrating monocytes that did not express Trem2. In both groups, initial state transitions entailed the downregulation of monocytic transcripts (Chil3, Plac8). We observed immediate and late transcriptomic changes distinguishing the control and aTREM2 trajectories. Early changes upon TREM2-inhibition, included the rapid upregulation of pro-inflammatory factors Ccl4, Cd81, and inhibition of early upregulation of immunosuppressive factors. We have also analyzed the metabolic profile of TAMs, revealing that aTREM2 treatment induced a switch from glycolysis to oxidative phosphorylation, alongside the induction of polyamine pathways mediated by the rate-limiting enzyme SAT1. Moreover, using in vitro differentiation of bone marrow-derived macrophages (BMDMs) with GL261 cell culture supernatant we recapitulated the in vivo effects of TREM2 blockade and observed an increased Sat1 expression in BMDMs treated with aTREM2 antibody, suggesting a direct consequence of TREM2 blockade. By investigating ligand-receptor pairs we identified a small set of chemokines, mostly CCL3, CCL4, CCL5, CCL7, CCL8 and CXCL9, and complement factor C3, as key molecules mediating the effects of TREM2 inhibition in TAMs. The analysis revealed that these chemokines and C3 demonstrated temporal expression kinetics and widespread effects in modulating gene expression in receiver cells such as NK cells, monocytes, and monocyte-derived macrophages. These findings support the feasibility of using TREM2-targeting strategies and indicate its potential for modulating the monocyte-to-TAM trajectory and reprogramming the TME.

##### Research Tasks

###### Task 1.1: Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME

This research task focuses on elucidating the temporal dynamics of immune cell activity within the bone marrow (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME) using Zman-seq technology. The primary objective is to adapt and apply Zman-seq, previously validated in glioma models, to the BM TME to track the temporal trajectories of immune cell state transitions. The experimental design involves in vivo labeling of circulating immune cells with fluorescently tagged anti-CD45 antibodies at specific time intervals, followed by isolation of BM leukocytes via fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS). Zman-seq utilizes a pulse-chase principle, where cells are labeled in circulation and tracked upon infiltration into the BM TME. The temporal resolution of Zman-seq allows for the determination of tumor exposure time, defined as the duration a cell spends within the BM TME after initial labeling. This is achieved by sequentially injecting antibodies labeled with different fluorophores and then employing single-cell FACS index sorting to capture the fluorophore combinations, thereby inferring the time of BM infiltration. Data collection methods involve flow cytometry to quantify labeled cells and single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) to profile transcriptomic changes correlated with tumor exposure time. This will produce temporal maps of the BM TME, revealing the dynamics of cellular transitions and differentiation pathways. The analytical framework includes assigning continuous tumor exposure time (cTET) values to metacells based on the cumulative distribution function (CDF) of cells in different time bins, and subsequent trajectory analysis and identification of significant marker genes across time. Statistical methods will be used to correlate gene expression with tumor exposure time. This task does not involve clinical trials. Key technical terms include: Zman-seq, scRNA-seq, FACS, TME, tumor exposure time, cTET, and CDF. These terms are crucial for understanding the experimental methodology, data analysis techniques, and the research goals.   A potential limitation may be the number of detectable fluorophores impacting the ability to capture cell dynamics over extended periods and the fading of fluorescent signals with time, which may lead to lower signal-to-noise ratios for longer exposure times. This task is essential for understanding the dynamic regulation of immunosuppression in the BM TME, with the potential to identify gene modules that can be targeted to specifically interfere with critical checkpoints of immune cell differentiation and inform the development of novel immunotherapeutic strategies. There are no relationships to other research tasks specified in the provided data.

###### Task 1.2: Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME

This research task focuses on characterizing immune cell-cell interactions within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME) using PIC-seq technology, as stated in the task description: "Use PIC-seq to measure immune cell interaction in the BM TME." PIC-seq combines cell sorting of physically interacting cells (PICs) with scRNA-seq and computational modeling to profile cellular interactions and their impact on gene expression, as described in the provided context. This method allows for the analysis of in vivo cellular interactions at high resolution, overcoming limitations of traditional scRNA-seq approaches that disrupt cell-cell contacts. The experimental design involves applying a mild tissue dissociation protocol to the bone marrow to retain some in situ cellular structures, followed by fluorescence sorting using mutually exclusive cell type markers to isolate putative PICs and single cells. Massively parallel scRNA-seq will then generate gene expression profiles for these PICs, which will be computationally deconvolved to infer transcriptional states and contrast them with those of non-interacting single cells. This approach is adapted from the methodology described for T cell-DC interactions and immune-epithelial cell interactions in the lung, where cell surface markers like CD45 and EPCAM were used to distinguish cell types. Data collection will involve fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) to isolate the cell populations of interest, followed by scRNA-seq to generate gene expression profiles. The analysis framework will involve computational deconvolution of the PIC-seq data to identify interacting cell types and their transcriptional states, contrasting them with non-interacting cells. Statistical methods, such as those employed in the original PIC-seq publication, will be utilized to assess the significance of gene expression changes. The task does not explicitly involve clinical trials with human participants, so the associated sections are not applicable. This task is not related to any other tasks in the provided data. Key technical terms include PIC-seq, scRNA-seq, TME, cell-cell interactions, fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS), and computational deconvolution. The information regarding specific cell surface markers for immune cell subpopulations in the BM TME is missing, which is critical for cell sorting. This omission would significantly impact the experimental design. Potential challenges include optimizing the tissue dissociation protocol to maintain PIC integrity, identifying appropriate cell surface markers for sorting, and ensuring adequate computational deconvolution of complex PICs. Mitigation strategies would involve extensive protocol optimization and careful marker selection. This research will significantly impact our understanding of immune cell dynamics in the BM TME and will contribute to a broader understanding of how cell-cell interactions drive tumor progression.

###### Task 1.3: Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME

This research task aims to elucidate the spatial distribution of immune cells within the bone marrow (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME) using Stereo-seq technology. The primary objective is to map the complex cellular architecture of the BM TME, focusing on the spatial arrangement of various immune cell subsets. The methodology involves applying Stereo-seq for high-resolution, large field-of-view spatial transcriptomics. This approach will leverage DNA nanoball (DNB) patterned arrays for in situ RNA capture, cDNA amplification, library construction, and sequencing. The spatial coordinates of transcripts are determined by sequencing the coordinate identity (CID) of every DNB, allowing for the generation of spatially resolved transcriptomic profiles. Specifically, frozen tissue sections will be loaded onto Stereo-seq chips, followed by fixation, permeabilization, and in situ RNA capture. Image-based cell segmentation, using nucleic acid staining, will enable accurate integration of transcriptomic and spatial information, resulting in a cell-by-gene matrix. This matrix will undergo spatially constrained clustering (SCC) to identify distinct tissue domains and cell types. Data will be aligned with single-cell RNA-seq data using Tangram, for validation. The analysis will incorporate algorithms designed for spatially resolved data, such as SCC. Marker gene expression will be analyzed to identify immune cell subsets.   Statistical analysis will focus on characterizing the spatial relationships between different immune cell populations, including their proximity to tumor cells and other stromal components. This study does not involve a clinical trial or human subjects; it will be performed using mouse models. Key technical terms include Stereo-seq, DNB, CID, UMI, SCC, spatial transcriptomics, bone marrow tumor microenvironment, and image-based cell segmentation. A potential challenge is the segmentation of closely apposed small immune cells, which may be mitigated by employing advanced segmentation algorithms. Another limitation is the capture of low expression genes, although the large field of view in Stereo-seq should help address this issue. This research has the potential to significantly advance our understanding of the immune landscape within the BM TME, informing the development of targeted immunotherapies. The study will complement single cell RNA-seq studies by revealing crucial spatial information.

Severity: Medium. While Zman-seq has shown success in previous studies, these limitations can impact the accuracy and reliability of temporal data acquisition, which is critical for the research objective of characterizing immune cell dynamics. Mitigation: Optimize antibody concentrations and fluorophore combinations, use full spectrum analyzers, consider shorter tracking time windows (24-48 hours, based on Zman-seq GBM data), and use alternative labeling methods (e.g. metabolic labeling with RNA labeling as described in RAG results) to address signal fading and label loss. Alternative Approach: If Zman-seq proves insufficient, focus on complementary methods such as scRNA-seq with computational trajectory analysis using RNA velocity, or single-cell spatial transcriptomics to study TME without a temporal dimension. 2. Severity: Medium. Mitigation: Optimize tissue dissociation protocols (enzymatic digestion, mechanical dissociation), use microfluidic cell sorting methods, and include control experiments to assess cell recovery and bias. ##### Preliminary Results

Our preliminary studies have demonstrated the synergistic potential of combining an anti-TREM2 antibody with cytokines. Specifically, we have shown that this combination induces strong pro-inflammatory macrophage activity in vitro. We have successfully produced TREM2 immunocytokines, including a model IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein.   Furthermore, we have designed a masking strategy for the cytokine arm utilizing blocking moieties that are responsive to TAM-specific proteases and validated its efficacy in vivo. These findings build upon previous research demonstrating that genetic ablation of TREM2 or TREM2 blocking with monoclonal antibodies modify TAMs and evoke anti-tumor immunity. Specifically, our data showed that aTREM2 treatment resulted in a distinct TAM population compared to control, with the downregulation of immunosuppressive genes such as Arg1, Pirb, Il18bp, Vegfa, and Cd274 (PDL1), and the upregulation of pro-inflammatory genes (Ccl3, Ccl4, Cd81, and Cd83), supported by a bifurcation of monocyte-to-TAM trajectories.   Our previous studies utilized Zman-seq to characterize the in vivo molecular trajectory of monocytes reprogramming towards regulatory macrophages. Analysis of single-cell RNA sequencing data revealed that TREM2 inhibition disrupts the monocyte-to-TAM transition in the TME. This was further supported by in vitro experiments using bone-marrow derived macrophages (BMDMs) which showed that aTREM2 treatment induces a metabolic switch in TAMs from glycolysis to oxidative phosphorylation, in conjunction with induction of polyamine pathways mediated by SAT1. We have also shown that TREM2 inhibition reprograms the ligand-receptor interactions in the TME, influencing the expression of key chemokines and their receptors. These experiments demonstrate the feasibility of developing immunocytokines that can modulate the TME and enhance anti-tumor immunity. The design and in vivo validation of protease-cleavable masking strategy for the cytokine arm provides a method for reducing off-target effects, and the production of model immunocytokines such as IL-2-anti-TREM2 establishes a foundation for future immunocytokine development. These findings support our objective of using potent cytokines, identified in objective 2, incorporated into antibody-cytokine fusion proteins to target melanoma BMs and shift the TME towards an anti-tumor state. ##### Risks and Alternatives

The primary risk in this objective is the potential for off-target toxicity due to the cytokine component of the immunocytokine, as cytokines can bind to their receptors on normal cells, leading to systemic side effects. This is a high-severity risk because it could limit the therapeutic dose and efficacy, and has been documented with IL-2 and TNF. The RAG results indicate that some immunocytokines, such as anti-EGFR/IL-2, induced significant body weight loss and mortality in mice, highlighting the severity of this concern. Mitigation strategies include engineering cytokines with reduced receptor affinity, using cytokine prodrugs that are activated only in the tumor microenvironment (TME) and selecting antibodies that bind to highly specific tumor antigens expressed with minimal or no expression on normal cells. An alternative approach involves local rather than systemic administration which is also supported by the RAG results as a way of reducing off-target effects. Another risk is the potential for the immunocytokine to fail to elicit a sufficient antitumor response. This could arise if the selected cytokines do not effectively modulate the TME or if the antibody targeting is insufficient to concentrate the cytokine at the tumor site. This is a medium-severity risk because while it doesn't pose the same safety concern as off-target toxicity, it would render the therapeutic approach ineffective. Mitigation involves selecting potent cytokines, which we will accomplish based on results from objective 2, careful selection of tumor-specific antigens for the antibody and optimizing the antibody format and valency to ensure maximum tumor targeting.   Should these measures fail, an alternative approach could involve exploring dual cytokine payloads to enhance TME modulation and the use of combination therapies to potentiate the antitumor effect. Finally, a lower risk would be related to technical issues during the production of the fusion protein. This risk is low severity because although delays are possible, these are not expected to affect the overall research outcome. This will be mitigated by the use of established recombinant protein expression systems. The most critical risk is the off-target toxicity due to the cytokine component. Mitigating this risk involves the use of tumor-specific antigens, engineered cytokines with reduced receptor affinity or prodrug approaches, and the implementation of local delivery.
