# Project Summary
## General Audience Abstract
This research project aims to improve the treatment of melanoma that has spread to the brain, a condition with a poor prognosis. Using advanced technologies, we will study the unique environment of these brain tumors to understand why they are resistant to current therapies. Our goal is to develop new treatments that can effectively target and eliminate these tumors by enhancing the body's immune response. This research has the potential to significantly increase the life expectancy and quality of life for patients with melanoma brain metastases. Our findings will inform the development of novel immunotherapies and diagnostic tools, accelerating their translation into clinical practice and offering new hope to patients facing this challenging disease.
## Technical Abstract
Brain metastases (BMs) in melanoma are a significant clinical challenge due to their immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) and poor patient prognosis. This translational research project aims to address this challenge by leveraging advanced single-cell technologies and artificial intelligence (AI) to gain an in-depth understanding of the BM TME. Our hypothesis is that by using single-cell analysis techniques such as ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq, coupled with AI-driven analysis, we can identify key cytokines capable of remodeling the BM TME toward anti-tumor activity. Specifically, we aim to develop novel immunocytokines that combine these promising cytokines with our anti-TREM2 antibody, to modulate myeloid, T, and NK cell compartments, thus enhancing the anti-tumor immune response. Furthermore, we will identify biomarkers using our single-cell and AI analysis to design masking strategies to increase the specificity of the immunocytokine to the BM TME. This project has high translational potential, as effective therapies for melanoma BMs could significantly increase patient life expectancy and improve their quality of life. The expected outcomes include the identification of novel therapeutic targets, the design of effective immunocytokines, and the identification of biomarkers for targeted delivery of therapeutics to the BM TME, which will be tested in clinically relevant models. This will lead to the design of personalized therapeutic approaches for melanoma BMs.

# Project Description
## Background and Specific Aims
Brain metastases (BMs) occur in almost 50% of patients with metastatic melanoma, leading to a poor prognosis and reduced overall survival. While immunotherapy has significantly improved outcomes for melanoma patients, many still experience relapse or do not respond to treatment, particularly in the immunosuppressive microenvironment (TME) of BMs. Single-cell technologies, combined with machine learning, offer powerful tools to dissect complex cellular interactions within the TME. We have identified a subtype of immune cell, regulatory (TREM2+) macrophages, that plays a crucial role in suppressing anti-tumor immunity, especially in immunotherapy-resistant melanoma. We have developed an antibody that blocks the suppressive action of these cells. Our research is based on the rationale that advanced single-cell technologies can provide an in-depth understanding of the immune environment of BMs, facilitating the development of novel approaches to activate the immune system. Our approach involves simultaneously modulating different immune cell compartments (macrophages, T cells, NK cells) to induce a potent anti-tumor immune response. Our hypothesis is that by using our advanced single cell technologies, including cell temporal tracking (ZMAN-seq, differentiation flows), cell-cell interactions (PIC-seq), and improved strategies for AI analysis of spatial transcriptomics with Stereo-seq, we can gain an in-depth understanding of the BM TME and identify cytokines capable of remodulating the TME towards anti-tumor activity. This understanding will enable us to design novel immunocytokines combining the most promising cytokines with our antiTREM2 antibody to modulate both the myeloid and T and NK cell compartments. Our hypothesis is that our single cell and AI analysis will also enable us to identify biomarkers to design masking strategies to increase the specificity of the immunocytokine to the BM TME. The specific aims of this project are to: (1) Develop BM TME models with holistic, multimodal AI-driven analysis, creating single-cell atlases of immune, stromal, and tumor cells from human and murine BMs. (2) Conduct preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models, identifying effective cytokines and cytokine combinations. (3) Design tumor-targeting immunocytokines, incorporating potent cytokines into antibody-cytokine fusion proteins that target melanoma BM antigens.
## Preliminary Data
Our preliminary data provides strong supporting evidence for the feasibility of our proposed research. We have demonstrated the synergistic potential of combining an anti-TREM2 antibody with cytokines, showing induction of strong pro-inflammatory macrophage activity in vitro. This is a key finding, as it validates our approach of targeting TREM2+ regulatory macrophages to remodel the tumor microenvironment (TME). Furthermore, we have successfully tested the feasibility of producing TREM2 immunocytokines, and have developed preliminary model molecules, including an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein. This demonstrates our capability to design and produce the therapeutic molecules proposed in the research plan. Crucially, we have also designed a masking strategy for the cytokine arm using blocking moieties responsive only to tumor-associated macrophage (TAM)-specific proteases. We have tested this strategy in vivo, demonstrating its potential to enhance the specificity of our therapeutic molecules to the BM TME. These preliminary findings, including in vitro and in vivo validation steps, provide a strong basis for the feasibility of our proposed research, establishing that we can effectively target TREM2+ macrophages, design immunocytokines, and implement masking strategies for enhanced specificity.
## Research Plan

### Research Objectives

#### Objective 1: Developing BM TME models with holistic, multimodal AI-driven analysis

This objective aims to develop a comprehensive understanding of the brain metastasis (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME) with a focus on immune cell dynamics, interactions, and spatial distribution, utilizing advanced single-cell technologies and AI-driven analysis. The central hypothesis is that a detailed characterization of the BM TME will reveal novel targets for immunomodulatory therapies, particularly cytokines and antibodies. Specifically, this objective seeks to define the temporal trajectories of immune cell infiltration and functional adaptation within the BM TME, to map cell-cell interactions that dictate immunosuppression, and to establish the spatial organization of immune populations in relation to tumor cells. This will provide a foundational understanding for the development of novel immunocytokines.

To address these goals, three main tasks are proposed. The first task, "Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME", will employ ZMAN-seq, a single-cell technology that enables the tracking of transcriptomic dynamics over time. This method involves introducing fluorescent antibody time stamps into immune cells in circulation, allowing us to monitor their transcriptional changes as they infiltrate the tumor. This approach will define the differentiation pathways of NK cells and monocytes as they transition to dysfunctional states in the BM TME. The second task, "Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME", will utilize PIC-seq to identify cell-cell interactions within the TME. This method will elucidate which ligand-receptor interactions drive the immunosuppressive phenotype of macrophages and NK cells within the BM TME. Finally, the third task, "Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME", will use Stereo-seq to analyze spatial transcriptomics. This approach will define the spatial organization of immune cells in relation to tumor cells, and map the heterogeneity of the TME. These analyses will be performed using both murine BM models and human patient samples. These approaches will generate single-cell atlases of immune, stromal, and tumor cells. A continuous tumor exposure time will be calculated by the area under the curve of the cell's cumulative distribution function across different time bins to observe differentiation trajectories. Integrated tissue models from single-cell data will be constructed to holistically infer cell physiology and potential drug response.

The expected outcomes of these tasks include the identification of key molecular circuits that drive immune dysfunction in the BM TME. This will include a detailed temporal map of cell-state transitions, a comprehensive understanding of cell-cell interaction networks and the spatial context within which these interactions occur. These outcomes will directly contribute to identifying therapeutic targets for immunocytokines and antibodies in Objective 2. All tasks in this objective are prerequisites for the subsequent objectives, as they provide the foundational data for target identification and immunocytokine design.

##### Preliminary Results

Our preliminary data demonstrates the feasibility and potential impact of our proposed research on the brain metastasis (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME). We have successfully developed and validated an in vivo single-cell technology, Zman-seq, which enables the tracking of transcriptomic dynamics across time by introducing fluorescent antibody time stamps into immune cells in circulation. This approach, applied in a glioblastoma (GBM) model, revealed that cytotoxic NK cells rapidly transition to a dysfunctional state within 24 hours of tumor infiltration, regulated by TGF-β activation. Concurrently, infiltrating monocytes differentiated into immunosuppressive macrophages characterized by the upregulation of key suppressive myeloid checkpoints such as Trem2, Il18bp, and Arg1 over 36 to 48 hours. Specifically, we observed that the expression of Trem2 increased with time as monocytes progressed to TAMs. Importantly, we demonstrated that blocking TREM2 with an antagonistic mAb redirected the monocyte differentiation trajectory towards pro-inflammatory TAMs, suggesting a potential therapeutic strategy.

Furthermore, we have established the synergistic potential of combining anti-TREM2 antibodies with cytokines. We observed that the combination induced a strong pro-inflammatory macrophage activity in vitro. This led to the development of preliminary model immunocytokines, including an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein, which serves as a proof-of-concept for the therapeutic approach proposed in the subsequent objective. We also designed and validated a masking strategy for the cytokine arm of our immunocytokines using blocking moieties responsive only to TAM-specific proteases, demonstrating successful in vivo functionality.

These preliminary results strongly support the feasibility of our proposed approach to characterize the BM TME with a focus on immune cell dynamics. The Zman-seq data not only reveals the temporal differentiation of immune cells but also provides a methodology to track the continuous tumor exposure time (cTET) of each cell. This allows the identification of the most significant genes correlating with time. In NK cells for example, the expression of the homing receptor :formula: decreased with time, while Xcl1 expression increased. In the myeloid compartment, the expression of Plac8 decreased while Trem2 expression increased with tumor exposure time. Using this approach we have also identified the temporal TF circuits regulating NK cell dysfunction and myeloid cell differentiation in the TME. We have also determined through analysis of cell-cell interaction data that ligands, including TGF-β1 and CCL12, are major factors dampening NK cell activity in the tumor. These results pave the way for identifying specific cell-cell interactions which drive immunosuppression in the BM TME, supporting the use of PIC-seq. Furthermore, these findings highlight the applicability of our approach to develop targeted immunotherapies by identifying pathways amenable for modulation by immunocytokines. These data support the use of Stereo-seq for the spatial analysis of immune cell distribution in relation to tumor cells, allowing us to understand the heterogeneity of the TME. The preliminary data serves as a strong foundation for the proposed research in Objective 1 and provides a clear pathway for achieving the stated goals.

##### Research Tasks

###### Task 1.1: Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME

This research task will employ Zman-seq to investigate temporal changes in immune cell activity within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME). Building upon previous work adapting Zman-seq from glioma models to the BM, this study will utilize fluorescent antibody time stamps introduced into circulating immune cells. These cells will be tracked as they infiltrate the BM TME, allowing for the empirical measurement of their exposure time to the tumor environment. Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) will be performed on sorted cells using fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS), with fluorescent stamps on individual cells captured via index-sorting to infer infiltration time. This approach will generate temporal maps of the BM TME, revealing immune cell state-transition trajectories and differentiation pathways. The analysis will focus on identifying time-dependent gene modules, transcription factor (TF) circuits, and receptor-ligand interactions that drive immune cell dysfunction. Specifically, this task will analyze the progression of NK cells from cytotoxic to dysfunctional states, and monocytes to immunosuppressive tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs), noting the dynamics of key factors like TGF-β1, CCL12, and TREM2. The data analysis will involve a statistical method to assign continuous tumor exposure time (cTET) values to metacells, calculated using cumulative distribution functions (CDF) based on cell frequencies at different time points (e.g. 12h, 24h, and 36h). The area under the curve (AUC) of each metacell-CDF will be used to define the cTET, enabling the tracking of transcriptional trajectories between cell states. The temporal sequence of cell-cell interactions and their influence on cell state in tissue will be examined, providing insights into the dynamic regulation of immunosuppression in the BM TME.

###### Task 1.2: Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME

Task 2 will employ PIC-seq to dissect immune cell-cell interactions within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME). PIC-seq combines cell sorting of physically interacting cells with single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) and computational modeling, enabling the profiling of cellular interactions and their impact on gene expression. This technology is crucial for in vivo molecular characterization of cell-cell interactions and overcomes the limitations of standard scRNA-seq, which requires single-cell suspensions that disrupt the study of cell-cell interactions. The methodology involves sorting physically interacting cells (PICs) using markers, generating gene expression profiles via massively parallel scRNA-seq and computationally deconvolving these profiles to infer the transcriptional states of interacting cells, contrasting them with non-interacting single cells. This approach will determine the in vivo applicability of PIC-seq by focusing on physical interactions between immune cells. The transcriptomic profiles of interacting cells need to be sufficiently 'distant' for deconvolution by the PIC-seq algorithm, which will be an important consideration during analysis. Task 2 is dependent on a temporal understanding of cell states established in Task 1.1, as these temporal dynamics of cell states are critical for accurately deconvolving cell-cell interactions, and will inform spatial distributions of cell types and interactions established in task 1.3, thereby providing spatial context to the identified cell-cell interaction. The expected output will be data describing specific immune cell interactions within the BM TME, with associated gene expression changes occurring in interacting partners. This task will use the orthotopic immunocompetent murine models for melanoma BMs developed by the Weiss group, in addition to patient clinical samples. Spatial analysis will be conducted using Stereo-seq to determine the spatial distribution of interacting cell types within the BM TME, which will be correlated with PIC-seq data to provide spatial context to the cell-cell interactions. Data analysis will involve computational deconvolution of PIC-seq data to identify interacting cell types and their associated gene expression profiles, statistical analysis of gene expression changes in interacting versus non-interacting cells using packages such as SCANPY, and correlation analysis to integrate PIC-seq data with temporal information from Task 1.1 and spatial information from Stereo-seq. The Leiden algorithm will be used for community detection in single-cell data. Data integration will be performed using methods such as those described in Hao et al, 2021, to combine scRNA-seq, spatial transcriptomics, and temporal data. This will provide a comprehensive view of cell-cell interactions in the BM TME.

###### Task 1.3: Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME

This research task will employ Stereo-seq technology to investigate the spatial distribution of immune cells within the bone marrow (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME). Stereo-seq, a spatial transcriptomics method, uses DNA nanoball-patterned arrays and tissue RNA capture to achieve high-resolution, large field-of-view analysis. This approach enables the dissection of spatial cell-type heterogeneity in tissues, providing a more detailed understanding of the immune landscape. The methodology involves designing a DNB patterned array chip, determining spatial coordinates of barcoded oligonucleotides via in situ sequencing, preparing capture probes by ligating UMI-polyT containing oligonucleotides to each spot, performing in situ RNA capture from tissue sections, and subsequent cDNA amplification, library construction, and sequencing. Computational analysis of the sequencing data will allow for high-resolution spatially resolved transcriptomics. Specifically, a spatially constrained-clustering algorithm will be applied to analyze the Stereo-seq data, optimized for large and complex tissues, enabling the identification of distinct tissue domains and their associated immune cell populations. This analysis will build upon the findings of prior tasks that examine temporal changes and cell-cell interactions, using the high-resolution spatial information from Stereo-seq to correlate these findings with the physical locations of immune cell subsets within the BM TME. The expected output is a spatially resolved transcriptomic map of the BM TME, highlighting the distribution of various immune cell types and their interactions within the tumor microenvironment. This methodology provides superior resolution compared to other methods such as Visium and Slide-seqV2, offering a more detailed spatial analysis of immune cell distribution.

##### Risks and Alternatives

Several risks are associated with the proposed research. A primary technical risk involves the ZMAN-seq methodology, where the introduction of fluorescent antibody time stamps might not accurately capture the temporal dynamics of all immune cell subsets, potentially leading to skewed differentiation trajectories. To mitigate this, we will validate the antibody staining efficiency and perform rigorous controls, including comparisons with traditional static scRNA-seq. Additionally, the PIC-seq method, used for identifying cell-cell interactions, may not fully capture the complexity of these interactions, particularly transient or weak contacts. We will complement this approach with spatial transcriptomics (Stereo-seq) to provide spatial context, and we will validate the identified ligand-receptor interactions using complementary techniques. Another potential risk is the computational analysis of high-dimensional single-cell data, which requires significant computational resources and robust analytical pipelines. The AI-driven analysis may encounter biases or inaccuracies if not handled properly. To address this, we will implement rigorous quality control steps, validate our findings using independent datasets, and employ multiple computational methods. Furthermore, reliance on both murine models and human patient samples presents a logistical challenge. Variations in sample quality and handling might introduce confounding factors. We will standardize protocols and incorporate batch correction methods in the analysis to minimize this risk. Contingency plans include the use of additional cell lines or patient samples if issues arise with the original material, as well as establishing collaborations with additional facilities with high-throughput sequencing capabilities.

#### Objective 2: Preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models

Objective 2 focuses on the preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent brain metastasis (BM) models. The central hypothesis is that modulation of the BM tumor microenvironment (TME) via selected cytokines, identified in Objective 1, will enhance anti-tumor immune activity. This objective is designed to validate the findings from single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) analyses performed in Objective 1 and to identify potent cytokines for downstream development of immunocytokines. The goal is to shift the immunosuppressive TME towards an immunologically "hot" state.

Task 2.1, "Screening of cytokines in BM TME," initiates with the generation of cytokine-secreting clones from brain-tropic melanoma cell lines. These clones will be engineered to conditionally express a panel of cytokines identified in Objective 1. Tumor spheroids, containing these cytokine cassettes, will be co-cultured with immune cells. Doxycycline-induced expression will enable testing of varied cytokine combinations. Task 2.2, "In-vitro validation of cytokines," will assess the impact of these cytokine combinations on myeloid, NK, and T cell activity. This will involve in-vitro assays of co-cultured human monocyte-derived macrophages (huMDMs), NK, or T cells. Cell activity will be quantified by flow cytometry and cytokine secretion via ELISA and qPCR. Data from Task 1.1 will serve as control. Task 2.3, "In-vivo validation of cytokines," will translate the most promising in-vitro results to in-vivo models. B16 clones, expressing the selected cytokines, will be intracardially injected to generate melanoma BMs in immunocompetent mice. Cytokine expression will be induced using doxycycline. Tumor response will be monitored by MRI and survival measurement.

These preclinical studies will employ advanced single-cell technologies such as scRNA-seq to deeply characterize the immune activity within the BM TME. This data-driven approach aims to pinpoint the most effective cytokine combinations, and validate targets for antibodies and cytokines to modulate the immune activity in the brain, ultimately increasing anti-tumor activity. The results from this objective are directly dependent on the cytokine candidates identified in Objective 1. Successful completion of this objective will provide a data set of cytokines for use in the development of immunocytokines in Objective 3.

##### Preliminary Results

Preliminary studies have demonstrated the synergistic potential of combining anti-TREM2 antibodies with cytokines to induce pro-inflammatory macrophage activity in vitro. These findings align with Objective 2's goal of modulating the brain metastasis (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME) to enhance anti-tumor immune activity. Specifically, the prior work showed that TREM2 blockade with an antagonistic antibody resulted in a distinct TAM population, downregulating immunosuppressive genes such as *Arg1*, *Pirb* (*LILRB3*), *Il18bp*, *Vegfa*, and *Cd274* (*PDL1*), and upregulating pro-inflammatory genes (*Ccl3*, *Ccl4*, *Cd81*, and *Cd83*). This indicates that blocking TREM2 enhances CCL3 signaling, supporting inflammation by increasing expression and decreasing its cognate receptor on TAMs, which can be used to redirect the monocyte-to-macrophage trajectory toward a pro-inflammatory state, which is the goal of objective 2. Furthermore, the feasibility of producing TREM2 immunocytokines has been established, with the development of preliminary model molecules, such as an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein. A masking strategy using blocking moieties responsive to TAM-specific proteases was also successfully tested in vivo, showing the translatability of our in vitro findings to an in vivo model and validating the design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines as described in Aim 3. These preliminary findings demonstrate our capacity to develop and test cytokine-based immunotherapies, directly supporting the proposed in vitro and in vivo screening of cytokines described in Tasks 2.1, 2.2, and 2.3. The inducible cytokine expression system developed for the previous studies is directly applicable to Objective 2, where the tumor spheroids containing cytokine cassettes will be generated and co-cultured with immune cells, where doxycycline will be used to induce cytokine expression, enabling testing of varied cytokine combinations. The Zman-seq technology, which is used to characterize cellular trajectories and differentiation pathways, will be used to analyze the effect of these cytokine combinations on myeloid, NK, and T cell activity, which can be quantified via flow cytometry, ELISA, and qPCR, building on the single cell analysis pipeline established in Tasks 1.4 and 1.5. The successful in vivo validation of the blocking moieties highlights the readiness for the in vivo studies of cytokine combinations, where B16 clones expressing selected cytokines will be intracardially injected into mice to generate melanoma BMs, and MRI scans will be used to assess tumor response as a method of validation. The data will then be used to select the most effective cytokine combinations to be used in Aim 3.

##### Research Tasks

###### Task 2.1: Screening of cytokines in BM TME

This task involves an in-vitro screening of a panel of approximately 30 cytokines, using our in-house cytokine library, to identify those that modulate immune activity within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME). The screen will be performed in combination with our E3C7 mAb, with control assays conducted without the mAb or cytokines. The effects of these cytokines on myeloid, NK, and T cell activity will be assessed through in-vitro co-culture assays using human monocyte-derived macrophages (huMDMs), NK cells, and T cells. Cellular activity will be evaluated by quantifying proliferative potential using flow cytometry and by measuring cytokine secretion via ELISA and qPCR. To evaluate cytotoxic activity, co-cultures of huMDMs, NK, or T cells with tumor cells will be treated with the selected cytokines and the E3C7 mAb, with tumor cell death quantified by FACS. The data obtained from the assays, specifically regarding the down-regulation of myeloid cell suppressive activity and enhancement of myeloid and T cell inflammatory activity, will be aggregated to identify 8-10 optimal cytokine and anti-TREM2 mAb combinations. This initial in-vitro screening is a prerequisite for subsequent in-vitro validation detailed in a related task, which will utilize a functional screening system, including a control culture derived from TREM2-/- mice. The results of this screen will inform the selection of cytokines for use in in-vivo models for assessment of the most promising modulatory functions in the context of anti-TREM2 mAb treatments.

###### Task 2.2: In-vitro validation of cytokines

Task 2 focuses on the in-vitro validation of cytokines identified in Task 1. This validation will employ in-vitro models, specifically utilizing cytokine-secreting clones of brain tropic melanoma cell lines transduced to conditionally express a broad spectrum of BM immune re-activation candidate cytokines. These clones will form tumor spheroids, incorporating one or multiple cytokine cassettes, and will be co-cultured with immune cells. Doxycycline induction will be used to test varied cytokine combinations. Evaluation of cytokine activity will include assessment of cytotoxic T and NK cell activity by quantifying tumor cell death using FACS, along with measurements of NK and T cell proliferative potential via flow cytometry and cytokine secretion via ELISA and qPCRs. The most effective cytokines and cytokine combinations identified in vitro will be used for subsequent in vivo testing. This task, designated as Task 2.2, relies on the results of Task 2.1 and serves as a prerequisite for in vivo validation in Task 2.3. Data from Task 1.1, which measures cytokine and anti-TREM2 effects on myeloid, NK, and T cell activity, will serve as a control. Additionally, cell-based binding and reporter assays, as well as SPR and ELISA, will be employed for validation. The experiments will require cell culture medium, along with reagents for flow cytometry, ELISA and qPCRs. Furthermore, the execution of task 2.2 relies on the prior completion of task 2.1, which focuses on design and manufacturing of immunocytokines.

###### Task 2.3: In-vivo validation of cytokines

Task 3 focuses on the in-vivo validation of cytokines, specifically assessing the therapeutic efficacy of trans-acting MiTEs on myeloid, NK, and T cell activity. This task directly builds upon the in-vitro cytokine selection and validation performed in Tasks 2.1 and 2.2. The experimental design involves utilizing an in-vivo functional screening system, which includes an inducible cytokine overexpression platform in barcoded tumor cell lines. A panel of 5-8 cytokines, preselected from Task 1.4, will be cloned into expression vectors and screened in combination with anti-TREM2 mAbs. In vivo assays will employ a platform developed to overexpress cytokines by using barcoded tumor cell lines, and the cytokine expression will be activated with doxycycline. Tumor response will be monitored using MRI and survival measurement. Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) analysis will be applied to characterize the effects of cytokine and anti-TREM2 mAb combinations on immune cell populations. The scRNA-seq data will be used for data-driven selection of 3-5 cytokines, which will then be progressed for use in downstream fusion protein design. Furthermore, the activity of these cytokines will be assessed by measuring the activity of co-cultured huMDMs, NK or T cells, as measured by quantifying the proliferative potential using flow cytometry, and cytokine secretion using ELISA and qPCRs. The data collected in Tasks 1.1 and 1.2 will be used as control. The goal is to validate the pro-inflammatory and anti-neoplastic effects of the selected cytokines identified in the previous tasks.

##### Risks and Alternatives

A primary risk is the failure to identify a sufficient number of cytokines in Objective 1 that demonstrate the desired immunomodulatory effects in the brain metastasis TME. This would directly impact the ability to perform preclinical screening in Objective 2. To mitigate this, we will employ a broad panel of cytokines identified through single-cell analysis, ensuring redundancy and multiple candidates for downstream validation. If the initial cytokine panel proves insufficient, we will utilize additional datasets from published literature and our existing scRNA-seq data to identify new candidate cytokines. Another risk is the potential for inconsistent cytokine expression from engineered cell lines or inadequate TME modulation in vivo. To mitigate this, we will use a doxycycline-inducible system to carefully control cytokine expression. We will also use advanced imaging modalities like MRI to monitor tumor response and cytokine expression levels within the BM TME. If in vivo results are not optimal with single cytokines, we will explore combinations of cytokines based on the in-vitro data. We will also evaluate various expression levels and timing of cytokine expression to optimize the anti-tumor response. Finally, there is a risk that in-vitro results do not translate into effective in-vivo responses. To address this, we will use an orthotopic immunocompetent model closely resembling human brain metastases, which will allow us to test the cytokines in a more physiologically relevant setting. The use of single cell analysis in-vivo will enable deep characterization of the TME. If limited in-vivo efficacy is observed, we will analyze scRNA-seq data to identify mechanisms of resistance and use this to inform the design of alternative strategies such as combining cytokine therapies with immune checkpoint blockade.

#### Objective 3: Design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines

This objective focuses on the design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines, leveraging potent cytokines that can reactivate the immune compartment and eradicate melanoma brain metastases (BMs). The central hypothesis is that by incorporating these cytokines into antibody-cytokine fusion proteins (immunocytokines) targeting species-conserved tumor antigens highly expressed in melanoma BMs, we can overcome the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) and enhance anti-tumor activity. This objective is dependent on the identification of optimal mAb-cytokine combinations from Objective 2. The primary goal is to identify the most effective constructs for the Investigational New Drug (IND) stage, moving beyond the classical response/no-response dichotomy through cycles of model-driven optimization.

The initial task involves the design of fusion proteins and cleavage sites. This will include selecting appropriate antibody formats (e.g., intact IgG, antibody fragments) and cytokines (e.g., IL-2, IL-10, IL-12, IL-15, IL-24), considering factors such as tumor target (cell membrane antigens, extracellular matrix components) and potential synergistic effects. We will explore various fusion configurations, such as placing the cytokine on the carboxy termini of either the heavy or light chains, to optimize receptor binding and minimize off-target effects. The design will incorporate cytokine engineering strategies, such as creating cytokine mutants with reduced receptor affinity or conditionally activated prodrugs, to address safety concerns and improve tumor targeting. Cleavage sites will be designed to ensure optimal release of the cytokine in the tumor microenvironment. The second task is the production of the designed fusion proteins. This involves recombinant expression in suitable host systems (e.g., mammalian cells), followed by purification using affinity chromatography and size-exclusion chromatography. The purified proteins will be characterized by SDS-PAGE, Western blotting, and mass spectrometry to verify their integrity and purity. Finally, the in vitro validation of the immunocytokines will involve assessing their binding affinity to target antigens, their ability to activate immune cells (e.g., T cells, NK cells), and their cytotoxicity against melanoma BM cell lines. We will use assays like flow cytometry, ELISA, and cytotoxicity assays. The expected results include identifying immunocytokine constructs with high binding affinity to tumor antigens, potent immune activation in vitro, and minimal off-target toxicity. These results will directly inform the selection of lead candidates for preclinical in vivo studies.

##### Research Tasks

###### Task 3.1: Design fusion proteins and cleavage site

This task will focus on the computational design of fusion proteins incorporating the top 3-5 mAb-cytokine combinations identified in Task 2.3. Advanced protein design techniques will be employed to optimize the fusion protein structures, including the selection of appropriate peptide linkers, blocking moieties, and tumor-associated macrophage (TAM)-specific cleavage sites. Computational methods will be utilized to optimize protein structure and stability. Specifically, the design process will involve in silico modeling and simulation of various fusion protein configurations to predict their stability and binding properties. The selection of peptide linkers will consider flexibility, length, and potential for immunogenicity. Blocking moieties will be incorporated to prevent premature cytokine activation, ensuring tumor-specific activation upon cleavage by TAM-specific proteases. The selected TAM-specific cleavage sites will be validated for their specificity and cleavage efficiency. The computational optimization of protein structure will include minimizing unfavorable interactions, and maximizing protein stability. This task is dependent on the completion of Task 2.3, which provides the mAb-cytokine combinations to be used as the basis for fusion protein designs. The resulting protein designs generated in Task 3.1 will then be used as input for Task 3.2, which is focused on protein production. The design process aims to generate stable and functional fusion proteins with specific tumor targeting and activation characteristics. Potential challenges include optimizing the linker design to ensure appropriate protein folding and preventing steric hindrance during target engagement, as well as ensuring specific TAM-mediated cleavage while minimizing off-target activation.

###### Task 3.2: Produce fusion proteins

Task 2 will focus on the production of 3-5 mAb-cytokine fusion proteins. The manufacturing process will be outsourced to a contract research organization (CRO) with demonstrated expertise in quality and punctual protein production. The selection of the CRO will be based on prior experience with leading providers. The protein synthesis will incorporate the selection of stable molecules exhibiting high protein expression and minimal aggregation. ###### Task 3.3: In-vitro validation of immunocytokines

This task focuses on the in-vitro validation of immunocytokines. The study will begin by confirming binding through surface plasmon resonance (SPR), enzyme-linked immunosorbent assays (ELISA), cell-based binding assays, and reporter assays, ensuring accurate measurement of immunocytokine interactions with target cells. The impact of immunocytokines on myeloid and lymphoid cell interactions will be rigorously examined through in-vitro co-culture assays using human monocyte-derived macrophages (huMDMs) co-cultured with NK or T cells. To evaluate the efficacy of fusion proteins in inducing cytotoxic NK and T cell activity, co-cultures of huMDMs, NK, or T cells and tumor cells will be treated with various mAb-cytokine chimeras. These assays will quantitatively measure tumor cell death using FACS analysis, ensuring a robust measure of cytotoxicity. The activity and cytotoxicity levels of NK and T cells will be determined, specifically measuring the amount of tumor cell death, as previously detailed in Task 1.2. This task is dependent on the generation of fusion proteins in Task 3.2, as these proteins are necessary for all downstream experiments. The data from this task will validate the efficacy of the fusion proteins in vitro. Data will be analyzed to determine the binding affinities and the impact of the immunocytokines on cell activity and cytotoxicity. Appropriate statistical methods, such as ANOVA and t-tests, will be used to confirm the significance of the findings. These assays directly address the functional impact of the immunocytokines, linking binding affinity to measurable cell activation and tumor cell death.

##### Risks and Alternatives

The primary risk for this objective is the potential for off-target toxicity due to the cytokine component of the immunocytokines. This risk is exacerbated by the possibility of target-antigen expression on normal cells, leading to on-target, off-tumor binding. Additionally, Fc gamma receptors, FcRn, and C-type lectin receptors may cause target-independent trafficking of immunocytokines, further increasing off-target effects. To mitigate these risks, we will employ cytokine engineering to create mutants with reduced receptor affinity, thereby decreasing the "cytokine sink" effect and improving tumor targeting. We will also use conditionally activated prodrug strategies, which rely on tumor-specific properties to activate the cytokine within the tumor microenvironment. This will involve the design of cleavage sites that are specific to the TME, ensuring targeted release of the cytokine. Furthermore, we will consider engineering the Fc region of the antibody to minimize ADCC by incorporating mutations such as L234A/L235A or N297A. Alternative approaches will include the use of antibody fragments, which are smaller and more rapidly cleared from circulation, reducing the potential for systemic toxicity, though this may affect half-life and require higher dosing. We will also explore different fusion configurations (e.g., cytokine placement at the N- or C-termini) and linkers to optimize immunostimulatory activity upon antigen binding. The in vitro assays will focus on assessing the binding affinity to target antigens and immune activation, while minimizing off-target toxicity.
## Experimental Design and Methods
This project will employ a rigorous experimental design utilizing advanced single-cell technologies and AI-driven analysis to achieve its aims. For Aim 1, we will develop comprehensive BM TME models using orthotopic immunocompetent murine models of melanoma BMs, alongside a large cohort of patient clinical samples. Single-cell atlases of immune, stromal, and tumor cells will be created from both human and murine BMs. We will utilize novel technologies including ZMAN-seq for cell temporal tracking, PIC-seq for cell-cell interaction analysis, and Stereo-seq for spatial transcriptomics, enhanced by AI analysis. These integrated tissue models will allow us to infer cell physiology and potential drug responses, identifying shared processes between models and human TMEs. We will perform single-cell analysis using an in-vitro functional screening system, co-culturing myeloid and NK-T cells with and without cytokine treatment (8-10 cytokines), in combination with our anti-TREM2 mAb. We will compare these results to those obtained from TREM2-/- myeloid cells. We will also use an in-vivo screening system with inducible cytokine expression in tumor cell lines using 5-8 promising cytokines identified in our in-vitro screen. We will select 3-5 cytokines with the most promising modulatory function using in depth scRNA-seq analysis combined with our anti-TREM2 mAb. For Aim 2, we will conduct preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models. This screening will involve testing identified cytokines and cytokine combinations. Data collection will primarily be through single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq), utilizing our advanced in-house pipelines for data processing and analysis. Controls will include TREM2-/- mice to assess the effect of TREM2 modulation. The use of our existing in-vitro system for bone-marrow-derived monocyte diversification into M2 suppressor macrophages, with a control culture of monocytes from TREM2-/- mice, provides a strong basis for data validation and scientific rigor. We will employ both in vitro and in vivo approaches and use a high number of mice per experimental condition. Data management will follow our established protocols, with all raw and processed data stored in secure, cloud-based repositories. We have already demonstrated the feasibility of producing TREM2 immunocytokines and have developed preliminary model molecules, including an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein, providing validation of this approach. For Aim 3, tumor-targeting immunocytokines will be designed by incorporating potent cytokines into antibody-cytokine fusion proteins targeting species-conserved tumor antigens highly expressed in melanoma BMs. This will involve breaking through the classical dichotomy of response/no-response through cycles of model-driven optimization. Masking strategies for enhanced specificity to the BM TME, using TAM-specific protease-responsive blocking moieties, will also be used to improve the targeting specificity and reduce off-target effects. We will employ melanoma BM murine models with our advanced technologies to optimize the constructs to be used for the IND stage. The study design incorporates both in vitro and in vivo experiments and data will be collected by single-cell analysis to determine cell state transitions. Reproducibility will be ensured through detailed documentation of all procedures, reagents, and experimental conditions. Data will be analyzed using established bioinformatics tools and the analysis workflows will be made available. This robust methodology will allow to determine the most effective immunocytokine combinations.
## Statistical Plan
The statistical analysis of single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data, generated from both in vitro and in vivo experiments, will employ robust biostatistical methods to ensure rigor and reproducibility. For sample size calculation, we will perform power analysis based on preliminary data and expected effect sizes to determine the number of biological replicates per experimental condition. We will utilize a high number of mice per experimental group to achieve adequate statistical power. Statistical tests will be chosen based on the nature of the data and experimental design. For comparing gene expression levels, differential gene expression analysis will be performed using DESeq2 or similar methods, accounting for multiple testing corrections to control the false discovery rate. To identify cell state transitions, we will utilize trajectory analysis methods including CellRank and those from the SCANPY package, informed by temporal information from ZMAN-seq data. For cell-cell interaction analysis from PIC-seq, we will employ NicheNet to infer ligand-receptor interactions, and graph neural networks will be used to identify statistically significant communication patterns. Spatial transcriptomics data will be analyzed using methods developed for Stereo-seq, incorporating AI analysis to identify spatial patterns and associations. Data interpretation will be performed by integrating results from different experimental modalities, using integrated analysis tools like those from Hao et al. to identify statistically significant associations between experimental conditions, cell states, and treatment responses. All data will be stored according to our data management plan, and the analysis workflows will be made available to ensure reproducibility of findings.
