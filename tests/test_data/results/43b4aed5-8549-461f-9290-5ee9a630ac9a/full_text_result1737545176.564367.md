# Project Summary
## General Audience Abstract
This research project aims to improve treatment for melanoma that has spread to the brain, a condition with a poor prognosis. Using advanced single-cell technologies and artificial intelligence, we will study the tumor's environment in the brain to identify ways to stimulate the immune system to fight the cancer. We will design novel immunotherapies that combine antibodies and cytokines, aiming to activate different immune cell types and increase treatment specificity to the brain tumor microenvironment. This approach could significantly extend the life expectancy and improve the quality of life for patients with metastatic melanoma.
## Technical Abstract
This translational research project addresses the critical need for effective therapies against melanoma brain metastases (BMs), which exhibit a highly immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). The specific aims are to utilize advanced single-cell technologies, including ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq, combined with AI analysis to comprehensively characterize the BM TME. This will facilitate identification of cytokines capable of remodeling the TME towards anti-tumor activity, enabling the design of novel immunocytokines combining these cytokines with an anti-TREM2 antibody to modulate myeloid, T, and NK cell compartments. Furthermore, the project aims to identify biomarkers for masking strategies, enhancing the immunocytokine's specificity to the BM TME. The research design includes in-depth analysis of melanoma BM tumor models and the development of first-in-class therapeutic modalities that simultaneously target multiple immune cell compartments. Expected outcomes encompass a deep understanding of BM TME dynamics, novel immunocytokines with increased specificity, and biomarkers for personalized therapy design. The clinical impact of this research is significant, as it has the potential to improve treatment outcomes for melanoma BM patients by leveraging innovative single-cell and AI technologies to develop targeted immunotherapies.

# Project Description
## Background and Specific Aims
Brain metastases (BMs) occur in approximately 50% of patients with metastatic melanoma, resulting in poor overall survival. Immunotherapy has improved outcomes for some patients, but many still experience relapse or do not respond, particularly in the immunosuppressive brain tumor microenvironment (TME). Single-cell technologies and machine learning enable the study of complex cellular interactions, facilitating the design of targeted immunotherapies. We have identified regulatory (TREM2+) macrophages as key suppressors in the TME and developed a blocking antibody. Our rationale is that advanced single-cell technologies can enable in-depth understanding of the immune environment of BMs, identifying novel approaches for activating the immune system to eliminate BMs and that modulating multiple immune cell compartments (macrophages, T cells, NK cells) can induce a potent anti-tumor response. Our hypothesis is that by using single-cell technologies, including cell temporal tracking (ZMAN-seq), cell-cell interaction analysis (PIC-seq), and improved AI for spatial transcriptomics (Stereo-seq), we can gain an in-depth understanding of the BM TME and identify cytokines capable of remodeling it towards anti-tumor activity. This will enable the design of novel immunocytokines combining the most promising cytokines with our anti-TREM2 antibody to modulate both myeloid and T and NK cell compartments. Our single-cell and AI analysis will also enable the identification of biomarkers to design masking strategies to increase the specificity of the immunocytokine to the BM TME. Specific Aims: 1) Characterize the BM TME using advanced single-cell and AI technologies to identify shared processes and pathways between human and murine models, compiling a list of cytokines with potential anti-tumor activity. 2) Preclinically screen identified cytokines in vitro and in vivo using orthotopic immunocompetent BM models to determine the most effective cytokines or combinations. 3) Design tumor-targeting immunocytokines by incorporating potent cytokines into antibody-cytokine fusion proteins that target tumor antigens in melanoma BMs, refining constructs for IND stage, thus, maximizing the translational potential.
## Preliminary Data
Our preliminary data demonstrates the feasibility of our approach to modulate the tumor microenvironment (TME) in brain metastases (BMs). We have shown that combining an anti-TREM2 antibody with cytokines induces robust pro-inflammatory macrophage activity in vitro, providing supporting evidence for our hypothesis that targeting TREM2 can reprogram the TME. We have also developed preliminary TREM2 immunocytokine models, including an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein, showcasing the technical feasibility of our proposed therapeutic modality. Furthermore, we have designed and tested masking strategies for the cytokine arm using TAM-specific protease-responsive blocking moieties in vivo. These pilot studies validate the methodology and establish a strong foundation for the proposed research. Our prior work using ZMAN-seq has enabled us to track the differentiation of monocytes to immunosuppressive TAMs and has revealed that TREM2 blockade redirects this trajectory towards a pro-inflammatory phenotype, thus providing key supporting evidence for our approach.

##### Research Tasks

###### Task 1.1: Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME

This research task will employ Zman-seq, a single-cell technology, to investigate the temporal dynamics of immune cell activity within the Bone Marrow (BM) Tumor Microenvironment (TME). The methodology adapts previously established Zman-seq protocols from glioma studies to the BM TME, utilizing fluorescent antibody time stamps to track transcriptomic changes in immune cells over time. In vivo, circulating immune cells will be labeled with a temporal sequence of fluorophores and tracked as they infiltrate the BM TME. Cells will be profiled using fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS), and their fluorescent profiles captured using index-sorting, allowing for the determination of tumor exposure time for individual cells. This approach enables the generation of temporal maps of the BM TME by combining tumor exposure time measurements with single-cell transcriptomic profiling. The specific fluorophores, antibody concentrations, and injection time points will require titration and optimization to ensure high resolution, temporal specificity, and minimal signal loss. Data analysis will involve translating single-cell time stamps into a continuous tumor exposure time axis for observing differentiation trajectories. Cumulative distribution functions (CDF) based on cell frequencies in defined time bins will be calculated, with the area under the curve (AUC) representing continuous tumor exposure time. Metacell analysis will be utilized to track transcriptional trajectories between cell states. ###### Task 1.2: Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME

Task 2 will employ PIC-seq to dissect immune cell interactions within the BM TME. This method, which combines cell sorting of physically interacting cells with scRNA-seq, allows for the profiling of cellular interactions and their impact on gene expression. PIC-seq uses a mild tissue dissociation protocol to preserve in situ cellular structures, followed by fluorescence sorting using mutually exclusive cell type markers to obtain mixtures of cell aggregates (putative PICs) and single cells. Massively parallel scRNA-seq will then generate gene expression profiles for these PICs, which are computationally deconvolved to infer transcriptional states and distinguish them from those of non-interacting single cells. This approach contrasts with traditional scRNA-seq, which requires single-cell suspensions, thereby disrupting native cell-cell interactions. The task will leverage the orthotopic immunocompetent murine models for melanoma BMs developed by the Weiss group and patient samples collected by the Weiss group and collaborators in hospitals in Israel. These samples will be processed for PIC-seq analysis to measure the interactions between immune cells and other cell types within the BM TME. The resulting data will be analyzed using computational methods to deconvolve the transcriptional states of the interacting cells and identify key regulatory programs. This task is dependent on the successful development of the BM TME models in Task 1.1, and will complement the temporal (Task 1.1) and spatial data (Task 1.3) from Aim 1, providing a comprehensive multimodal dataset. While PIC-seq has the potential to characterize cell-cell interactions in vivo, the transcriptional profiles of interacting cells need to be sufficiently 'distant' for deconvolution by the algorithm, a potential challenge to address.

###### Task 1.3: Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME

This research task will employ Stereo-seq technology to investigate the spatial distribution of immune cells within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME). Utilizing orthotopic immunocompetent murine models for melanoma BMs, and patient clinical samples, this study aims to generate high-resolution spatial transcriptomic data. Stereo-seq, a DNB-based spatial transcriptomics method, will enable a detailed mapping of cell types, including infiltrating immune cells, and their interactions. The technology's high resolution and large field of view allows for efficient image-based cell segmentation. However, it is noted that segmentation may be imperfect in areas with closely packed cell types such as immune cells. The Stereo-seq method involves in situ RNA capture, cDNA amplification, and sequencing, followed by computational analysis for high-resolution spatial transcriptomics. Data analysis will include spatially constrained clustering (SCC) to identify tissue domains and cell type mapping using annotated scRNA-seq datasets via methods such as Tangram to validate cell type distribution patterns. This task is dependent on the successful completion of Task 1.3, as it uses Stereo-seq data generated in that task, and will complement the temporal (1.1) and cell-cell interaction (1.2) data. The integration of these multi-modal datasets is critical for a holistic understanding of the BM TME. This research will build on prior studies using Stereo-seq in mouse models, including the mouse olfactory bulb and whole mouse embryos, with adaptations to the BM TME. While the technology offers genome-wide coverage, potential limitations in capturing low expression genes will be addressed by ensuring sufficient cell numbers for statistical analysis. Further, the high-throughput nature of Stereo-seq facilitates the analysis of large tissue areas, enabling comprehensive spatial profiling of the BM TME.

##### Risks and Alternatives

The primary risk involves the successful application of ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq to the melanoma BM TME, as these are advanced technologies with inherent complexities. Specifically, ZMAN-seq relies on temporal labeling with fluorescent antibodies and subsequent scRNA-seq, which could be hindered by inconsistent labeling or low cell recovery. Mitigation includes optimizing antibody concentrations and labeling protocols, and using multiple time points to ensure robust temporal resolution. PIC-seq depends on efficient capture of cell-cell interactions, which can be affected by tissue processing and RNA quality. We will optimize tissue handling protocols and implement rigorous quality control measures. Stereo-seq for high-resolution spatial transcriptomics presents a risk in terms of data analysis, requiring specialized algorithms for spatial clustering and cell segmentation. We will employ our previously developed SCC algorithm and validate with ISH data, using both segmentation and image-based methods. Another risk is the reliance on orthotopic immunocompetent murine models and patient clinical samples, with challenges regarding tumor engraftment rates and sample availability, and quality, respectively. We will mitigate this by utilizing multiple murine models and working closely with our clinical collaborators to secure sufficient and high-quality specimens. In vitro functional assays may not fully replicate the complex in vivo TME, we will address this by combining in vitro with in vivo results and using bone-marrow derived monocytes and co-culture systems to simulate the complex TME environment. If the selected cytokines or antibody target fail to show efficacy in vitro, we will conduct secondary screens utilizing additional cytokines and antibody targets. The dependence on in vivo models presents the risk of animal attrition; this will be mitigated using our standard practices for housing and animal care protocols, while also securing a sample size for statistical analysis. Furthermore, the inherent complexity of the BM TME and its dynamic nature may hinder the discovery of consistent and robust targets. Therefore, we will apply rigorous statistical analyses to identify consistent patterns across different experiments and models. The failure to identify therapeutically relevant targets will be mitigated by expanding the range of our in vitro screening assays, and a literature review of additional targets.

#### Objective 2: Preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models

This objective aims to preclinically screen cytokines identified in Aim 1 for their capacity to modulate the immunosuppressive BM TME using orthotopic immunocompetent murine models. These modified cell lines will be used to create tumor spheroids, either alone or in combination with other cytokine cassettes. These spheroids will then be co-cultured with immune cells, and cytokine combinations will be tested via doxycycline induction. Task 2.2, "In-vitro validation of cytokines", will further analyze the impact of cytokine perturbation on immune cell activity via co-culture assays with huMDMs, NK, or T cells. Cellular activity will be measured using flow cytometry to quantify proliferation, and cytokine secretion will be quantified by ELISA and qPCR. Task 2.3, "In-vivo validation of cytokines", will transition the most promising cytokine candidates to in vivo validation. B16 clones expressing the shortlisted cytokines will be used to generate melanoma BMs via intracardial injections. Cytokine expression will be activated with doxycycline, and tumor response will be assessed through MRI and survival measurements. Flow cytometry will be instrumental for identifying alterations in immune cell populations. MRI will serve as the primary imaging modality in Task 2.3. The expected outcomes are a prioritized list of cytokines and cytokine combinations that significantly enhance antitumor activity in vitro and in vivo. This objective directly relies on the results from Aim 1, specifically the list of cytokines with potential antitumor activity, while the outcome will inform the selection of cytokines for Aim 3 where they will be incorporated in immunocytokines.

## Experimental Design and Methods
This project employs a multi-faceted experimental design integrating advanced single-cell technologies with in vivo and in vitro validation, ensuring scientific rigor and reproducibility. Aim 1 utilizes orthotopic immunocompetent murine models of melanoma BMs, alongside human patient samples, to generate single-cell atlases of the TME. These atlases will be constructed using ZMAN-seq for cell temporal tracking, PIC-seq for cell-cell interaction analysis, and Stereo-seq for spatial transcriptomics. Data from these platforms will be integrated via AI-driven analysis to identify shared processes and pathways between murine models and human patient TMEs, identifying potential anti-tumor cytokines. Controls include TREM2-/- mice and wild-type counterparts, as well as untreated tumor-bearing mice for baseline comparison, as well as in-vitro cultures of cells not treated with the cytokines or anti-TREM2 antibodies. Aim 2 involves in vitro and in vivo screening of identified cytokines, employing tumor spheroids co-cultured with immune cells. Cytokine expression will be conditionally induced using doxycycline, and resulting changes will be assessed. In vivo, B16 melanoma cells expressing cytokines, or combinations, will be introduced intracardially, and tumor response will be monitored using MRI and survival metrics. Data analysis will include scRNA-seq to dissect the effects of the selected cytokines in combination with anti-TREM2 antibody on the myeloid and T cell populations. Controls include cells and mice treated with the anti-TREM2 antibody alone as well as with control cytokines. Aim 3 will focus on the design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines using the cytokines identified in Aim 2. The most effective cytokine candidates will be fused to anti-TREM2 antibodies targeting tumor antigens highly expressed in melanoma BMs. These immunocytokines will be evaluated in vivo using melanoma BM models, employing MRI and survival measurements to monitor tumor response, and scRNAseq to dissect the modulation of the TME. Reproducibility will be maintained by using standardized protocols and reagents and by performing each experiment in triplicate. Data will be managed using a centralized, secure database, complying with all ethical guidelines, with comprehensive metadata documenting all experimental parameters. The use of our cutting-edge technologies (ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, Stereo-seq) allows the collection of high-resolution multimodal data that will be integrated using robust AI algorithms, allowing high statistical power. This ensures the reliability and validation of the results.
## Statistical Plan
Statistical analysis of single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data, generated by ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq, will be performed using established biostatistics methods. Sample size calculations for in vivo experiments will be determined based on power analysis, aiming for a minimum of 8 mice per group to achieve 80% power to detect a statistically significant difference in tumor volume or survival with an alpha of 0.05. For scRNA-seq data, sample sizes will be determined based on the number of cells needed to detect differences in gene expression, aiming for at least 10,000 cells per condition to achieve statistical power to identify differential gene expression. Statistical tests will include t-tests or ANOVA for comparisons of continuous variables, and chi-squared tests or Fisher's exact tests for categorical variables. Differential gene expression analysis will be performed using DESeq2 or similar methods with adjusted p-values to correct for multiple testing. The MetaCell package will be used for pre-processing, quality control, and construction of metacells for data analysis. Spatial data from Stereo-seq will be analyzed using spatially constrained clustering algorithms (SCC). Temporal data from ZMAN-seq will be analyzed by generating cumulative distribution functions (CDF) and continuous tumor exposure time (cTET) values, allowing for analysis of cell trajectories and gene expression dynamics over time. Data interpretation will be grounded in biological relevance, supported by visualization tools to present gene expression changes and cell state transitions. Reproducibility will be ensured by adhering to rigorous statistical practices and detailed documentation of all analysis steps, as well as performing experiments in triplicate and using standardized methods. The statistical methods are chosen to maximize the information gained from the complex multimodal data sets, ensuring rigor and reproducibility, as well as enabling robust data interpretation.
