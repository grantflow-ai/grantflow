# Project Summary
## General Audience Abstract
This research project aims to improve outcomes for patients with melanoma, a serious skin cancer that can spread to the brain. Melanoma incidence is rising, and brain metastases significantly reduce survival rates. This project will focus on early detection, diagnosis, and treatment of melanoma, utilizing novel methods and existing therapies. The goal is to translate research findings into clinical practice, improving patient care and survival rates by optimizing treatment strategies. This study will explore biomarkers and combine therapies to maximize the impact of treatment and reduce negative effects, leading to better disease management and improving survival rates.
## Technical Abstract
This translational research project addresses the critical need for effective therapies for melanoma brain metastases (BMs), which exhibit poor prognosis and limited treatment options. The specific aims include: 1) developing comprehensive BM tumor microenvironment (TME) models using AI-driven analysis of single-cell atlases from murine models and patient samples to identify potential biomarkers; 2) preclinical screening of candidate cytokines for their ability to convert the immunosuppressive TME into an immunologically active state using in vitro and in vivo models; and 3) designing tumor-targeting immunocytokines using potent cytokines to eradicate melanoma BMs. The research design involves creating orthotopic immunocompetent murine models of melanoma BMs, employing advanced technologies for single-cell analysis (ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, spatial transcriptomics), and developing cytokine-secreting clones for in vitro and in vivo screening. Expected outcomes include identification of key cytokines and optimized immunocytokine constructs with high anti-tumor activity. This project aims to define novel biomarkers and therapeutic strategies, and to provide the most effective constructs for IND stage, thereby having a significant clinical impact on melanoma brain metastases management.

# Project Description
## Background and Specific Aims
Melanoma incidence has risen significantly in developed nations, representing 1.7% of global cancer diagnoses and the fifth most common cancer in the US. Geographic variations in incidence correlate with pigmentation, with fair-skinned populations exhibiting higher rates. Melanoma is responsible for a disproportionate number of skin cancer deaths; thus, research into effective prevention, diagnosis, and treatment is critical. While early-stage melanoma has a high survival rate, metastatic melanoma (MM) is associated with poor prognosis and a high mortality rate, with brain metastasis being a major contributor. Current treatments for MBM include chemotherapy, targeted therapy, immunotherapy and radiotherapy, but challenges remain, particularly due to the blood-brain barrier (BBB) hindering drug delivery. Research gaps include the need for more reliable biomarkers for early MBM detection, the optimization of combined treatment approaches and the personalized approach to MBM. The scientific rationale of this project stems from the need to improve the treatment landscape of MBM by addressing these research gaps, focusing on early detection and effective treatment strategies for MBM to improve patient outcomes and survival rates. Specifically, this project aims to 1) identify novel biomarkers for early detection of melanoma brain metastases, 2) evaluate combined therapeutic strategies to overcome drug resistance in MBM and 3) assess the translational potential of these findings in preclinical models to guide clinical trial design. The rationale is that improved early detection, treatment efficacy, and personalized approaches will enhance survival and quality of life for melanoma patients with MBM.

## Research Plan

### Research Objectives

#### Objective 1: Developing BM TME models with holistic, multimodal AI-driven analysis

This research objective aims to elucidate the temporal dynamics, cellular interactions, and spatial distribution of immune cells within the brain metastasis (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME). The central hypothesis is that understanding these parameters will reveal key targets for immunomodulatory therapies, shifting the TME from an immunosuppressive to an anti-tumor state. This will be achieved through three interconnected tasks. Task 1, 'Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME,' will employ Zman-seq, a single-cell technology that combines fluorescent temporal tracking with scRNA-seq, to measure the tumor exposure time of infiltrating immune cells. This approach will reveal the transcriptomic changes occurring as immune cells transition within the TME, identifying temporal gene expression trends. Specifically, the study will use a syngeneic murine model for melanoma BMs and a large cohort of patient clinical samples to determine the progression of immune cell states across time. This includes the transition of NK cells from cytotoxic to dysfunctional states and monocytes to immunosuppressive tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs). Task 2, 'Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME', will utilize PIC-seq to map cell-cell interactions, identifying receptor-ligand interactions that drive immune evasion in the TME. This task will complement the temporal data from Task 1, providing insights into the molecular mechanisms driving immune cell dysfunction. Task 3, 'Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME,' will leverage Stereo-seq for spatial transcriptomic analysis, providing a high-resolution map of immune and stromal cell distribution within the TME. This will identify areas of high immune infiltration and determine spatial relationships between different cell types. Together, these tasks will create a holistic, multimodal dataset describing the immune landscape of melanoma BM TMEs. These datasets will be analyzed using AI to identify shared processes and pathways between murine models and human patient TMEs. These studies will lead to a list of cytokines with high anti-tumor activity in melanoma BMs. The expected results include detailed single-cell atlases, temporal maps, cell-cell interaction networks, and spatial transcriptomic maps of the BM TME which are necessary to identify targets for effective immunotherapies. These are foundational to objectives 2 and 3.

##### Preliminary Results

Our prior research demonstrated that an anti-TREM2 antibody, when combined with cytokines, induces a strong pro-inflammatory macrophage response in vitro. We successfully produced preliminary TREM2 immunocytokine model molecules, including an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein, and developed a masking strategy utilizing TAM-specific protease-responsive blocking moieties, validated in vivo. These findings directly support the feasibility of our proposed immunomodulatory approach targeting the BM TME. Zman-seq, a technology we developed, allows for temporal tracking of transcriptomic changes in immune cells within the TME. Using this technology, we have shown that in a murine GBM model, cytotoxic NK cells transition to dysfunctional states within 24 hours of tumor infiltration, while monocytes differentiate into immunosuppressive TAMs over 36-48 hours, characterized by increased expression of Trem2, Il18bp, and Arg1. Furthermore, treatment with an anti-TREM2 antibody redirected the monocyte-to-macrophage trajectory, shifting it from an immunosuppressive to a pro-inflammatory phenotype, evidenced by the downregulation of Arg1, Pirb (LILRB3), Il18bp, Vegfa and Cd274 (PDL1) and upregulation of Ccl3, Ccl4, Cd81 and Cd83 (p<0.05, Wilcoxon test). This underscores the potential of TREM2 blockade in modulating the TME. Zman-seq data revealed that the monocyte-to-TAM differentiation trajectory involves early downregulation of monocytic transcripts (Chil3, Plac8) followed by time-dependent upregulation of immunosuppressive markers in control mice, while aTREM2 treatment resulted in an early upregulation of pro-inflammatory factors including Ccl4 and Cd81. Ligand-receptor analysis identified CCL3 and CCL4 as critical signaling hubs mediating TME reprogramming upon TREM2 blockade and a decrease in the CCL3 cognate receptor CCR5 on TAMs. These findings highlight the importance of both temporal dynamics and cell-cell interactions in the TME, further motivating the proposed studies. Although we have successfully applied Zman-seq to GBM, and the current proposal will utilize a melanoma model, the core mechanisms of monocyte to TAM transition are consistent across these tumor types. We have shown feasibility of Zman-seq in other tumor types, and have experience in adapting these technologies to new models, providing strong evidence for the success of the proposed work. The preliminary data provides a strong foundation for the proposed experiments aimed at generating detailed single-cell atlases, temporal maps, cell-cell interaction networks, and spatial transcriptomic maps of the BM TME, which will be crucial for identifying targets for effective immunotherapies, specifically through the use of our immunocytokine designs.

##### Research Tasks

###### Task 1.1: Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME

This research task will employ Zman-seq to investigate temporal changes in immune activity within the bone marrow (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME). This approach builds upon prior work adapting Zman-seq from glioma models to the BM context. Zman-seq, a technology combining fluorescent temporal tracking of cells with single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq), will be used to track immune cell exposure time in the BM TME. The experimental design involves in vivo labeling of circulating immune cells with a temporal sequence of fluorophores. These labeled cells will be tracked as they infiltrate the BM TME. Fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) will be used to isolate immune cells, and index-sorting will capture the fluorescent time stamps on individual cells, allowing inference of their time of BM TME infiltration. This will enable the determination of the tumor exposure time of each immune cell, which will then be correlated with single-cell transcriptomic profiles. Data analysis will involve reconstructing immune cell state transitions over time. Specifically, cumulative distribution function (CDF) analysis will be applied to assign continuous tumor exposure time (cTET) values on the metacell level. This will be used to define cellular trajectories and identify time-dependent gene modules, transcription factor (TF) circuits, and receptor-ligand interactions. Statistical analysis will include correlation plots and Spearman's correlation to assess gene expression trends over time. Specific analysis will focus on changes in expression of key genes, such as Trem2, Plac8, Xcl1, and homing receptors, as well as the temporal activity of transcription factors such as SMAD3 and SMAD4. This will allow for the grouping of leukocytes into tumor exposure time bins based on their fluorescent profiles. ###### Task 1.2: Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME

This research task will employ PIC-seq to dissect immune cell-cell interactions within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME). PIC-seq, a method that combines cell sorting of physically interacting cells with single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq), will enable the profiling of cellular interactions and their impact on gene expression. This approach involves a mild tissue dissociation protocol, maintaining some in situ cellular structures. Fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) using mutually exclusive cell type markers will yield cell aggregates, representing putative physically interacting cells (PICs), and single cells. Massively parallel scRNA-seq will then generate gene expression profiles for the PICs, which will be computationally deconvolved to infer their transcriptional states, contrasting them with those of non-interacting single cells. The computational deconvolution process requires that the transcriptional profiles of interacting cells are sufficiently 'distant' for accurate analysis. This task depends on prior data collection (Task 1.1) to provide necessary input for understanding the complex immune cell interactions within the BM TME. The analysis will involve using the scanpy framework for processing the scRNA-seq data, including normalization, log1p transformation, and retrieval of highly variable genes. Probabilistic modeling, using a framework similar to that used in the analysis of BMDM scRNA-seq data, may be employed to resolve the effects of experimental covariates. The data will be used to construct neighbor graphs and UMAP representations. A sample-sample distance matrix will be computed for further analysis. Dot plots of selected genes will be generated to aid interpretation of the results. This will allow for a detailed characterization of the immune cell interactions within the BM TME and their influence on cell state. Further analysis may involve examining expression of regulatory T cell markers and co-stimulatory genes. **MISSING INFORMATION: Specific markers for cell sorting are not mentioned and need to be defined.**

###### Task 1.3: Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME

Task 3 will employ Stereo-seq to spatially characterize immune cell distribution within the bone marrow (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME). Stereo-seq, which combines DNA nanoball-patterned arrays and tissue RNA capture, enables large field-of-view spatial transcriptomics at cellular resolution. This approach will provide an unbiased assessment of transcriptomic organization and cell-type composition within the BM TME. The methodology involves in situ RNA capture, cDNA amplification, library construction, and sequencing. Data analysis will utilize spatially constrained clustering (SCC) algorithms to identify tissue domains and assess the spatial distribution of immune cell types. Specifically, the analysis will be optimized for larger, complex tissues, accounting for spatial coordinates which are critical for spatially resolved data. Data processing will include mapping reads using STAR and collapsing UMI with the same CID and gene locus, allowing one mismatch to correct for sequencing and PCR errors, integrated into the publicly available pipeline SAW. Image-based cell segmentation will also be performed by registering the DNB image with nucleic acid staining, using Scikit-image to perform cell segmentation analysis, and aggregating UMI from all DNB within the corresponding segmentation per-gene, generating a cell by gene matrix for downstream analysis. This task is dependent on the data collection from Task 1.1, in which initial spatial transcriptomic data will be collected. Spatial analysis and the use of Tangram for alignment of scRNA-seq data will be performed using existing datasets to complement data obtained using Stereo-seq. The output from this task will feed into Task 1.3, providing crucial information for spatial analysis and characterization of immune cell distribution within the BM TME. The expected outcome is a detailed spatial map of immune cell distribution and their transcriptomic organization in the BM TME.

#### Objective 2: Preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models

Objective 2 focuses on the preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models, building upon the cytokine identification efforts of Objective 1. The central hypothesis is that select cytokines, identified through in-vitro and in-vivo screening, will modulate the BM TME to promote anti-tumor immune activity. This aim is structured into three main tasks: Screening of cytokines in BM TME, In-vitro validation of cytokines, and In-vivo validation of cytokines. The initial task involves creating cytokine-secreting clones of brain-tropic melanoma cell lines, using a doxycycline-inducible system to express a broad range of candidate cytokines. These clones will be used to generate tumor spheroids, which will be co-cultured with immune cells to evaluate cytokine effects on immune cell activity. Cytokine combinations will be tested via doxycycline induction and evaluated using scRNA-seq to analyze changes in immune cell states and interactions. The second task will focus on in-vitro validation of the most promising cytokines and combinations, using similar co-culture assays of immune cells and tumor spheroids, with readouts including flow cytometry for proliferation, and ELISA and qPCR for cytokine secretion. Finally, the selected cytokines will undergo in-vivo validation, using B16 melanoma cell lines engineered to express the cytokines. These cells will be introduced via intracardial injections to establish melanoma BMs in mice. Cytokine expression will be activated via doxycycline administration and tumor response will be monitored using MRI and survival measurement. This will allow for the evaluation of the effect of the selected cytokines in vivo in the context of a complex TME, with additional scRNA-seq analysis of the tumor microenvironment at different time points. Common methodologies include cell culture, lentiviral transduction, flow cytometry, ELISA, qPCR, scRNA-seq, and in-vivo imaging, with the addition of MRI to monitor tumor burden. The expected result is the identification of a subset of cytokines with a demonstrable ability to modulate the BM TME in favor of anti-tumor activity, supported by comprehensive datasets of in-vitro and in-vivo studies, and providing a basis for the design of immunocytokines in Objective 3. This objective relies on the identification of cytokines in Objective 1 and will produce a list of cytokines for use in Objective 3.

This will be mitigated by employing a doxycycline-inducible system for cytokine expression, allowing precise temporal control and reduction of systemic exposure. Additionally, cytokine expression will be rigorously quantified using qPCR and ELISA. The reliance on specific cell lines introduces a risk of variability in experimental outcomes. Another risk is the potential for unsuccessful in-vivo validation due to poor tumor engraftment, a common issue in intracardial injection models. #### Objective 3: Design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines

This research objective focuses on the design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines to enhance anti-tumor immune responses in melanoma brain metastases (BMs). The central hypothesis is that by fusing potent, immune-reactivating cytokines with antibodies targeting tumor-associated antigens highly expressed in melanoma BMs, we can redirect cytokine activity to the tumor microenvironment (TME), thereby converting the immunosuppressive TME into an immunologically 'hot' one. This objective depends on the cytokine screening in Objective 2, leveraging identified cytokines to design immunocytokines. The tasks are structured to produce and validate these constructs, ultimately identifying the most effective candidates for IND studies.

The first task, 'Design fusion proteins and cleavage site', involves engineering antibody-cytokine fusion proteins. We will select species-conserved tumor antigens highly expressed in melanoma BMs as antibody targets, such as FAP, EDB, EGFR, CBD, integrin, PD-L1 and PD-1. The identified cytokines (e.g. IL-2, IL-10, IL-12, IL-15) from Aim 2 will be fused to the selected antibodies. The fusion will include a cleavable linker to ensure conditional activation of the cytokine within the TME. The second task, 'Produce fusion proteins', involves the production and purification of the designed immunocytokines. This will be done using recombinant expression systems, and purified using standard chromatography methods. The third task, 'In-vitro validation of immunocytokines', involves assessing the immunocytokines' activity in vitro using co-culture assays with immune cells and melanoma BM cell lines. Cytokine release, immune cell activation, and tumor cell killing will be assessed. This multi-faceted approach aims to break through the classical dichotomy of response/no-response by employing a model-driven optimization cycle using our advanced single-cell technologies to pinpoint the most effective constructs for further development. Expected outcomes include the identification of specific antibody-cytokine combinations that show high anti-tumor activity and minimal off-target effects, as well as optimized designs based on data-driven iterative models. The successful completion of this aim will provide robust preclinical data for moving these immunocytokine constructs forward to clinical testing.

##### Research Tasks

###### Task 3.1: Design fusion proteins and cleavage site

This research task focuses on the design of optimal fusion proteins, combining monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) with cytokines, building upon the top 3-5 mAb-cytokine combinations identified in previous work. Advanced protein design techniques will be employed, including the selection of peptide linkers, blocking moieties, and tumor-associated macrophage (TAM)-specific cleavage sites. Computational optimization will be used to ensure protein structure and stability. The design of the fusion proteins will incorporate tumor-specific properties, such as protease-mediated activation, potentially using masking domains or conditionally activated prodrug strategies to limit off-target effects. Specifically, the use of potency-reduced cytokine mutants will be explored to mitigate the 'cytokine sink' effect, potentially enhancing tumor accumulation of the fusion protein. The design will also consider the pharmacokinetic properties of the components and optimize the relative levels of each cytokine to achieve synergistic effects, accounting for varied pharmacological properties and half-lives. ##### Risks and Alternatives

A primary risk lies in the potential for off-target effects due to the chosen antibody's binding to normal cells, causing systemic toxicity. This risk will be mitigated by selecting highly specific tumor-associated antigens (FAP, EDB, EGFR, CBD, integrin, PD-L1, and PD-1) and incorporating Fc mutations (L234A/L235A or N297A) to reduce off-target FcγR activation and ADCC. Another risk involves the immunocytokines eliciting insufficient anti-tumor activity or inducing dose-dependent toxicities. To mitigate this, we will engineer cytokines with lower receptor affinity or use a tumor-conditional prodrug approach with a cleavable linker that is responsive to the TME, limiting systemic exposure. The screening of multiple cytokine-antibody combinations is a strategy to identify superior candidates. Methodological risks include potential challenges in producing high-quality, stable fusion proteins. This will be addressed by optimizing recombinant expression and purification using chromatography and rigorous in-vitro validation using co-culture assays to assess activity, immune cell activation and tumor cell killing. In case of insufficient activity using the identified cytokines (IL-2, IL-10, IL-12, IL-15) we will consider exploring novel cytokines, including IL-18 and IL-24. Furthermore, the risk of not being able to translate in-vitro results to in-vivo efficacy exists. We will mitigate this by testing multiple optimized lead candidates in melanoma BMs murine models. In case of insufficient anti-tumor activity or high toxicity in murine models, a detailed analysis of constructs, and re-engineering of the constructs will be performed.
