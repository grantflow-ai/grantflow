# Project Summary
## General Audience Abstract
This research project aims to tackle the significant challenge of melanoma brain metastases, which drastically reduce patient survival and quality of life. By employing cutting-edge single-cell technologies and artificial intelligence, we will gain a detailed understanding of the tumor's environment in the brain. This understanding will enable us to develop novel immunotherapies that specifically target and activate the immune system to fight cancer cells within the brain. Our approach includes a unique antibody that blocks immune-suppressing cells, combined with immune-stimulating molecules, and a masking strategy to ensure the therapy works only at the tumor site. This project's impact lies in the potential to transform treatment for melanoma brain metastases, significantly extending patient lives and improving their quality of life. The knowledge gained will pave the way for more effective and personalized cancer therapies.
## Technical Abstract
Metastatic melanoma frequently leads to brain metastases (BMs), characterized by a highly immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) and poor patient survival. This project addresses this critical need by employing advanced single-cell technologies including ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq, coupled with AI analysis, to dissect the BM TME. Our central hypothesis is that through a detailed understanding of the BM TME, including identification of cytokines capable of remodeling the TME towards anti-tumor activity, we can design novel immunocytokines that combine the most promising cytokines with our anti-TREM2 antibody. This approach aims to modulate both the myeloid and T and NK cell compartments. Our research will also focus on identifying biomarkers to enable masking strategies that enhance the specificity of the immunocytokine to the BM TME. The expected outcomes include a detailed characterization of the BM TME, identification of novel cytokine targets, and the development of a first-in-class immunocytokine with increased specificity to the BM TME. This translational research has the potential to significantly impact the clinical landscape by improving treatment options for melanoma BM patients.

# Project Description
## Background and Specific Aims
Brain metastases (BMs) occur in almost 50% of patients with metastatic melanoma, resulting in a dismal prognosis and poor overall survival. The brain's milieu creates a highly immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME), which contributes to treatment resistance. Immunotherapy has improved survival for some melanoma patients, but many still face relapse or non-response, highlighting critical research gaps. Specifically, single-cell technologies combined with machine learning have revealed a subtype of immune cell, regulatory (TREM2+) macrophages, that suppress the body's anti-cancer response, particularly in immunotherapy-resistant tumors. Our rationale is that advanced single-cell technologies enable an in-depth understanding of the immune environment of BMs and the identification of novel approaches for activating the immune system to eliminate these tumors. Moreover, simultaneously modulating different immune cell compartments (macrophages, T cells, NK cells) can induce unprecedented anti-tumor immune response. Our hypothesis is that using advanced single-cell technologies, including cell temporal tracking (ZMAN-seq), cell-cell interactions (PIC-seq), and improved AI analysis of spatial transcriptomics (Stereo-seq), we can achieve an in-depth understanding of the BM TME and identify cytokines capable of remodeling the TME towards anti-tumor activity. This will enable us to design novel immunocytokines combining promising cytokines with our anti-TREM2 antibody to modulate both myeloid and T and NK cell compartments. Furthermore, our analysis will also enable the identification of biomarkers for designing masking strategies to increase the specificity of the immunocytokine to the BM TME. This approach has strong translational potential to improve treatment outcomes for melanoma patients with brain metastases.
## Preliminary Data
Our preliminary data demonstrates the feasibility of our approach to modulate the tumor microenvironment (TME) in brain metastases (BMs). We have shown that an anti-TREM2 antibody combined with cytokines induces strong pro-inflammatory macrophage activity in vitro, providing supporting evidence for our hypothesis. We have also successfully produced preliminary TREM2 immunocytokine model molecules, including an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein, showing the feasibility of creating these constructs. Furthermore, we designed and validated a masking strategy for the cytokine arm using blocking moieties responsive only to tumor-associated macrophage (TAM)-specific proteases, and successfully tested them in vivo, demonstrating the potential for targeted drug delivery to the BM TME. These key findings lay a robust foundation for our proposed research plan.
## Research Plan

### Research Objectives

#### Objective 1: Developing BM TME models with holistic, multimodal AI-driven analysis

This research objective aims to characterize the melanoma brain metastases (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME) to identify novel targets for immunotherapeutic intervention. The central hypothesis is that a comprehensive, multimodal analysis of the BM TME, utilizing advanced single-cell technologies and AI-driven approaches, will reveal critical mechanisms of immunosuppression and enable the design of targeted immunocytokine therapies. To achieve this, three interconnected tasks will be undertaken. The first task, "Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME," will employ ZMAN-seq to track the dynamic changes in immune cell states and gene expression patterns over time within the BM TME. This will involve using orthotopic immunocompetent murine models of melanoma BMs, combined with clinical samples from patients. The murine models will be created by the Weiss group, and clinical samples will be provided by the Weiss group and collaborators in Israeli hospitals. The goal is to generate a time-resolved atlas of immune cell trajectories and identify early molecular events associated with the transition to a dysfunctional state. The second task, "Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME," will focus on deciphering the complex intercellular communication networks within the TME. This will use PIC-seq to map cell-cell interactions, providing insights into the ligand-receptor dynamics that drive immunosuppression. The third task, "Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME," will employ Stereo-seq to analyze the spatial organization of immune and tumor cells within the BM. This will allow us to determine the spatial relationships and potential cell-cell interactions that contribute to the immunosuppressive TME. The integration of these multi-modal data sets will be achieved through AI-driven analysis, which will identify shared processes and pathways between our murine models and human patient TMEs. This will enable the compilation of a list of cytokines with high potential for anti-tumor activity in melanoma BMs. The expected outcome is a comprehensive understanding of the immune landscape of the BM TME, including time-dependent changes, cell-cell communication networks, spatial organization and key molecular drivers of immunosuppression. This data-driven approach will enable the rational design of immunocytokine therapies to effectively reprogram the BM TME from immunologically "cold" to "hot", thus, enhancing anti-tumor immunity.

##### Preliminary Results

Our preliminary data strongly supports the feasibility of our proposed approach. We have previously demonstrated the synergistic effect of combining an anti-TREM2 antibody with cytokines to induce a robust pro-inflammatory macrophage response in vitro. Specifically, we observed that blocking TREM2 with an antagonistic antibody redirected monocyte differentiation trajectory towards pro-inflammatory TAMs, with a corresponding increase in pro-inflammatory signaling and a decrease in suppressive signaling. This effect is accompanied by a significant increase in oxidative phosphorylation and polyamine metabolism. Temporal ligand-receptor analysis revealed that chemokines such as CCL3 and CCL4 are important signaling hubs mediating TME reprogramming upon TREM2 blockade. In contrast to CCL4, which recruits TAMs, CCL3 is pro-inflammatory and recruits T and NK cells. Critically, we have also confirmed the feasibility of producing TREM2 immunocytokines, developing preliminary model molecules such as an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein. Additionally, we have designed a masking strategy for the cytokine arm using blocking moieties responsive to TAM-specific proteases, which have been tested in vivo. These blocking moieties are designed to be cleaved only in the TME by TAM-specific proteases. Further, our prior work in glioblastoma models using ZMAN-seq has shown that within 24 hours of tumor infiltration, cytotoxic NK cells rapidly transition to a dysfunctional state, while infiltrating monocytes differentiate into immunosuppressive macrophages over 36 to 48 hours. We have also demonstrated that anti-TREM2 treatment can redirect the monocyte-to-macrophage trajectory from immunosuppressive to pro-inflammatory, and that ZMAN-seq has the capacity to resolve temporal dynamics of the TME and identify key gene modules involved in immune cell differentiation. Moreover, the data revealed that TREM2 inhibition reprogrammed the monocyte-to-regulatory macrophage trajectory, and the temporal molecular circuits downstream of TREM2 inhibiting the transition of circulating monocytes conversion to suppressive TAM. This suggests that our planned experiments using ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq, combined with AI-driven analysis, can help us identify the critical factors and pathways driving immunosuppression in melanoma BMs, as well as cytokines with high potential for anti-tumor activity.

##### Research Tasks

###### Task 1.1: Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME

This research task will employ Zman-seq to investigate the temporal dynamics of immune cell activity within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME). Our approach builds upon previous work that successfully adapted Zman-seq from glioma models to the bone marrow. The Zman-seq technology will be utilized to track transcriptomic changes in immune cells over time by introducing fluorescent antibody time stamps into circulating immune cells, which can then be tracked in the BM TME from hours to days post labeling. This method allows for empirical measurements of the differentiation trajectories of these immune cells. The data generated will allow us to analyze how the BM TME influences the functional states of infiltrating immune cells, identifying temporal sequences of cell-cell interactions and their impact on cell state. Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data will be combined with the temporal information from Zman-seq to construct temporal maps of the BM TME. The experimental design will involve a pulse-chase approach using fluorescently labeled circulating immune cells in a syngeneic model for BM TME. The labeled leukocytes can be tracked upon entry into the bone marrow. Fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) will be used to profile the immune cells within the tumor. The temporal dynamics of NK cells and myeloid cells will be investigated using continuous tumor exposure time (cTET) values that will be computed based on the frequency of cells in time bins (e.g., 12h, 24h, and 36h). Gene expression trends, including changes in homing receptors, chemoattractants, and myeloid checkpoints, will be analyzed in relation to the time of tumor infiltration. Ligand-receptor interaction analysis will be conducted to reveal factors driving the immune cell trajectories.

###### Task 1.2: Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME

This research task will employ PIC-seq to investigate immune cell interactions within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME). PIC-seq, a technology that combines cell sorting of physically interacting cells with single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) and computational modeling, will be used to profile these interactions and their impact on gene expression. This method allows for the study of cell-cell interactions that are often disrupted by the single-cell suspension requirements of standard scRNA-seq. The BM TME model developed in Task 1.1 is essential for this task as it will provide the in vivo system for this analysis. Specifically, the orthotopic immunocompetent murine models for melanoma BMs developed by the Weiss group and patient clinical samples will serve as the foundation of the BM TME models. The study will leverage the ability of PIC-seq to distinguish between physically interacting cells (PICs) and non-interacting single cells, enabling a detailed analysis of transcriptional states and how cell-cell interactions influence them. This will be accomplished through the isolation of interacting cell aggregates, representing PICs, and subsequent scRNA-seq, followed by computational deconvolution. The analysis will include the identification of unique co-stimulatory genes induced in antigen-presenting PICs. Data will be analyzed to characterize the temporal sequence of cell-cell interactions within the BM TME and to determine their influence on cell state. The computational analysis will involve utilizing tools for cell sorting of physically interacting cells and scRNA-seq data processing using scanpy, as well as the deconvolution algorithms specific to PIC-seq to infer the transcriptomic states of interacting cells and contrast them to non-interacting cells. This task will leverage single-cell atlases of immune, stromal, and tumor cells generated from murine and human BMs.

###### Task 1.3: Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME

This task will employ Stereo-seq technology to investigate the spatial distribution of immune cells within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME). The Stereo-seq approach will enable high-resolution spatial transcriptomics, allowing for the identification of cell types and their spatial organization within the BM TME. Specifically, the methodology involves the use of DNA nanoball-patterned arrays for in situ RNA capture, where uniquely barcoded oligonucleotides with UMI-polyT sequences are ligated onto each spot, followed by cDNA amplification and sequencing. The resulting data will be analyzed using a spatially constrained-clustering (SCC) algorithm, optimized for larger and more complex tissues, to identify distinct tissue domains based on gene expression profiles. Image-based cell segmentation will be performed to accurately map cell types, followed by integration with single-cell RNA-seq data using a k-nearest neighbors (knn) based method to validate cell type identities. To assess lateral RNA diffusion, the spatial distribution of transcripts for nuclear-localized RNAs (Malat1 and Neat1), mitochondrial RNAs, and specific neuropeptides (Vip) will be analyzed. Statistical comparisons of gene expression profiles between different spatial regions and cell types will be conducted using appropriate statistical methods, such as t-tests or ANOVA, followed by multiple testing corrections. The Stereo-seq technology offers a large field of view, capturing a significant portion of the tissue, while maintaining cellular resolution, which is critical for studying complex tissue microenvironments. This approach will overcome limitations of cell segmentation in traditional methods by performing image-based cell segmentation, thereby enabling more accurate spatial mapping of cell types. The bone marrow tumor microenvironment model developed in Task 1.1 will be essential to conduct this analysis. The spatial data obtained from this task will be used to refine the understanding of immune cell localization and interactions, contributing to a detailed spatial map of the BM TME.

##### Risks and Alternatives

A primary risk involves the inherent complexity of analyzing single-cell data from murine models and human clinical samples. Variability in sample quality and the potential for batch effects during sequencing could introduce bias. To mitigate this, we will employ rigorous quality control measures, including the use of standardized protocols, spike-in controls, and advanced computational methods for batch correction. Furthermore, we acknowledge the risk of model-specific artifacts in the murine models that may not fully recapitulate human BM TME. We will address this by a comparative analysis of both murine and human data with AI-driven methods to identify conserved pathways and therapeutic targets that are relevant to the human condition. Another risk is the potential failure to identify specific cell-cell interactions or spatial relationships that are critical for immunosuppression due to technological limitations in spatial transcriptomics and PIC-seq. To overcome this, we will use multiple methods to validate our findings, focusing on consistency across different experimental conditions. In case of poor performance of any of our primary methods we will explore alternative methodologies like co-immunoprecipitation assays combined with mass spectrometry to validate the PIC-seq findings and the use of multiplexed immunofluorescence to validate our findings using the Stereo-seq. Finally, there is a risk that the identified immunocytokines may not translate into effective therapies due to unpredictable responses. To address this, we will develop a robust in-vitro system for functional screening of the selected immunocytokines prior to in vivo testing in our established orthotopic mouse models.

#### Objective 2: Preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models

The objective of Aim 2 is to perform preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models to identify promising candidates for modulating the immune activity within the brain tumor microenvironment (TME). This aim builds upon the findings of Aim 1, which establishes the BM TME models and provides the necessary single-cell atlases of immune, stromal, and tumor cells for data-driven screening. We hypothesize that specific cytokines, identified through Aim 1's AI-driven analysis, can shift the balance of the BM TME from immunosuppressive to anti-tumor. To test this, we will employ a multi-faceted approach utilizing both in vitro and in vivo platforms. Initially, the Amit group will generate cytokine-secreting clones of brain-tropic melanoma cell lines using inducible (dox-induced) expression of a broad spectrum of cytokines identified in Aim 1. These clones will be utilized in tumor spheroid co-culture systems with immune cells, where varied cytokine combinations will be tested using doxycycline induction. The in vitro screening of cytokines and their combinations will be performed by monitoring cell viability and immune cell activation using techniques such as flow cytometry and ELISA. Subsequently, the most effective cytokines and combinations identified from in vitro validation will be tested in vivo. The Weiss group will generate melanoma brain metastases via intracardial injection of B16 clones expressing the selected cytokines. Cytokine expression will be activated using doxycycline and the tumor response will be monitored through MRI and survival measurements. This data will then undergo scRNA-seq analysis to obtain in-depth understanding of the effects of the perturbation created by treatment and data-driven selection of the 3-5 most promising cytokines with modulatory function. A key component of this analysis includes single-cell temporal tracking (ZMAN-seq), cell-cell interactions (PIC-seq), and improved strategies for AI analysis of spatial transcriptomics with Stereo-seq to determine cellular kinetics and molecular changes of leukocytes, including the precise trajectories of newly infiltrating immune cells and the molecular circuits involved in the development of dysfunctional immune cell states within the TME.

Our preliminary studies have demonstrated the potential of combining cytokines with immunomodulatory antibodies to enhance anti-tumor immunity. Specifically, we have shown that an anti-TREM2 antibody synergizes with certain cytokines, leading to strong pro-inflammatory macrophage activation in vitro. This finding aligns with our objective to identify cytokines that can shift the balance of the BM TME from immunosuppressive to anti-tumor. Furthermore, we have successfully generated and tested preliminary model molecules of TREM2 immunocytokines, such as an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein, demonstrating the feasibility of our approach. To enhance the specificity of cytokine delivery, we have designed a masking strategy for the cytokine arm, using blocking moieties responsive only to TAM-specific proteases, and have successfully tested this strategy in vivo, mitigating potential off-target effects. These preliminary results provide a strong foundation for Aim 2. They confirm our ability to produce and test cytokine-based therapies and validate our targeted approach to modulate the immune microenvironment of brain metastases (BM). We have also developed an in vitro system for bone-marrow-derived monocyte diversification toward the M2 suppressor macrophage state, and a control culture of monocytes derived from TREM2-/- mice. This system will be used for in vitro functional screening to identify the most effective cytokines and cytokine combinations for immune cell activation, using techniques such as flow cytometry and ELISA. In addition, we have developed a platform to over-express cytokines in an inducible fashion by barcoded tumor cell lines. The 5-8 most promising cytokines selected from in vitro studies will be cloned into our vectors and screened in combination with our anti-TREM2 mAbs in vivo, with scRNA-seq analysis to make a data-driven selection of the 3-5 cytokines with the most promising modulatory function. This is all supported by our established methodologies for single-cell analysis, which will enable us to obtain an in-depth understanding of the effects of the perturbation created by treatment.

##### Research Tasks

###### Task 2.1: Screening of cytokines in BM TME

This research task focuses on screening a library of in-house cytokines to identify those that modulate immune activity within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME). The initial step involves utilizing an in-vitro system for bone-marrow-derived monocyte differentiation towards the M2 suppressor macrophage state, incorporating a control culture of monocytes from TREM2-/- mice. This system will be used to co-culture myeloid and NK-T cells, followed by the application of single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) to comprehensively analyze the impact of selected cytokines. The cytokine library, comprising approximately 30 different cytokines, including CCL3, CSCL7, CCL26, TGFBRII, IL-1, IL-2, IL-6, IL-7, IL-12, IL-15, IL-18, IL-21, IFNa, IFNb, IFNg, CCL1, CCL2, CCL3, CCL5, CCL7, CCL8/MCP-2, CCL11, CCL13/MCP-4, CCL14/HCC-1, CCL17/CTAC, CCL19, CCL22, CCL23, CCL24, CCL26, CCL27, lymphotactin/XCL1, Eotaxin, CXCL6/GCP-2, CXCL7/NAP-2, CXCL8, CXCL10, CXCL11/ITAC, CXCL12, CXCL13, and CXCL15 will be screened in combination with the E3C7 mAb. The effects on immune cell activity will be evaluated by performing in-vitro assays of co-cultured huMDMs, NK, or T cells, and compared to controls without the anti-TREM2 mAb or cytokines. Immune cell activity will be measured by quantifying proliferative potential using flow cytometry and assessing cytokine secretion through ELISA and qPCR. Cytotoxic T and NK cell activity will be assessed through killing assays involving co-cultured huMDMs, NK or T cells and tumor cells treated with various cytokine and E3C7 mAb combinations, with tumor cell death quantified using FACS. Data collected from these assays will be combined to identify 8-10 cytokine and anti-TREM2 mAb combinations with the most promising potential for down-regulating myeloid cell suppressive activity and enhancing myeloid and T cell inflammatory activity. The most promising 5-8 cytokines identified will then be cloned into inducible expression vectors in tumor cell lines for in vivo screening in combination with anti-TREM2 mAbs, utilizing scRNA-seq for data-driven selection of the top 3-5 cytokines. The effects of the cytokines in combination with the E3C7 mAb on inducing immune cell activity will be also evaluated.

###### Task 2.2: In-vitro validation of cytokines

Task 2 focuses on the in-vitro validation of cytokines identified in Task 1. Specifically, Task 2.2 is contingent upon the results of Task 2.1, which involves screening cytokines in the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME) to determine which cytokines will be used for validation. This validation process will utilize in-vitro models, which will include the use of cytokine-secreting clones of brain tropic melanoma cell lines. These clones will be engineered by transducing the cell lines to conditionally express a range of candidate cytokines that are known to promote BM immune re-activation. Tumor spheroids will then be generated with these clones, which may contain single or multiple cytokine cassettes. The spheroids will be co-cultured with immune cells, and varied cytokine combinations will be tested by the addition of doxycycline. The most effective cytokines and cytokine combinations identified from these in-vitro assays will be advanced for further in-vivo testing. Additionally, single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) analysis will be employed to understand the impact of cytokine treatments on the co-cultured cells. Furthermore, the in-vitro validation will include assays to assess the impact of the cytokines in combination with anti-TREM2 mAb on inducing immune cell activity, employing co-cultured human monocyte-derived macrophages (huMDMs), NK, or T cells. Cell activity will be measured by quantifying the proliferative potential via flow cytometry and assessing cytokine secretion using ELISA and qPCR. Cytotoxicity will be measured by quantifying tumor cell death using FACS.

###### Task 2.3: In-vivo validation of cytokines

Task 3 will focus on the in-vivo validation of cytokines identified as promising modulators of myeloid, NK, and T cell activity from in-vitro experiments, specifically task 2.2. This validation will employ a single-cell analysis approach utilizing an in-vivo functional screening system. The system will involve barcoded tumor cell lines engineered to inducibly overexpress the selected 3-5 cytokines. These cytokines will be screened in combination with our anti-TREM2 mAbs in vivo. The experiments will utilize a platform we developed which includes a temporal tracking system named Zman-seq which combines fluorescent temporal tracking of cells with scRNA-seq. We will intravenously inject mice with fluorescently labeled anti-CD45 antibodies at different time points to allow for tracking of immune cells. This will enable a temporal analysis of immune cell infiltration and state transitions by applying our in-depth scRNA-seq pipeline. This pipeline allows us to define the time each immune cell spends in the tumor. The temporal maps created will enable a data-driven selection of the most promising cytokines. The analysis will focus on the impact of the selected cytokines on myeloid, NK, and T cell activity within the tumor microenvironment. We will use flow cytometry to quantify proliferative potential and ELISA and qPCR to quantify cytokine secretion. Data from Task 2.2 will be used for comparison. This in-vivo analysis will build on the single cell analysis established in task 1.5, which incorporates scRNA-seq to make a data-driven selection of the 3-5 cytokines with the most promising modulatory function in combination with our anti-TREM2 mAb.

##### Risks and Alternatives

A primary risk in Aim 2 is the potential for limited efficacy of selected cytokines in modulating the immunosuppressive brain tumor microenvironment (TME), despite promising in vitro results. This could stem from the complex in vivo milieu, where factors like metabolic limitations and suppressive secreted factors may hinder cytokine activity. To mitigate this, we will employ a multi-faceted approach, including testing various cytokine combinations and utilizing a temporal single-cell analysis (ZMAN-seq) to track the precise trajectories of immune cell infiltration and dysfunction. An alternative strategy would be to shift focus towards cytokines with established clinical benefits, such as IL-2, IL-12, IL-15, and GM-CSF, if initial screening fails. Another risk is the technical challenge of generating stable cytokine-secreting clones and ensuring consistent, inducible cytokine expression in vivo. We will mitigate this through rigorous clone validation and the use of a dox-inducible expression system for tight temporal control. If this approach fails to provide sufficient control of cytokine expression, we will use alternative vector designs and transduction protocols. Furthermore, potential off-target effects or systemic toxicity of cytokines remain a risk. To address this, we will focus on using immunocytokines which can be targeted to the tumor site, minimizing systemic exposure. If direct cytokine delivery proves to be too toxic or inefficient, we will consider cytokine engineering strategies to reduce systemic toxicity. Finally, there is a risk of challenges in the AI analysis of spatial transcriptomics. To mitigate this, the group will implement improved strategies for AI analysis of spatial transcriptomics with Stereo-seq, while also incorporating data from ZMAN-seq and PIC-seq.

#### Objective 3: Design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines

The overarching goal of Objective 3 is to engineer tumor-targeting immunocytokines that can effectively modulate the immunosuppressive brain metastases (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME) into an immunologically active state. This will be achieved by designing antibody-cytokine fusion proteins that target species-conserved tumor antigens highly expressed in melanoma BMs, utilizing insights from single-cell analysis performed in Objective 1 and the lead mAb-cytokine combinations identified in Objective 2. The hypothesis is that targeted delivery of potent cytokines via immunocytokines will reactivate the immune compartment within the BM TME, overcoming the classical dichotomy of response/no-response to cancer therapies. The design process will involve advanced protein engineering techniques, including the selection of appropriate peptide linkers, blocking moieties, and tumor-associated antigen (TAM)-specific cleavage sites, while optimizing protein structure and stability. Specifically, Task 2.1 focuses on the design of these fusion proteins, leveraging computational modeling to achieve optimal configuration, with consideration of the positioning of the cytokine moiety to enable adaptable immunostimulatory activity upon antigen binding. Task 2.2 encompasses the production of 3-5 mAb-cytokine fusion proteins through a contract research organization (CRO), ensuring selection of stable molecules with high protein expression and minimal aggregation. Subsequently, Task 2.3 will validate the binding and activity of the produced immunocytokines using surface plasmon resonance (SPR), ELISA, cell-based binding, and reporter assays. This comprehensive in-vitro validation will provide crucial data regarding the functional properties of the engineered immunocytokines. Additionally, Task 2.4 will assess the immunocytokine's impact on interactions between myeloid and lymphoid cell activity through co-culture assays with human monocyte-derived macrophages (huMDMs), natural killer (NK) cells, and T cells. Lastly, Task 2.5 focuses on validating the efficacy of these fusion proteins in inducing cytotoxic NK and T cell activity through co-culture assays with huMDMs, NK, T cells, and tumor cells, using the data collected in Objective 1 as control. This iterative cycle of design, production, and validation will pinpoint the most effective constructs for subsequent preclinical evaluation. The success of this objective is directly linked to the identification of lead mAb-cytokine combinations in Objective 2 and will inform the selection of the most promising immunocytokines for IND-enabling studies.

##### Research Tasks

###### Task 3.1: Design fusion proteins and cleavage site

This task focuses on the design of optimal fusion protein structures combining monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) with cytokines. The top 3-5 mAb-cytokine combinations identified in Aim 2 will be used as a basis for this task. Advanced protein design techniques, including computational optimization of protein structure and stability, will be employed to develop these structures. The design process will involve the selection of peptide linkers, blocking moieties, and tumor-associated macrophage (TAM)-specific cleavage sites to ensure targeted delivery and activation of the fusion proteins. These designs will be engineered to minimize off-target effects while maximizing therapeutic efficacy. The selection of appropriate peptide linkers will be crucial for maintaining the structural integrity and biological activity of both the mAb and cytokine components of the fusion proteins. Furthermore, blocking moieties will be designed to prevent premature activation of the cytokine, ensuring its activity is restricted to the tumor microenvironment. The inclusion of TAM-specific cleavage sites will allow for controlled release of the cytokine within the tumor, promoting local immune activation. The computational optimization of protein structure and stability will ensure that the fusion proteins are stable and well-folded, thus maintaining their biological activity and reducing the risk of aggregation. The fusion protein designs will be evaluated in silico prior to experimental validation. This will involve simulations of the protein structure, stability and potential interactions with other molecules. The methodology will leverage prior experience with leading CROs to ensure high quality protein production and the selection of stable molecules with high protein expression and no aggregation.

###### Task 3.2: Produce fusion proteins

This task involves the production of 3-5 mAb-cytokine fusion proteins, building upon the designs developed in Task 2.1. The protein synthesis will be outsourced to a contract research organization (CRO), selected based on prior experience with leading CROs, prioritizing quality and punctual production. The production process includes the selection of stable molecules exhibiting high protein expression and minimal aggregation. The fusion proteins will be manufactured using established protein synthesis techniques, with a focus on ensuring the structural integrity and biological activity of both the mAb and cytokine components. This process will involve rigorous quality control measures to confirm the identity, purity, and stability of the produced fusion proteins. The selection of suitable peptide linkers and blocking moieties, along with the computational optimization of protein structure and stability from the designs developed in Task 2.1, will be implemented during the synthesis to produce functional fusion proteins. This task is directly dependent on the designs generated in Task 2.1 which specifies the mAb-cytokine combinations, and will inform subsequent validation of the produced proteins in Task 2.3, where the binding activity will be confirmed via SPR, ELISA, cell-based binding, and reporter assays, and also be used to assess the impact of fusion proteins on cell activity in Tasks 2.4 and 2.5. The produced fusion proteins will be used to validate the MiTE activity in both NK and T cell killing assays.

###### Task 3.3: In-vitro validation of immunocytokines

Task 3 focuses on the in-vitro validation of the generated immunocytokines. Initial validation will confirm binding characteristics via Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR), Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay (ELISA), cell-based binding assays, and reporter assays. These methods will establish the specific interactions between the immunocytokines and their target receptors. The subsequent stage will investigate the immunocytokines' impact on myeloid and lymphoid cell interactions. This involves co-culturing human monocyte-derived macrophages (huMDMs) with either Natural Killer (NK) cells or T cells to assess changes in cellular activity. Furthermore, the efficacy of the fusion proteins in inducing cytotoxic activity will be evaluated. Co-cultures of huMDMs, NK cells, or T cells, with tumor cells will be treated with various monoclonal antibody-cytokine chimeras. The resulting cytotoxic effects on tumor cells will be quantified, providing insight into the therapeutic potential of the immunocytokines. These experiments build upon the data collected in Task 1.2, where similar killing assays are conducted using E3C7 mAb and cytokines. Task 2.5 also utilizes similar co-culture assays to measure MiTE activity. These assays will measure NK and T cell activity and cytotoxicity levels. The results from these assays will be used to evaluate the ability of the fusion proteins to activate immune responses and will directly contribute to understanding the mechanism of action of the immunocytokine products, focusing on the activation of NK and T cell-mediated cytotoxicity.

##### Risks and Alternatives

The primary risk associated with Objective 3 is the potential failure to produce stable and functional immunocytokines with the desired binding and activity profiles. This could arise from challenges in protein engineering, including suboptimal linker design, poor cytokine positioning, or issues with the selected TAM-specific cleavage sites. The use of a contract research organization (CRO) introduces a dependency on external resources, with potential delays or inconsistencies in production. The selection of stable molecules with high protein expression and minimal aggregation is a key hurdle. Mitigation strategies include rigorous computational modeling during the design phase (Task 2.1), extensive in-vitro validation using SPR, ELISA, and cell-based assays (Task 2.3), and thorough testing of the immunocytokine's impact on myeloid and lymphoid cell activity. If the initial designs fail, alternative strategies could include exploring different linker lengths and compositions, re-positioning the cytokine moiety, or using alternative tumor-associated antigen (TAM)-specific cleavage sites. To mitigate CRO-related risks, a secondary CRO could be identified to avoid project stagnation. Additionally, cytokine engineering or prodrug strategies may be employed to optimize activity and safety. Should the initial 3-5 mAb-cytokine combinations not yield effective constructs, the top candidates from Objective 2 will be reevaluated for fusion protein design. The most effective constructs will undergo further preclinical evaluation.
## Experimental Design and Methods
The experimental design will employ advanced single-cell technologies, including ZMAN-seq for cell temporal tracking, PIC-seq for cell-cell interaction analysis, and Stereo-seq for improved AI analysis of spatial transcriptomics. These technologies will be applied to both murine models of melanoma brain metastases (BMs) and patient-derived clinical samples to create comprehensive single-cell atlases of immune, stromal, and tumor cells within the BM TME. Orthotopic immunocompetent murine models, developed by the Weiss group, will be used in conjunction with patient clinical samples obtained in collaboration with hospitals in Israel. The experimental methodology will incorporate an in vitro functional screening system using bone-marrow-derived monocytes, with controls using TREM2-/- monocytes. This system will be used to co-culture myeloid and NK-T cells, followed by scRNA-seq analysis to assess the impact of selected cytokines in combination with our anti-TREM2 antibody. Additionally, an in vivo screening system will be utilized by overexpressing selected cytokines using barcoded tumor cell lines. These will be used to identify a subset of cytokines with the most promising modulatory function in combination with our anti-TREM2 antibody. ZMAN-seq will be used to track the transcriptomic dynamics of immune cells by introducing fluorescent antibody time stamps into circulating immune cells, enabling tracking of cell state transition trajectories. The data will be analyzed using advanced computational tools, including algorithms for cell lineage reconstruction, spatial data analysis, and AI-driven analysis to identify cellular trajectories, interactions, and gene expression patterns associated with effective anti-tumor responses. The data management plan will ensure that all data is securely stored, backed up and accessible to the research team, with appropriate measures to protect patient data and comply with data-sharing policies. Data will be processed using robust statistical methods including DESeq2 for RNA-seq data analysis and other established single-cell analysis tools including SCANPY, NicheNet and graph neural networks. Data will be made available in a timely manner in line with journal policies and will be publicly accessible at the completion of the project. This rigorous experimental design, coupled with our team's expertise, ensures the reproducibility and validity of the results and will facilitate the identification of novel immunocytokine constructs that can be translated into effective therapies for melanoma BMs.
## Statistical Plan
The statistical analysis will be performed using established biostatistical methods suitable for single-cell RNA-seq data, spatial transcriptomics, and temporal tracking data generated by ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq and Stereo-seq technologies. For differential gene expression analysis, we will employ DESeq2, and SCANPY which are well-validated tools for RNA-seq data. Sample size calculations will be based on power analysis to ensure statistical significance, taking into account the expected variability and effect sizes observed in single-cell data. We will be using a large cohort of patient clinical samples to create single-cell atlases of immune, stromal and tumor cells from human and murine BMs. For cell lineage reconstruction and trajectory analysis, we will use algorithms such as redPATH and CellRank, applying methods for continuous tumor exposure time (cTET) value assignments based on the cumulative distribution function (CDF), as detailed previously. Statistical tests, including t-tests, ANOVA, and non-parametric alternatives, will be applied as appropriate to the nature of the data. For spatial data analysis from Stereo-seq, we will employ graph neural network models to estimate cell-wise metabolic flux using single-cell RNA-seq data. We will use NicheNet to model intercellular communication by linking ligands to target genes and use MetaCell to analyze the single-cell data. For each method, we will apply multiple test correction methods. The data interpretation will be rigorous and data driven, with a focus on identifying statistically significant changes in cell state, interactions, and gene expression patterns associated with effective anti-tumor responses. Reproducibility will be ensured by clearly documenting all statistical methods and parameters used in the analysis, making the data and code available to the scientific community in line with journal policies.
