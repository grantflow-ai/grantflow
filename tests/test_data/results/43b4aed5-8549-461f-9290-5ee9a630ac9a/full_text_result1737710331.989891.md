## General Audience Abstract
Melanoma, a serious form of skin cancer, has seen a rise in cases, particularly in developed nations. While it constitutes a small percentage of all skin cancers, it accounts for a significant majority of skin cancer-related deaths. When detected early, melanoma is highly treatable, but the prognosis worsens considerably when it metastasizes, especially to the brain. Brain metastases (BMs) from melanoma are particularly devastating, occurring in almost half of patients with advanced melanoma, and lead to a drastically reduced life expectancy and quality of life. Immunotherapy, a revolutionary treatment, has improved survival rates for many melanoma patients; however, its effectiveness is limited by the immunosuppressive environment of BMs. This project aims to overcome these challenges by developing a novel therapeutic approach that targets the unique characteristics of the melanoma BM tumor microenvironment (TME). Our research will utilize cutting-edge single-cell technologies and AI to understand the complex interactions within the BM TME, specifically focusing on a unique subset of immunosuppressive macrophages, which we have identified as TREM2+ macrophages. These macrophages play a key role in suppressing the immune system, preventing the body from fighting the tumor. We have already developed an antibody that blocks the suppressive action of these cells. We propose to design novel immunocytokines that combine the suppressive blockade with immune-stimulating proteins (cytokines). These immunocytokines will be tailored to activate the immune system within the BM, turning the tumor microenvironment from "cold" to "hot", thus enabling the body to fight the cancer more effectively. Our approach involves several steps: first, we will create detailed models of the BM TME using advanced technologies to track cells over time and map their interactions. Second, using the knowledge gained from these models and AI, we will design effective immunocytokines, including an anti-TREM2 antibody and specific cytokines to activate the immune system. Third, we will use novel masking technologies to ensure these therapies target the BM specifically, thus limiting side effects and increasing their effectiveness. The expected impact of our research is substantial. By developing a novel therapy that can effectively treat melanoma brain metastases, we aim to extend the life expectancy and improve the quality of life for patients affected by this devastating condition. Furthermore, our work will provide crucial information about the mechanisms of immunosuppression in BMs, potentially leading to additional innovative treatments in other immunotherapy-resistant cancers. The success of this project will rely on the expertise of our multidisciplinary team, which consists of world leaders in immunology, brain tumors, and computational research, and the advanced infrastructure of our lab. The project's feasibility is backed by previous research that demonstrates the potential of our approach and our capacity to develop cutting-edge therapies. Our work not only advances basic scientific knowledge but also will have significant clinical and societal impact in the development of novel immunotherapeutic strategies for advanced melanoma patients. The combination of these approaches is expected to provide unprecedented control of melanoma metastases, offering a new perspective in the treatment of metastatic melanoma and other cancers characterized by immunosuppressive microenvironments. Our findings will be translated into clinical practice through the development of novel therapeutics for cancer, ensuring the rapid transfer of knowledge from the lab to the clinic. This project aligns with the need for more effective treatments for brain metastases and aims at the development of a first-in-class immunotherapeutic that has the potential to profoundly impact patient outcomes.
## Technical Abstract
This research project focuses on developing novel immunocytokine therapies for melanoma brain metastases (BMs), a significant clinical challenge due to their immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) and poor patient prognosis. Melanoma BMs occur in almost 50% of patients with metastatic melanoma and result in a dismal prognosis with limited overall survival. While immunotherapy has revolutionized melanoma treatment, many patients still face early relapse or do not respond to treatments, especially in BMs, where the brain milieu creates a highly immunosuppressive TME. Our team has identified a subtype of immune cell, regulatory (TREM2+) macrophages, that play a crucial role in suppressing anti-tumor immunity within the TME. We have developed an antibody that blocks the suppressive action of these cells. The overarching hypothesis is that by employing advanced single-cell technologies, including cell temporal tracking (ZMAN-seq, differentiation flows), cell-cell interactions (PIC-seq), and spatial transcriptomics with Stereo-seq coupled with AI analysis, we can gain an in-depth understanding of the BM TME. This will enable the identification of cytokines capable of remodeling the TME towards anti-tumor activity. We will use this knowledge to design novel immunocytokines that combine the most promising cytokines with our anti-TREM2 antibody to modulate both the myeloid and T and NK cell compartments. Furthermore, our hypothesis includes the identification of specific biomarkers through single-cell and AI analyses to design masking strategies, thereby increasing the specificity of the immunocytokine to the BM TME. We aim to develop first-in-class therapeutic modality simultaneously modulating different immune cell compartments (macrophages, T cells, NK cells). The central goal is to harness the power of single-cell technologies and AI to develop immunocytokines that are both potent and highly specific to the BM microenvironment, therefore overcoming immunosuppression and inducing effective anti-tumor responses.

Specific Aims of this project include:

**Aim 1: In-depth characterization of the melanoma BM TME using advanced single-cell technologies.** This will involve employing ZMAN-seq for temporal tracking of cell fate, PIC-seq to unravel cell-cell interactions, and Stereo-seq for spatial transcriptomic analysis. AI-driven analysis of these data will enable us to identify key cell types, molecular pathways, and spatial relationships contributing to immunosuppression in the BM TME. We will focus on defining the suppressive role of TREM2+ macrophages and their interaction with other cell types such as T cells, NK cells and other myeloid cells.

**Aim 2: Identification of cytokines that remodel the BM TME towards anti-tumor activity.** Based on the results from Aim 1, we will use our advanced computational approaches to analyze single cell data and identify cytokines with the highest potential to shift the immunosuppressive balance toward activation. We will prioritize cytokines that can activate T and NK cells, downregulate TREM2+ macrophages, and reduce myeloid derived suppressor cells (MDSC) activity. These cytokines will be chosen based on their ability to induce robust anti-tumor responses and their compatibility with immunocytokine format.

**Aim 3: Design and optimization of tumor-targeting immunocytokines.** We will incorporate potent cytokines identified in Aim 2 into antibody-cytokine fusion proteins (immunocytokines) targeting species-conserved tumor antigens highly expressed in melanoma BMs. These immunocytokines will include our anti-TREM2 antibody. We will employ melanoma BM murine models and our advanced technologies to optimize the immunocytokine design and identify the most effective constructs, moving towards the IND stage. We will also develop masking strategies to increase the specificity of the immunocytokine to the BM TME.

This approach represents a significant advancement in the field of melanoma brain metastases by simultaneously targeting multiple immune cell compartments within the tumor microenvironment and using masking technologies for enhanced specificity, which are expected to result in unprecedented anti-tumor responses. The translational potential of this project is substantial. The successful development of novel immunocytokines has the potential to significantly improve the outcomes for patients with melanoma BMs. By converting the highly immunosuppressive BM TME into an active anti-tumor environment, we aim to overcome the limitations of current therapies and significantly improve patient survival and quality of life. The identification of biomarkers to increase the specificity of immunocytokines is also a critical part of our translational strategy as it will potentially lower the potential for on-target off-tumor toxicities. This research is particularly timely given the high unmet need for effective therapies for melanoma BMs, and the increasing understanding of the critical role of the TME in tumor progression and treatment resistance. This study will contribute novel insights and practical therapeutic tools to address this important challenge in oncology.

# Project Description
## Background and Specific Aims
Melanoma, the most lethal form of skin cancer, frequently metastasizes to the brain, with brain metastases (BMs) occurring in approximately 50% of patients with metastatic disease. This grim statistic underscores the urgent need for effective therapeutic interventions, as melanoma BMs are associated with a poor prognosis and diminished overall survival. While recent advances in immunotherapy have revolutionized melanoma treatment, extending median survival from approximately 6 months to nearly 6 years in some advanced cases, many patients still experience early relapse or fail to respond to treatment. The unique milieu of the brain creates a highly immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME), which is a significant contributor to therapeutic resistance and disease progression. Single-cell technologies, coupled with machine learning, provide powerful tools for dissecting the complex interactions within the TME, which allows the development of data-driven designs of immunotherapies. Our research focuses on exploiting these tools to gain a deeper understanding of the immunosuppressive mechanisms within the melanoma BM TME and to develop novel, cytokine-based approaches that can transform this 'cold' environment into an immunologically 'hot' state, thereby enhancing anti-tumor immune responses.

Our preliminary research, leveraging advanced single-cell technologies, has identified a subset of immune cells within the TME, specifically regulatory (TREM2+) macrophages, that play a critical role in suppressing anti-tumor immunity, particularly in immunotherapy-resistant tumors such as melanoma BMs. We have successfully developed an antibody that effectively blocks the suppressive action of these TREM2+ macrophages. This discovery represents a novel therapeutic target and a crucial step toward overcoming immune resistance in the BM TME.

Given the limitations of current therapies and the urgent clinical need for more effective treatments for melanoma BMs, our research aims to develop a novel immunotherapeutic approach that addresses the immunosuppressive nature of the BM TME. Our central hypothesis is that through the use of advanced single-cell technologies, such as cell temporal tracking (ZMAN-seq, differentiation flows), cell-cell interaction mapping (PIC-seq), and improved AI-driven analysis of spatial transcriptomics (Stereo-seq), we can gain a comprehensive understanding of the BM TME and identify key cytokines capable of modulating the TME toward a more anti-tumorigenic state. This understanding will guide the design of novel immunocytokines that combine the most potent cytokines with our anti-TREM2 antibody. This combination will enable us to simultaneously modulate different immune cell compartments, including macrophages, T cells, and NK cells, which we hypothesize will induce unprecedented anti-tumor immune response. Furthermore, we hypothesize that our single-cell and AI-based analyses will allow us to identify specific biomarkers that will inform the development of masking strategies, thereby enhancing the specificity of our immunocytokines for the BM TME.

Our approach is based on the rationale that the advancements in single-cell technologies enable unprecedented insights into the immune landscape of BMs, paving the way for novel therapeutic strategies that activate the immune system to effectively target and eliminate these tumors. Simultaneously targeting multiple immune cell compartments (macrophages, T cells, NK cells) is a novel strategy that can potentially elicit a more potent and durable anti-tumor response compared to existing therapies. The proposed study directly addresses critical gaps in our current understanding of melanoma BM biology and will significantly advance our ability to effectively treat this devastating disease. Current therapeutic options for melanoma brain metastases are limited, often involving surgery, chemotherapy, and radiotherapy, which have limited efficacy, and novel immunotherapies are urgently needed. While immune checkpoint inhibitors have shown some promise, many patients do not respond or develop resistance. Our approach, using immunocytokines to simultaneously modulate different immune compartments offers a novel approach that is highly innovative and has the potential to overcome these limitations and deliver significant clinical improvements in melanoma brain metastasis treatment. Our approach also includes the use of masking techniques to increase the molecule's specificity to the BM TME and reduce the systemic toxicity.

Specifically, this project has three specific aims:

Aim 1: Develop comprehensive BM TME models with holistic, multimodal AI-driven analysis. We will use orthotopic immunocompetent murine models for melanoma BMs, along with human patient clinical samples, to create single-cell atlases of immune, stromal, and tumor cells from both murine and human BMs. We will use our novel technologies for cell temporal tracking (ZMAN-seq), cell-cell interactions (PIC-seq), and spatial transcriptomics (Stereo-seq), combined with AI analysis, to identify shared pathways and processes between our models and human patient TMEs. This will enable the identification of cytokines with high anti-tumor potential in melanoma BMs.

Aim 2: Characterize the immune dysfunction trajectories in melanoma BMs. Building upon the single-cell atlases developed in Aim 1, we will employ our advanced temporal analysis tools to define the differentiation flows of immune cells in the BM TME, and identify the key molecular events leading to immune suppression and therapeutic resistance. We will specifically focus on TREM2+ macrophages and their dynamic interactions with other immune cells. This will provide a detailed understanding of the dysfunctional immune trajectories and enable us to identify novel targets for therapeutic intervention.

Aim 3: Design tumor-targeting immunocytokines. We will incorporate potent cytokines that reactivate the immune compartment and eradicate melanoma BMs into antibody-cytokine fusion proteins (immunocytokines) that target tumor antigens highly expressed in melanoma BMs. Using melanoma BM murine models, and the technologies developed from Aims 1 and 2, we will use model-driven optimization to pinpoint the most effective constructs for the IND stage. This includes implementing our masking strategy for the cytokine arm using blocking moieties responsive only to TAM-specific proteases, therefore increasing the specificity of our immunocytokine to the BM TME.

## Research Plan

### Research Objectives

#### Objective 1: Developing BM TME models with holistic, multimodal AI-driven analysis

The overarching hypothesis for this objective is that a comprehensive, multi-modal analysis of the brain metastasis (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME) will reveal key cellular interactions, temporal dynamics, and spatial relationships that can be targeted to shift the balance from immunosuppression to anti-tumor immunity. This will be achieved through three interconnected tasks: temporal understanding of immune activity in the BM TME, immune cell-cell interaction analysis, and evaluation of immune spatial distribution within the TME. Each task is designed to contribute a unique facet to our holistic model of the BM TME.

The first task, focused on the temporal understanding of immune activity, will employ Zman-seq, a single-cell technology that tracks transcriptomic dynamics across time by introducing fluorescent antibody time stamps into immune cells. This method provides empirical time measurements for tissue exposure of immune cells relative to fluorophore pulse labels applied in circulation, allowing for reconstruction of immune cell state transitions within the TME. We will use this approach to resolve the trajectories of immune cell dysfunction, specifically characterizing the timeline and molecular mechanisms by which cytotoxic NK cells and monocytes transition to immunosuppressive states. This task will define the temporal dynamics of immune cell infiltration, differentiation, and functional changes in response to the TME over a period of 12, 24 and 36 hours, as well as beyond. The expected outcome of this task is a defined timeline of immune cell state transitions within the BM TME, including the identification of key genes and pathways that correlate with time-dependent changes in cell function. Specifically, we anticipate observing a rapid transition of NK cells to a dysfunctional state within 24 hours, alongside the differentiation of monocytes into immunosuppressive tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) over a 36-48 hour period, as previously observed in glioblastoma. This task relies on the successful application of the Zman-seq technology, which is already established in the lab and validated in the context of glioblastoma models.

The second task will focus on understanding immune cell-cell interactions in the BM TME, using PIC-seq. This technology enables the study of cell-cell interactions by identifying the ligands and receptors expressed by interacting cells. We will apply PIC-seq to the single-cell atlases generated, aiming to identify the specific cell types and interaction pathways that drive immunosuppression in the BM TME. This includes an in-depth analysis of the interplay between myeloid and NK-T cells, and the effect of perturbation with selected cytokines in combination with our E3C7 mAb. Expected results include the identification of novel interaction pairs driving the immunosuppressive network. This will reveal the key receptor-ligand interactions that are involved in the communication between the myeloid cells and NK/T cells, specifically looking for interactions that can explain the dysfunction in the NK cells. This task is contingent upon the availability of single-cell atlases from the first task and requires specialized equipment for PIC-seq experiments.

The final task will be dedicated to evaluating the immune spatial distribution in the BM TME, using Stereo-seq. This spatial transcriptomics approach allows us to map the location of different cell types and their gene expression profiles within the tissue. We will use Stereo-seq data to characterize the spatial organization of immune cells relative to tumor cells and stromal components. This will be used to understand if the spatial distribution affects the immune response and whether there are spatial relationships between immune cells at different functional states. This will reveal the spatial relationships that can be used to target specific spatial areas of the TME with immunomodulatory therapies. This task depends on the availability of BM models and the successful application of Stereo-seq which is an established method. The expected outcome of this task is the identification of key spatial niches in the TME where immune suppression is most pronounced, offering crucial insights for targeted therapeutic interventions. Taken together, this integrated, multi-modal approach will provide a comprehensive understanding of the BM TME, creating a foundation for targeted immunotherapy development.

##### Preliminary Results

Our preliminary data strongly support the feasibility of our proposed approach. We have previously demonstrated that an anti-TREM2 antibody can synergize with cytokines to induce pro-inflammatory macrophage activity in vitro, a key component of our proposed therapeutic strategy. This finding aligns with our objective to modulate myeloid cell function within the BM TME. Further, we have successfully produced and tested preliminary TREM2 immunocytokine model molecules, including an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein. This confirms our capacity to generate the therapeutic agents central to our proposed interventions. The development of a masking strategy for the cytokine arm using TAM-specific protease-responsive blocking moieties and its validation in vivo further supports the technical feasibility of our approach to reduce off-target effects. These preliminary results validate our ability to manipulate the TME using targeted immunocytokines.

Our preliminary work using Zman-seq in a glioblastoma model has provided critical insights that are directly translatable to the proposed study of melanoma brain metastases. Specifically, Zman-seq revealed the rapid transition of NK cells to a dysfunctional state within 24 hours of tumor infiltration, marked by the upregulation of TGF-β-related gene programs. This observation is consistent with our hypothesis that immune dysfunction is a time-dependent process that can be tracked using our technology. Furthermore, we observed that monocytes differentiate into immunosuppressive TAMs within 36-48 hours, and that this differentiation is driven by the upregulation of immunosuppressive myeloid checkpoints, including TREM2. Crucially, blocking TREM2 with an antagonistic antibody redirected the monocyte differentiation trajectory toward pro-inflammatory TAMs, highlighting the potential of our approach to reshape the TME. These findings show our proficiency in using Zman-seq to resolve temporal immune cell dynamics and indicate the translational relevance of our technology. Our experience with Zman-seq, coupled with the successful validation of our immunocytokine constructs and masking strategy, puts us in a strong position to address our research objectives in melanoma brain metastases. These previous findings are directly related to our first aim, which is to characterize the temporal dynamics of the immune system in the BM TME. We have also already established that Zman-seq is capable of dissecting the cellular trajectories in the TME, allowing us to investigate the temporal relationship of cell state transitions, like NK cell dysfunction and monocyte differentiation to TAMs, and we expect to see similar trends in the BM TME.

The data generated so far also highlights some critical gaps that our current project will need to address. While we have demonstrated TREM2 blockade as a method to redirect myeloid cell differentiation, the specific cytokines that will synergize most effectively in the context of brain metastases remain to be defined. Additionally, although we have used the Zman-seq platform in glioblastoma, it has not yet been applied to melanoma brain metastases. This will require adaptation of our methods to this specific tumor type. Furthermore, we will use PIC-seq to look for ligand-receptor interaction pairs that might be driving the myeloid-T and NK-T cell interactions, which were not part of the previous studies. Similarly, we need to apply Stereo-seq to characterize spatial cell-type heterogeneity in the brain metastases model. These points are crucial to move forward with our understanding of the TME, and will be directly addressed by our proposed objectives.

##### Risks and Alternatives

## Experimental Design and Methods
This section details the experimental design and methods employed to achieve the project's specific aims, focusing on rigor, reproducibility, and validation. **Aim 1: Development of Comprehensive BM TME Models with Multimodal AI-Driven Analysis**

To generate comprehensive models of the BM TME, we will employ orthotopic immunocompetent murine models of melanoma BMs, alongside human patient clinical samples. The Weiss group has established these murine models and has access to a large cohort of patient clinical samples, ensuring the feasibility of our research. Specifically, we will collect BM samples from mice at various stages of tumor development and from human patients undergoing resection or biopsy. These samples will be processed to generate single-cell suspensions for analysis using our advanced single-cell technologies. This will generate a comprehensive single cell atlas of murine and human BM TMEs.

For cell temporal tracking, we will use ZMAN-seq, a technology developed in our lab that introduces fluorescent antibody time stamps into immune cells in circulation, allowing us to track transcriptomic dynamics across time. This method will be used to determine the exposure time of infiltrating immune cells to the tumor microenvironment and to map their differentiation trajectories. Cells will be harvested at 12, 24, and 36 hours post labeling, and we will use a robust statistical method to assign continuous tumor exposure time (cTET) values, enabling us to define the dynamic changes in cell states across time. This approach will allow us to resolve the temporal trajectories of immune cell dysfunction within the BM TME. This technology allows empirical measurements of differentiation trajectories, which is crucial for developing precise and more efficacious immunotherapies.

To assess cell-cell interactions, we will use PIC-seq, which enables the mapping of physical interactions between cells at the single-cell level. This will allow us to identify the key cellular partners of TREM2+ macrophages and to define the molecular pathways mediating these interactions. Briefly, cells will be fixed, barcoded, and subjected to sequencing of the interacting cell regions. This will identify the interacting partners of TREM2+ macrophages and enable us to identify key molecular drivers of their immunosuppressive effects. To map spatial transcriptomics, we will use Stereo-seq, which allows large field-of-view spatial transcriptomics at cellular resolution. This technology will be applied to both murine and human BM samples, providing a spatially resolved view of gene expression patterns within the TME. We will use AI tools to analyze the spatial transcriptomic data to identify spatial relations between cell populations and the tumor margins. This will help identify specific niches within the TME that are enriched for immunosuppressive cells or cytokine production. This multimodal approach will enable us to build integrated tissue models from single cells and infer their physiology and potential drug response.

AI-driven analysis will be critical for integrating and interpreting the multi-omics data generated from ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq. We will employ machine learning algorithms to identify shared processes and pathways between our murine models and human patient TMEs. This approach will enable us to compile a list of cytokines with potentially high antitumor activity in melanoma BMs and identify key immunosuppressive pathways that are conserved between species. Specifically, the generated data will be used to train AI models that can identify cell states, cell-cell interactions, and spatial organization patterns that correlate with therapeutic response or resistance. These models will be used for selecting optimal cytokine combinations for immunocytokine design, and for identifying novel biomarkers for patient stratification and treatment monitoring. This analysis will be conducted in collaboration with the computer science team that has developed the relevant AI algorithms.

The masking moieties will be selected based on their affinity to known TAM-specific proteases and will be evaluated in vitro and in vivo. The fusion proteins and cleavage sites will be designed and tested in vitro to confirm protease cleavage, and then in vivo in the melanoma BM murine model to assess the biodistribution and efficacy of the masked and unmasked molecules.

For in vivo validation, we will use the same orthotopic immunocompetent murine models of melanoma BMs used in Aim 1. Mice will be treated with the optimized immunocytokine constructs, and tumor growth and survival will be monitored. We will use immunohistochemistry and live imaging techniques to assess the biodistribution and efficacy of the masked and unmasked constructs. We will also collect samples for single-cell analysis using the technologies described in Aims 1 and 2, to assess the impact of the immunocytokine treatment on the TME. This iterative approach will allow us to pinpoint the most effective constructs for the IND stage. Our group has extensive experience in protein design and generation using computational tools and recombinant protein expression, and has successfully developed preliminary TREM2 immunocytokines, which demonstrates our capacity to carry out this aim.
## Statistical Plan
The statistical analysis of the multi-omics data generated from ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq will be rigorous and comprehensive, employing advanced biostatistical methods to ensure the reproducibility and reliability of our findings. For each experiment, we will perform a power analysis to determine the necessary sample size to achieve statistical significance. The primary objective of our statistical plan is to rigorously analyze the complex, multi-dimensional data generated by our single-cell technologies to identify key drivers of immune dysfunction in melanoma BMs, and to validate the efficacy of our immunocytokine therapy. The single-cell data generated by ZMAN-seq will be analyzed using a statistical method developed for this technology that assigns continuous tumor exposure time (cTET) values, enabling us to define the dynamic changes in cell states across time. This method involves calculating a cumulative distribution function (CDF) for each metacell based on the frequency of cells in 12, 24, and 36-hour bins. The area under the curve (AUC) of each metacell-CDF will represent the cTET, allowing us to overlay the AUC profile on the metacell map to visualize cell state shifts across time. Statistical tests, such as ANOVA or Kruskal-Wallis tests, will be used to compare cTET values between different cell clusters and treatment groups. Genes significantly correlated with cTET will be identified, revealing time-dependent gene expression changes in response to tumor exposure. This approach allows us to resolve the temporal trajectories of immune cell dysfunction within the BM TME. PIC-seq data, which maps physical interactions between cells, will be analyzed using network analysis tools to identify key cellular interaction partners. Statistical comparisons of the frequency of cell-cell interactions will be performed, employing Chi-squared or Fisher's exact tests to identify statistically significant changes in interaction patterns across different experimental conditions. The molecular pathways mediating these interactions will be defined using pathway enrichment analysis, focusing on pathways enriched in interacting cell pairs. For spatial transcriptomics data from Stereo-seq, we will use AI tools and spatial statistics to identify spatial relations between cell populations and the tumor margins. We will use clustering algorithms to define distinct spatial niches within the TME. Spatial statistical methods will be used to identify cell populations enriched in specific spatial niches. Differences in spatial patterns of cell populations across treatment conditions and time points will be compared using spatial statistics such as Ripley's K function or spatial autocorrelation. Correlation analysis will be performed to identify gene expression patterns that correlate with spatial location within the tumor. For the analysis of single cell RNA-seq data, we will use established pipelines, including quality control, normalization, and batch effect removal. Data will be processed using the Scanpy framework, normalized, and log1p transformed. The top 2000 highly variable genes will be used for dimensionality reduction using methods such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP). For trajectory inference from scRNA-seq data, we will employ algorithms such as Monocle and Palantir. These computational trajectories will be integrated with the empirical temporal data from ZMAN-seq for a robust analysis of immune cell dynamics. We will use probabilistic modeling tools to resolve the effect of experimental covariates, such as treatment conditions, on the single cell profiles. This involves training a model using the raw counts of the most variable genes and computing a sample-infused latent representation to identify sample-specific gene expression patterns. For the in vivo experiments, we will use appropriate statistical tests such as t-tests or ANOVA to compare tumor growth, survival, and other relevant parameters between different treatment groups. Post-hoc tests, such as Tukey's HSD or Dunnett's test, will be applied when necessary. The sample size for each in vivo experiment will be determined based on power calculations to detect a statistically significant difference with a power of 80% at a significance level of 0.05. The statistical analysis of all the data will be conducted using R and Python programming languages, utilizing established biostatistical packages such as DESeq2, edgeR, and scikit-learn. The data will be stored in secure and well-organized databases, ensuring the long-term availability of all the data. All the code and statistical methods will be documented to ensure reproducibility and transparency. All results will be presented with appropriate measures of statistical uncertainty including standard error and confidence intervals, and p values will be corrected for multiple comparisons where necessary. The statistical analysis will be done in collaboration with our team's biostatisticians with experience in single-cell data analysis, ensuring the rigor and reproducibility of the analysis.
