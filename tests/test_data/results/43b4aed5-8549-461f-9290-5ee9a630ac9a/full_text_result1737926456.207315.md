# Developing AI tailored immunocytokines to target melanoma brain metastases

## Narrative

### Research Strategy

### Objective 1: Developing BM TME models with holistic, multimodal AI-driven analysis

Objective 1 is designed to comprehensively characterize the immunosuppressive bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME) in melanoma brain metastases using cutting-edge single-cell technologies and advanced AI-driven analysis. This objective directly addresses the identified knowledge gap regarding the mechanisms of immunosuppression within the BM TME, which is a significant barrier to effective immunotherapy. Our innovative methodology integrates three advanced single-cell techniques: ZMAN-seq for temporal tracking of immune cell states, PIC-seq for mapping cell-cell interactions, and Stereo-seq for spatial transcriptomics, creating a multi-dimensional understanding of the TME. ZMAN-seq will be instrumental in determining the temporal dynamics of immune cell infiltration and differentiation within the BM TME, specifically focusing on how immune cells transition from functional to dysfunctional states upon exposure to the tumor environment. This will be done by introducing fluorescent antibody time stamps into immune cells in circulation and tracking them in tissues hours to days later, allowing for the empirical measurements of differentiation trajectories. We will apply this technology to resolve the key immune trajectories in the tumor, including the progression of cytotoxic NK cells to states with low cytotoxic anti-tumor activity and the transition of monocytes to immunosuppressive tumor-associated macrophages. PIC-seq will be used to identify critical cell-cell interactions, allowing us to map the signaling networks within the BM TME and identify key receptor-ligand interactions that promote immunosuppression. This technology will allow us to identify the interactions between cells in the BM TME, focusing on the interplay between the melanoma cells and the immune and stromal cells. Spatial transcriptomics using Stereo-seq will enable us to spatially map the cellular landscape, revealing the spatial organization of immune and tumor cells, and identifying micro-niches with varying immunosuppressive properties within the BM TME. This will be instrumental in identifying the key signaling pathways that drive the immunosuppression of the BM TME, and how they vary across different spatial locations. The integration of data from these three single-cell technologies, including transcriptomic data, spatial information, and temporal dynamics, will be analyzed using AI-driven algorithms to identify key biomarkers of immunosuppression and potential therapeutic targets, in combination with machine learning algorithms. Our data analysis strategy will include the identification of key gene modules and transcription factor (TF) circuits that orchestrate the immunosuppressive pathways in the BM TME, using empirical measurements across time. This approach will move beyond static snapshots of the TME and provide a dynamic model of cellular processes. This approach will also identify specific temporal ligand-receptor interaction events driving tumor immune evasion. The findings from this objective will directly inform the design of subsequent objectives. Specifically, the cytokines and cellular interactions identified here will be used in Objective 2 to design novel cytokine screening strategies. In addition, the information from Objective 1, particularly the identified key immune cell populations and their interactions, will also guide the design of targeted immunocytokines in Objective 3. The experimental design will be continuously refined based on initial results and feedback from the findings in Objectives 2 and 3, in an iterative manner to ensure the most robust analysis and conclusive results. This integrated approach will generate a comprehensive understanding of the BM TME immune activity, moving the field towards rational and targeted immunotherapeutic strategies for melanoma brain metastases. This understanding will be instrumental in identifying the most promising cytokines for modulation of the immunosuppressive nature of the BM TME.

#### 1.1: Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME

Task 1.1 will employ Zman-seq to dissect the temporal dynamics of immune cell populations within the BM TME. This methodology will enable the tracking of immune cell states over time, revealing how these cells transition from functional to dysfunctional states upon exposure to the tumor environment. Specifically, we aim to identify the temporal trajectories of key immune cell types, including cytotoxic NK cells, monocytes, and T cells, and quantify the changes in their transcriptomic profiles as they infiltrate the BM TME. The experimental design will utilize fluorescent antibody time stamps to track the exposure time of immune cells to the BM TME, in combination with single-cell RNA sequencing. This will provide high-resolution temporal maps of the BM TME and allow for the empirical measurement of differentiation trajectories. Data analysis will focus on identifying time-dependent gene modules, transcription factor circuits, and key receptor-ligand interaction events driving immunosuppression. This approach will provide a dynamic model of cellular processes within the BM TME, moving beyond static snapshots.

#### 1.2: Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME

Task 1.2 will employ PIC-seq to delineate cell-cell interactions within the BM TME, building upon the temporal context established by Task 1.1. PIC-seq, a methodology combining physical cell interaction sorting with single-cell RNA sequencing, will be used to identify interacting cell pairs, and map receptor-ligand interactions. This approach will move beyond static single-cell analysis by providing a direct measure of physical interactions, thus revealing the signaling networks that characterize the BM TME. Our experimental design will focus on identifying key interactions between melanoma cells, immune cells (including NK cells, macrophages and T cells), and stromal cells, within specific temporal windows identified in Task 1.1. This will be critical for mapping the immunosuppressive signaling networks, specifically focusing on interactions driving the transition of immune cells to dysfunctional states. The data analysis will include computational modeling to dissect the impact of these interactions on gene expression. This will lead to an understanding of how cell-cell communications are involved in immunosuppression. The limitations of PIC-seq, such as the requirement for sufficiently distinct transcriptional profiles for deconvolution, will be addressed by rigorous experimental design and computational analysis.

#### 1.3: Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME

Task 1.3 will employ Stereo-seq, a spatial transcriptomics technology, to map the spatial distribution of immune cells within the BM TME. This methodology will enable the identification of spatial niches and cellular neighborhoods that may influence immune cell function and interactions. By leveraging the high resolution of Stereo-seq, we aim to identify specific spatial patterns of immune cell infiltration and organization, which are expected to correlate with varying degrees of immunosuppression. The spatial data obtained from Stereo-seq will be integrated with the temporal information from Task 1.1 and the cell-cell interaction data from Task 1.2. This integrated analysis will provide a comprehensive, multi-dimensional view of the BM TME. Specifically, the temporal analysis from Task 1.1 will inform the selection of time points for spatial analysis in Task 1.3. The interaction analysis from Task 1.2 will allow for the identification of spatial context of cell interactions, informing the interpretation of Stereo-seq results. This holistic approach will move beyond static snapshots of the TME, allowing us to correlate spatial location with cellular states and interactions within the BM TME, which is essential for understanding the dynamics of immune suppression. This task is a crucial component of Objective 1, as it will provide a spatial dimension to the temporal and interactional analyses, thus enabling a more complete understanding of the complex BM TME.

### Objective 2: Preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models

Objective 2 focuses on the preclinical screening of cytokines to modulate the immunosuppressive BM TME, building directly upon the comprehensive characterization of the BM TME achieved in Objective 1. Utilizing the in-house cytokine library, informed by the findings of Objective 1, particularly the identified cytokines and cellular interactions, we will screen for cytokines capable of shifting the BM TME from an immunosuppressive to an immunostimulatory state. This will be achieved through a systematic in-vitro and in-vivo screening methodology.

The in-vitro validation process will involve co-culturing melanoma cells with immune cells, including NK cells and T cells, in the presence of individual cytokines and cytokine combinations. These melanoma cells will be engineered to conditionally express a broad spectrum of candidate cytokines identified in Objective 1, which will be induced by the addition of doxycycline. The experimental design will focus on assessing the impact of these cytokines and their combinations on key immune cell functions, including cytokine production, cytotoxicity, and proliferation. The readouts will include single-cell RNA sequencing to determine the changes in gene expression in immune cells as a result of cytokine treatment. The in-vitro assays will be designed to measure the functional changes in immune cells, specifically focusing on the transition of dysfunctional immune cells to cytotoxic states and the shift from immunosuppressive to immunostimulatory phenotypes. These assays will utilize advanced techniques such as flow cytometry and confocal microscopy to measure key immune cell markers and cellular interactions.

Following the in-vitro validation, the most promising cytokines will be further evaluated in-vivo using immunocompetent BM models. This will involve the use of melanoma cells expressing the shortlisted cytokines or combinations, which will be introduced via intracardial injection to generate melanoma BMs. The expression of the cytokines will be activated by doxycycline, allowing for temporal control of cytokine exposure. Tumor response will be monitored using MRI to assess tumor size and burden. Survival measurements will also be taken to determine the impact of cytokine treatment on overall survival. The in-vivo experimental design will be focused on assessing the ability of selected cytokines, and combinations thereof, to modulate the BM TME, induce anti-tumor immune responses, and control tumor progression. Data analysis will focus on correlating in-vitro and in-vivo findings to select the most effective cytokines and cytokine combinations. The most promising cytokines identified in Objective 2, demonstrating a significant ability to modulate the BM TME and induce anti-tumor activity, will be incorporated into the design of targeted immunocytokines in Objective 3. The feedback from in-vivo experiments in Objective 2 will be used to refine the experimental design for both in-vitro and in-vivo experiments, optimizing for the most robust results.

#### 2.1: Screening of cytokines in BM TME

Task 2.1 will employ a high-throughput preclinical screening methodology using our in-house cytokine library to identify cytokines capable of modulating the BM TME. This task will involve screening a broad spectrum of cytokines, informed by the findings from Objective 1, to identify those that shift the BM TME from an immunosuppressive to an immunostimulatory state. The experimental design will involve co-culturing melanoma cells with immune cells, including NK cells and T cells, in the presence of individual cytokines and cytokine combinations. Initial readouts will include single-cell RNA sequencing to determine the changes in gene expression in immune cells, with a focus on markers of activation, cytotoxicity, and proliferation. The cytokines will be ranked based on their impact on the immune cell phenotype within the BM TME, measured by the readouts, to identify the most promising candidates for further in-vitro validation in Task 2.2 and in-vivo validation in Task 2.3. This screening is a crucial step towards identifying specific cytokines with the potential to re-program the BM TME. The data generated from this task will directly inform the experimental design of Tasks 2.2 and 2.3.

#### 2.2: In-vitro validation of cytokines

Task 2.2 will focus on the in-vitro validation of selected cytokines using co-culture models of melanoma cells with immune cells, specifically NK and T cells, mirroring the BM TME. This in-vitro validation will leverage the cytokine library established in Objective 1, and will prioritize cytokines identified as key modulators of the TME. The experimental design will involve co-culturing melanoma cells, engineered for inducible cytokine expression, with immune cells. The impact of individual cytokines and their combinations on immune cell function, such as cytokine production and cytotoxicity, will be assessed. Readouts will include single-cell RNA sequencing to analyze changes in immune cell gene expression upon cytokine treatment, providing a deep understanding of their effects. Functional changes in immune cells will be monitored using flow cytometry and confocal microscopy to analyze key immune cell markers and cellular interactions, focusing on the transition of dysfunctional to cytotoxic states. This rigorous in-vitro validation will provide a strong basis for the selection of cytokines for in-vivo studies in Task 2.3, directly informing the design of subsequent immunotherapy strategies.

#### 2.3: In-vivo validation of cytokines

Task 2.3 will focus on the in-vivo validation of selected cytokines using immunocompetent BM models. This task will assess the efficacy of individual cytokines and their combinations, identified from Task 2.1, in modulating the BM TME. Melanoma cells expressing candidate cytokines will be introduced into mice, and cytokine expression will be temporally controlled using doxycycline. The impact of these cytokines on myeloid, NK, and T cell activity within the BM TME will be evaluated using single-cell analysis. Specifically, we will use single-cell RNA sequencing to measure changes in gene expression in these immune cell subsets. We will quantify the infiltration and activity of cytotoxic NK cells, as well as the modulation of monocyte differentiation into immunosuppressive TAMs. These data will be analyzed to identify cytokines that effectively shift the BM TME towards an immunostimulatory state. The experimental design will be iterative, integrating findings from Task 2.2, using in-vitro assays to refine the in-vivo screening. This in-vivo validation will provide crucial data on the therapeutic potential of selected cytokines for melanoma BM immunotherapy and directly inform the immunocytokine design in Objective 3. Data analysis will focus on correlating in-vivo single cell analysis with tumor response measured by MRI.

### Objective 3: Design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines

Objective 3 is the translational step of this project, focusing on the design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines for melanoma BMs, which builds directly upon the findings of Objectives 1 and 2. This objective aims to translate the insights gained from the characterization of the BM TME and the screening of effective cytokines into a novel therapeutic modality. The design process will involve the engineering of antibody-cytokine fusion proteins, combining the anti-TREM2 antibody with the most promising cytokines identified in Objective 2. This targeted approach is designed to enhance therapeutic efficacy by directing cytokine activity specifically to the BM TME, thereby minimizing systemic toxicity and maximizing the local activation of anti-tumor immune responses. The immunocytokine design will leverage the information gained in Objective 1 to choose the optimal target in the BM TME. This will also include a masking strategy to increase the specificity of the immunocytokine to the BM TME, preventing its activation in healthy tissues, and relying on tumor-specific proteases for cleavage and activation within the BM TME. The design of the fusion proteins will take into account the optimal antibody-cytokine ratio and the linker characteristics to maximize the therapeutic efficacy of the immunocytokine. The in-vitro validation of the designed immunocytokines will involve a series of assays to assess their impact on myeloid and lymphoid cell activity. This will include co-culture models of melanoma cells with immune cells, specifically NK cells, T cells and macrophages, to measure changes in cytokine production, cytotoxicity, and proliferation. The assays will be designed to assess the ability of the fusion proteins to induce cytotoxic NK and T cell activity, and to modulate macrophage polarization. The in-vitro validation will also assess the impact of the masking strategy on the immunocytokine's specificity and activity. We will utilize single-cell RNA sequencing to determine the changes in gene expression in immune cells as a result of immunocytokine treatment. The experimental design will also incorporate AI-driven algorithms to optimize the design of the immunocytokines. Specifically, AI will be used to predict the optimal antibody-cytokine fusion, as well as to assess the impact of the masking strategy. The AI analysis will be used to optimize the design of the immunocytokine to achieve the highest therapeutic efficacy. This objective directly addresses the need for targeted therapy for melanoma BMs by combining the specificity of antibody-based therapeutics with the immunomodulatory effects of cytokines. The findings from Task 3.3 will provide feedback to Objectives 1 and 2 to refine the experimental design based on the results of immunocytokine testing, iteratively optimizing the therapeutic potential of these novel constructs.

#### 3.1: Design fusion proteins and cleavage site

Task 3.1 focuses on the design of antibody-cytokine fusion proteins, specifically for targeted therapy in melanoma BMs. This involves advanced protein design techniques, including AI-driven computational methods, to optimize the structure and stability of the immunocytokine. The design process will prioritize the selection of peptide linkers, blocking moieties, and TAM-specific cleavage sites to enhance targeted delivery and reduce off-target effects. The chosen antibody, anti-TREM2, will be fused with the most promising cytokines identified in Objective 2. This task will consider the optimal antibody-cytokine ratio and employ a masking strategy to enhance specificity and minimize systemic toxicity, relying on tumor-specific proteases for activation within the BM TME. This design will provide the basis for the production and testing in the following tasks and objectives.

#### 3.2: Produce fusion proteins

Task 3.2 will focus on the manufacturing of the antibody-cytokine fusion proteins, building on the designs from Task 3.1. This will involve contracting a CRO with proven expertise in protein production to ensure high-quality, timely delivery of the fusion proteins. The selection of the CRO will be based on our experience with leading vendors, focusing on their track record in producing stable molecules with high expression levels and minimal aggregation. The process will include rigorous quality control to confirm the integrity of the produced antibody-cytokine fusion proteins, and their suitability for downstream validation in Task 3.3. Potential issues during protein production, such as low expression or aggregation, will be addressed through iterative optimization of the production process in collaboration with the CRO. The timeline for protein synthesis and delivery to our lab will be carefully managed to ensure seamless integration with downstream validation experiments, facilitating the timely progression of the project.

#### 3.3: In-vitro validation of immunocytokines

Task 3.3 will focus on the in-vitro validation of the designed immunocytokines, employing surface plasmon resonance (SPR) to confirm binding kinetics, ELISA assays to quantify target engagement, and cell-based binding assays to assess specificity. Furthermore, reporter assays will evaluate the activation of downstream signaling pathways, while co-culture assays of huMDMs, NK, and T cells will validate the immunocytokines' impact on cellular interactions and the induction of cytotoxic activity. These assays will be instrumental in assessing the efficacy of the fusion proteins in inducing cytotoxic NK and T cell activity, and in modulating myeloid cell activity. The data generated will provide critical insights into the optimal design of the immunocytokines for targeted therapy and immunotherapy of melanoma brain metastases. The methodology will specifically assess the immunocytokines' ability to enhance anti-tumor activity through modulation of both myeloid and lymphoid cell function. The results from these in-vitro validations will inform iterative design changes to the immunocytokines, optimizing their efficacy and specificity in the BM TME.

#### Preliminary Results

Our preliminary data demonstrates the synergistic potential of combining an anti-TREM2 antibody with cytokines, which resulted in a strong pro-inflammatory macrophage activity in vitro. This finding supports our hypothesis that modulating the tumor microenvironment (TME) with targeted immunotherapies can induce a potent anti-tumor response. Furthermore, we have successfully tested the feasibility of producing TREM2 immunocytokines and have developed preliminary model molecules, including an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein. The successful generation of these fusion proteins establishes the technical feasibility of our approach and demonstrates our ability to produce functional immunocytokines. These preliminary molecules will be used as a starting point to identify the most promising cytokines for optimal activity. We have also developed and tested a masking strategy for the cytokine arm using blocking moieties that are responsive only to tumor-associated macrophage (TAM)-specific proteases. This strategy is designed to enhance the specificity of our immunocytokines to the BM TME, which is crucial for minimizing off-target effects and maximizing therapeutic efficacy. This in-vivo validation of the masking approach is key to demonstrating the feasibility of targeted delivery and activation of our immunocytokines. The preliminary data strongly supports the feasibility of our research plan. The combined analysis of the TME using single-cell technologies, the identification of novel cytokines with anti-tumor activity, and the development of targeted immunocytokines using masking strategies, is technically feasible. Our existing models of melanoma brain metastases, including expertise in antibody design and single cell sequencing will ensure our goals are achievable within the scope of the project. The preliminary data provide a strong foundation for the proposed research and highlight our capacity to achieve the project's objectives. We are confident that our approach will yield significant insights into the immunobiology of melanoma brain metastases, and pave the way for the development of effective novel immunotherapies.### Risks and Mitigations

Several potential risks are associated with the proposed research. A primary risk is the inherent complexity of single-cell technologies, particularly ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq, which may yield data with high variability or technical artifacts that could confound the analysis of the BM TME. To mitigate this, we will implement rigorous quality control measures at each step, including optimized experimental protocols, and validation of the results by orthogonal technologies where possible. Additionally, we will establish strict criteria for data filtering and employ advanced computational methods to reduce technical noise and batch effects. Another significant risk involves the potential for challenges in data integration from the different single-cell technologies. The temporal, spatial, and interactional data need to be combined using robust algorithms. To address this, we will adopt a well-defined data management and integration strategy, using AI-driven tools to ensure seamless data integration. The computational modeling also poses a risk of the inability to generate interpretable models that correctly reflect the biological reality. To mitigate this risk, we will use multiple computational tools to generate models and test them for consistency using existing, publicly available datasets. Furthermore, there is a risk that the in-vitro and in-vivo screening of cytokines in Objective 2 may not yield cytokines that effectively shift the BM TME from immunosuppressive to immunostimulatory, impacting the progression of the project. To mitigate this, we will perform a comprehensive screen of a large cytokine library, using the data from Objective 1 to focus on promising candidates, and using multiple readouts to select the most promising candidates. We will also use an iterative design, to continuously refine the selection of cytokines based on in-vitro and in-vivo results. There is also a risk that the tumor-targeting immunocytokines designed in Objective 3 may not effectively target the BM TME or may exhibit unexpected toxicity. To mitigate this, we will use AI-driven approaches to optimize the design of the immunocytokines and employ a masking strategy to enhance their specificity. We will also conduct extensive in-vitro validation assays, using human cells, to test both the therapeutic potential and toxicity profile of the immunocytokines. Contingency plans include an iterative experimental design, using data from each task and objective to guide the next steps, and the use of several methodologies to ensure robustness and reproducibility.### Potential Impact

The potential clinical impact of this research is substantial, addressing a critical unmet need in melanoma treatment. Melanoma brain metastases (BMs) are a devastating complication, occurring in approximately 50% of patients with metastatic melanoma and resulting in poor overall survival. Current treatments, including chemotherapies and targeted therapies, have limitations, and the immunosuppressive nature of the BM tumor microenvironment (TME) often hinders the effectiveness of immunotherapies. This project aims to overcome these barriers by developing a novel immunotherapeutic approach that specifically targets the BM TME and modulates its immunosuppressive properties. By employing advanced single-cell technologies and AI-driven analysis, we will gain an in-depth understanding of the complex cellular interactions within the BM TME, identify key cytokines capable of shifting the TME from an immunosuppressive to an immunostimulatory state, and design targeted immunocytokines that combine the specificity of an anti-TREM2 antibody with the immunomodulatory effects of these selected cytokines. The successful translation of this research could lead to a significant improvement in patient outcomes, including increased survival rates and improved quality of life. The project's focus on early detection and personalized treatment approaches, coupled with the development of a novel immunotherapeutic strategy, has the potential to revolutionize the management of melanoma BMs. The development of a targeted immunocytokine, employing masking strategies to enhance specificity, is an innovative approach that could minimize systemic toxicity and maximize the local activation of anti-tumor immune responses in the brain. This research will contribute to the understanding of the mechanisms that underlie the immunosuppressive TME in the brain and provide new strategies for the development of effective therapies for melanoma BMs. This translational research is expected to directly translate into novel therapeutic options for patients with melanoma BMs.

## Abstract

This research project aims to develop novel immunotherapies for melanoma brain metastases (BMs) by leveraging advanced single-cell technologies and artificial intelligence (AI). BMs are a major cause of mortality in melanoma patients, with limited effective treatment options. Our approach will combine detailed characterization of the immunosuppressive bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME) with the design of targeted immunocytokines. The first objective involves a comprehensive, multi-dimensional analysis of the BM TME using ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq technologies, coupled with AI-driven data integration. This will provide a dynamic model of cellular processes, identifying key biomarkers and therapeutic targets. The second objective focuses on preclinical screening of cytokines using in-vitro and in-vivo immunocompetent BM models to shift the TME from an immunosuppressive to immunostimulatory state. This screening will use an in-house cytokine library informed by the findings of Objective 1 and will identify the most promising cytokines for downstream applications. The third objective is the design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines, based on the identified cytokines and using an anti-TREM2 antibody. The immunocytokines will also incorporate a masking strategy to enhance specificity, with activation dependent on tumor-specific proteases. The project will utilize AI-driven design algorithms to optimize the immunocytokine constructs, ensuring maximal therapeutic potential with minimal toxicity. The successful development of these novel targeted immunotherapies could significantly improve the prognosis of melanoma patients with BMs, addressing the crucial need for effective treatments in this patient population and impacting patient survival and quality of life.
