# Project Summary
## General Audience Abstract
This research project focuses on improving treatment for melanoma that has spread to the brain, a condition with very poor outcomes. We will use advanced technologies to study the environment around the tumor at a single cell level, identifying a specific type of immune cell that prevents the body from fighting the cancer effectively. We will then design a new therapy that combines a blocking antibody with molecules that stimulate the immune system, aiming to convert the tumor environment from suppressive to active in fighting the cancer. The impact of this research could significantly improve the life expectancy and quality of life for patients with metastatic melanoma by developing new and more effective therapies.
## Technical Abstract
Brain metastases (BMs) occur in almost 50% of patients with metastatic melanoma, leading to poor overall survival due to a highly immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). Single-cell technologies combined with machine learning are powerful tools to decipher complex interactions within the TME, enabling data-driven immunotherapy designs. Our research focuses on regulatory (TREM2+) macrophages, a key immunosuppressive cell type within the TME, particularly in immunotherapy-resistant melanomas. We hypothesize that advanced single-cell technologies, including ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq, combined with AI analysis, will provide an in-depth understanding of the BM TME and identify cytokines capable of remodeling it towards anti-tumor activity. This understanding will enable the design of novel immunocytokines combining promising cytokines with our anti-TREM2 antibody to modulate both myeloid and T/NK cell compartments. This approach will also identify biomarkers to design masking strategies to increase the specificity of the immunocytokine to the BM TME, enhancing clinical impact through targeted therapies. The translational potential of this research lies in the development of novel immunocytokines with improved specificity and efficacy against melanoma BMs, potentially increasing life expectancy and quality of life for metastatic melanoma patients.

# Project Description
## Background and Specific Aims
Brain metastases (BMs) occur in approximately 50% of patients with metastatic melanoma, leading to poor overall survival. Immunotherapy has significantly improved outcomes for melanoma patients, yet many still experience relapse or lack response, particularly in BMs where the brain creates an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). Single-cell technologies and machine learning provide powerful tools to analyze cell interactions within the TME, enabling the design of data-driven immunotherapies. Our research has identified regulatory (TREM2+) macrophages as a key immunosuppressive component of the TME, particularly in melanoma. We have developed an antibody to block the suppressive action of these cells. Our hypothesis is that advanced single-cell technologies, including cell temporal tracking (ZMAN-seq, differentiation flows), cell-cell interaction analysis (PIC-seq), and spatial transcriptomics with Stereo-seq, will provide an in-depth understanding of the BM TME. This analysis will identify cytokines capable of remodeling the TME towards anti-tumor activity. This knowledge will allow the design of novel immunocytokines, combining promising cytokines with our anti-TREM2 antibody, to modulate myeloid, T, and NK cell compartments. We further hypothesize that single-cell and AI analysis will identify biomarkers to design masking strategies, enhancing the specificity of immunocytokines to the BM TME. Our rationale is that advanced single-cell technologies can provide an in-depth understanding of the immune environment of BMs, facilitating the identification of novel strategies to activate the immune system and eliminate BMs. Simultaneously modulating multiple immune cell compartments (macrophages, T cells, NK cells) can induce an unprecedented anti-tumor immune response.
## Preliminary Data
Our preliminary data demonstrates the feasibility of our approach, showcasing the synergistic potential of an anti-TREM2 antibody combined with cytokines. In vitro studies revealed the induction of strong pro-inflammatory macrophage activity using this combination. We have successfully produced TREM2 immunocytokines, developing preliminary model molecules such as an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein. Furthermore, we have designed and tested a masking strategy for the cytokine arm, utilizing blocking moieties responsive to TAM-specific proteases in vivo. These findings provide robust supporting evidence for the feasibility of our research plan and set a strong foundation for the proposed studies.
## Research Plan

### Research Objectives

#### Objective 1: Developing BM TME models with holistic, multimodal AI-driven analysis

This research objective aims to develop a comprehensive understanding of the brain metastases (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME) using advanced single-cell technologies, specifically focusing on immune cell activity. The central hypothesis is that by employing temporal, interactional, and spatial analyses of the BM TME, we can identify novel targets for therapeutic intervention, shifting the immunosuppressive environment towards anti-tumor immunity. The goal is to generate a detailed map of the immune landscape within melanoma BMs, uncovering dynamic processes and key regulatory molecules. To accomplish this, three primary tasks are proposed. Task 1, "Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME", will leverage Zman-seq to track transcriptomic changes in immune cells over time within the BM TME, determining the trajectories of infiltrating immune cells and identifying early markers of dysfunction. This will involve the use of fluorescent antibody time stamps and single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) to map continuous tumor exposure time (cTET) and define differentiation pathways. Task 2, "Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME", will utilize PIC-seq to characterize cell-cell interactions within the BM TME. By mapping ligand-receptor interactions, we will identify key signaling pathways driving immune suppression, which will then be targeted. Task 3, "Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME", will employ Stereo-seq to obtain high-resolution spatial transcriptomics data, revealing the spatial organization of immune cells and their relationship to other components of the TME. This will provide insights into the heterogeneity of the tumor and enable a more targeted therapeutic approach. These data will be integrated to form a holistic, multimodal understanding of the BM TME. Expected results include the identification of key cytokines and checkpoints, such as TREM2, that modulate immune activity, which will be further explored in subsequent objectives. This objective provides foundational data on the BM TME, which informs the design of experiments and targets in Objectives 2 and 3. These tasks are interdependent, with results from the temporal analysis informing cell-cell interaction and spatial analyses, all providing a combined view of immune cell state transitions, interaction and location.

##### Preliminary Results

Our preliminary results demonstrate the feasibility and potential of our approach for characterizing the brain metastases TME. In prior work, we have shown that targeting TREM2 with an antagonistic antibody can shift the balance of macrophage polarization from immunosuppressive to pro-inflammatory in vitro. Specifically, we observed that anti-TREM2 treatment, in combination with cytokines, induces robust pro-inflammatory macrophage activity. Further, we have developed and tested preliminary TREM2 immunocytokine model molecules, including an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein, which enhances the specificity and potency of our approach. We also created a protease-responsive masking strategy for the cytokine arm which is activated only in the presence of TAM-specific proteases, and have demonstrated the feasibility of this approach in vivo.

These findings lay the groundwork for the proposed temporal, interactional, and spatial analyses of the BM TME. Our prior work using Zman-seq in a glioblastoma model showed that this technology enables the mapping of immune cell state transitions by measuring the exposure time of infiltrating immune cells to the tumor. For example, in the GBM model, we observed rapid transitions of NK cells to a dysfunctional state within 24 hours, characterized by the upregulation of TGF-β-responsive genes. We also identified key receptor-ligand interactions, such as TGF-β1 and CCL12, that drive NK cell dysfunction. We further showed that monocytes differentiate into immunosuppressive TAMs over 36-48 hours, with a concomitant increase in the expression of TREM2. Importantly, using Zman-seq, we demonstrated that blocking TREM2 with an antagonistic antibody redirected the monocyte differentiation pathway towards pro-inflammatory TAMs. We have also developed and tested in vitro and in vivo models, and single cell analysis systems, which will allow us to assess the effects of selected cytokines in combination with anti-TREM2 mAbs in the TME. These preliminary findings strongly support the feasibility of our proposed approach to develop a detailed map of the immune landscape within melanoma BMs, as we will employ Zman-seq to track transcriptomic changes over time, identify key cell-cell interactions via PIC-seq, and use Stereo-seq to map the spatial distribution of immune cells. Together, these tools will enable the identification of novel therapeutic targets and strategies to modulate the BM TME.

##### Research Tasks

###### Task 1.1: Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME

This research task will investigate the temporal dynamics of immune cell activity within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME). Utilizing Zman-seq, a single-cell technology that tracks transcriptomic changes over time, we will analyze how immune cell states transition following their infiltration into the BM TME. This approach builds upon previous work adapting Zman-seq from glioma models. The methodology involves introducing fluorescent antibody time stamps into circulating immune cells, tracking their subsequent migration and exposure time within the BM TME, and performing single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) to profile gene expression. Fluorescently labeled leukocytes will be sorted using fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS), and the fluorescent stamps on individual cells will be captured using index-sorting to determine the time of BM TME infiltration. This will generate a temporal map of the BM TME, allowing for the reconstruction of immune cell state progression across time. Data analysis will incorporate both tumor exposure time measurements with single-cell transcriptomic profiling, defining cell trajectories and differentiation pathways. We will employ a robust statistical method to assign continuous tumor exposure time (cTET) values at the metacell level using cumulative distribution functions based on the frequency of cells in different time bins. The area under the curve (AUC) for each metacell-CDF will represent the cTET, and this profile will be overlaid on the metacell map. This will allow us to define the trajectory underlying the observed cell state transitions, similar to the approach used in the previous glioma study. Temporal ligand-receptor interactions and transcription factor (TF) circuits that drive immune cell dysfunction will also be examined. This analysis will reveal time-dependent gene modules and receptor-ligand interaction events influencing immune responses in the BM TME. This methodology will provide data to construct dynamic models of the BM TME, which are crucial for developing next-generation immunotherapies.

###### Task 1.2: Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME

This research task will employ PIC-seq to investigate immune cell-cell interactions within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME). PIC-seq, which combines cell sorting of physically interacting cells with single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) and computational modeling, will be used to profile cellular interactions and their impact on gene expression. This approach enables the study of cell-cell interactions in vivo, overcoming the limitations of traditional scRNA-seq that requires single-cell suspensions. The methodology involves sorting physically interacting cells using surface markers, followed by massively parallel scRNA-seq to generate gene expression profiles. These profiles will then be computationally deconvolved to infer the transcriptional states of interacting cells versus non-interacting single cells. This task will leverage data from Task 1.1, which provides information on temporal immune activity, to analyze cell-cell interactions. Specifically, the temporal dynamics of immune cell infiltration and activation, as identified by Zman-seq in Task 1.1, will inform the selection of time points for PIC-seq analysis, ensuring that cell-cell interactions are studied at relevant stages of TME development. This integrated approach will allow for a dynamic understanding of how cell-cell interactions evolve in the BM TME, potentially identifying critical regulatory interactions, such as co-stimulatory genes induced in antigen-presenting PICs. The transcriptional profiles of interacting cells will need to be sufficiently 'distant' for accurate deconvolution by the PIC-seq algorithm. Additionally, this analysis will require advanced computational tools for deconvolution of the PIC-seq data and integration with the temporal data from task 1.1. The success of this task is dependent upon the completion of task 1.1.

###### Task 1.3: Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME

This research task will employ Stereo-seq to investigate the spatial distribution of immune cells within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME). Stereo-seq, a spatial transcriptomics technology, will be utilized to generate high-resolution maps of immune cell populations, allowing for the examination of cellular heterogeneity and spatial relationships. Specifically, we will analyze the distribution of various immune cell types, including NK cells, monocytes, and macrophages, within the BM TME. This analysis will be performed on murine BM TME models, leveraging the models developed in Task 1. The Stereo-seq data will be analyzed using spatially constrained-clustering (SCC) algorithms, which are optimized for large and complex tissues, to identify distinct tissue domains and cellular niches. These domains will be annotated based on gene expression patterns and known cell markers. The distribution of specific markers will be compared to in situ hybridization data to validate Stereo-seq results. Furthermore, the lateral diffusion of RNA will be assessed to determine the subcellular resolution of the data, analyzing markers such as nuclear-localized long non-coding RNAs and cytoplasmic-enriched mitochondrial transcripts. This task is dependent on the completion of task 1.1, as it will use data on temporal immune activity to analyze spatial distribution. Data generated from task 1.1, specifically data from Zman-seq experiments will be used to correlate temporal dynamics of immune cells with their spatial distribution using Stereo-seq. The spatial data will also be integrated with the single-cell temporal data to track transcriptional trajectories between states. The spatial coordinates will be used in conjunction with gene expression data to analyze cell-cell interactions in the BM TME. This integrated analysis will provide a comprehensive view of the immune landscape in the BM TME and how it evolves temporally, allowing for a better understanding of immune-tumor interactions.

##### Risks and Alternatives

A primary risk is the potential for technical challenges in the implementation of Zman-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq, as these are relatively novel techniques. Specifically, Zman-seq relies on precise temporal labeling with fluorescent antibodies, and signal fading or antibody shedding could compromise the accuracy of tumor exposure time measurements. Mitigation will involve rigorous optimization of antibody concentrations and staining protocols and validation through comparison with computational trajectory analyses. PIC-seq's ability to deconvolve interacting cells depends on transcriptomic dissimilarity; if the profiles of interacting cells are too similar, it could be challenging to identify specific cell-cell interactions. This risk will be mitigated by using high-resolution data analysis pipelines and by combining PIC-seq with spatial transcriptomics from Stereo-seq to validate cell-cell contact. Stereo-seq, while offering high spatial resolution, has limitations in capturing low expression genes or in segmenting closely juxtaposed cell types, particularly immune cells. This limitation will be addressed by employing image-based cell segmentation techniques and validating the spatial organization using immunohistochemistry. An alternative approach includes using larger Stereo-seq chips to ensure sufficient cell numbers for statistical power. Furthermore, the interdependence of the tasks means that delays in one task could cascade to others. To mitigate this, we will prioritize Task 1 (Temporal analysis) as a foundation for subsequent tasks.

#### Objective 2: Preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models

This objective focuses on the preclinical screening of cytokines within orthotopic immunocompetent brain metastasis (BM) models to identify therapeutic candidates that can modulate the tumor microenvironment (TME) towards anti-tumor activity. The central hypothesis is that specific cytokines, identified through single-cell analysis of the BM TME, can enhance immune cell infiltration and activation, thereby leading to tumor regression. This objective builds upon the findings of Objective 1, which aims to establish a comprehensive understanding of the BM TME, to screen for therapeutic cytokines.

The screening will initially involve in vitro assessments of a panel of approximately 30 cytokines, including CCL3, CSCL7, CCL26, TGFBRII, IL-1, IL-2, IL-6, IL-7, IL-12, IL-15, IL-18, IL-21, IFNa, IFNb, IFNg, CCL1, CCL2, CCL3, CCL5, CCL7, CCL8/MCP-2, CCL11, CCL13/MCP-4, CCL14/HCC-1, CCL17/CTAC, CCL19, CCL22, CCL23, CCL24, CCL26, CCL27, lymphotactin/XCL1, Eotaxin, CXCL6/GCP-2, CXCL7/NAP-2, CXCL8, CXCL10, CXCL11/ITAC, CXCL12, CXCL13, CXCL15, in combination with an anti-TREM2 mAb (E3C7). This will be achieved through co-culture assays of human monocyte-derived macrophages (huMDMs), natural killer (NK) cells, or T cells with tumor cells. The impact of these cytokine combinations on immune cell activity will be evaluated using flow cytometry to assess proliferation, and ELISA and qPCR to measure cytokine secretion. Cytotoxic activity will be assessed by quantifying tumor cell death using FACS, with tumor cells pre-stained for identification.

The 8-10 most promising cytokine combinations identified in vitro will be further evaluated in vivo. Brain tropic melanoma cell lines will be transduced to conditionally express (doxycycline-induced) the selected cytokines. These engineered cell lines will be used to generate melanoma BMs via intracardial injections in immunocompetent mice. Cytokine expression will be activated by doxycycline administration, and tumor response will be monitored via MRI and survival measurements. Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) will be used to gain an in-depth understanding of the effects of the cytokine perturbation and identify the 3-5 most promising cytokines with modulatory functions in combination with the anti-TREM2 mAb. This approach will generate a panel of cytokines to move forward into the immunocytokine design.

##### Research Tasks

###### Task 2.1: Screening of cytokines in BM TME

This research task focuses on screening cytokines within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME) to identify those that modulate immune activity. The methodology involves utilizing an in-house cytokine library to perform a comprehensive screen. The initial phase will involve in-vitro assays of co-cultured human monocyte-derived macrophages (huMDMs), natural killer (NK) cells, and T cells. These co-cultures will be treated with individual cytokines from the library, as well as in combination with an E3C7 mAb, a TREM2 monoclonal antibody. Control assays, omitting either the cytokines or the antibody, will be performed for baseline comparison. Assessment of immune cell activity will involve quantification of proliferative potential using flow cytometry, and cytokine secretion via ELISA and qPCR. Additionally, cytotoxic T and NK cell activity will be assessed through killing assays, with tumor cell death quantified by FACS after pre-staining of tumor cells. Data from these assays will be combined to select the 8-10 cytokine and anti-TREM2 mAb combinations that exhibit the most promising performance in terms of downregulating myeloid cell suppressive activity and enhancing myeloid and T cell inflammatory activity. Further in-depth analysis will be performed using a single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) approach. An in-vitro system for bone-marrow-derived monocyte differentiation towards the M2 suppressor macrophage state is already established. This system will be expanded to co-culture myeloid and NK-T cells and will be treated with selected cytokine combinations. scRNA-seq will be used to understand the effects of the perturbations created by the different cytokine treatments, comparing the effects with those of co-cultured T cells and TREM2-/- myeloid cells. The results of this task will contribute to a data-driven selection of 3-5 cytokines with the most promising modulatory functions in combination with anti-TREM2 mAb for further in vivo studies.

###### Task 2.2: In-vitro validation of cytokines

Task 2 will focus on the in-vitro validation of cytokines identified in Task 1. This will involve using in-vitro models, specifically tumor spheroids created from brain tropic melanoma cell lines transduced to conditionally express a range of cytokines. These clones will contain one or multiple cytokine cassettes. Co-culturing with immune cells, these spheroids will be used to test varying combinations of cytokines, with cytokine expression induced by doxycycline. The in-vitro assays will assess the impact of these cytokines on myeloid, NK, and T cell activity and cytotoxicity levels, measured by quantifying tumor cell death using FACS. Additionally, cytokine secretion will be measured using ELISA and qPCRs, and the proliferative potential of NK and T cells will be assessed via flow cytometry. These assays will use huMDMs, NK, and T cells co-cultured with tumor cells, with controls including assays without the anti-TREM2 mAb or without cytokines. The data collected will be analyzed to determine the most effective cytokines and cytokine combinations. The in-vitro findings from Task 2.1 will be validated in Task 2.2 using these in-vitro models. This involves creating cytokine-secreting clones of brain tropic melanoma cell lines by transducing them to express promising BM immune re-activation candidate cytokines. These clones will be used to generate tumor spheroids containing one or multiple cytokine cassettes for co-culture experiments with immune cells. Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) will be used for in-depth analysis of the effects of cytokine perturbations. Furthermore, the binding of the cytokines will be confirmed via SPR, ELISA, cell-based binding and reporter assays, in task 2.3.

###### Task 2.3: In-vivo validation of cytokines

Task 3 focuses on in-vivo validation of cytokine candidates identified through in-vitro screening. This task will employ single-cell analysis using both in-vitro and in-vivo functional screening systems to assess the impact of trans-acting MiTEs on myeloid, NK, and T cell activity. The in-vivo system will utilize a platform to over-express cytokines inducibly via barcoded tumor cell lines. Specifically, 5-8 cytokines selected from Task 1.4 will be cloned into vectors and screened in combination with anti-TREM2 mAbs in vivo. Single cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) will be used to analyze the modulatory function of 3-5 cytokines in combination with anti-TREM2 mAbs. The in-vitro system developed for bone-marrow-derived monocyte diversification toward the M2 suppressor macrophage state will be used in co-culture with myeloid and NK-T cells and apply scRNA-seq analysis. Data will be collected and analyzed in comparison to control cultures, including monocytes derived from TREM2-/- mice, to understand the effects of cytokine perturbation. Additionally, this task leverages results from Task 2.2 for cytokine candidate selection, and therefore dependent on task 2.2. In addition to single cell sequencing data, flow cytometry, ELISA and qPCRs will be utilized to quantify cell proliferation and cytokine secretion as a measure of NK and T cell activity. The data generated will be used to validate the efficacy of mAb-cytokine chimeras in inducing cytotoxic NK and T cell activity using co-cultured huMDMs, NK, or T cells and tumor cells. This task builds on the work done in Tasks 1.1, 1.2, 1.4 and 1.5, that screened for the most effective cytokines and has a dependency of data generated from Task 2.2.

##### Risks and Alternatives

A primary risk involves the potential for limited efficacy of selected cytokines in modulating the tumor microenvironment (TME) as hypothesized. This could stem from the complexity of the TME, where multiple factors beyond single cytokines influence immune cell activity, or from the cytokines not having the desired effect in the in-vivo model, where the TME is more complex. Additionally, the conditional expression of cytokines in transduced cell lines may not reach therapeutic levels, limiting the impact on tumor regression. To mitigate these risks, we will initially screen a broad panel of 30 cytokines with a focus on combinatorial effects. We will also optimize the doxycycline-induction system to ensure sufficient cytokine expression in vivo. An alternative approach involves the use of constitutively expressing cytokine clones as a backup strategy. Furthermore, we will use single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) for data-driven selections of the 3-5 most promising cytokines which are likely to work synergistically with the anti-TREM2 mAb. Another risk is the potential for off-target effects or systemic toxicity from the cytokine treatment in vivo. To address this, the cytokines selected for in vivo validation have been chosen for their demonstrated potential to modulate immune activity with limited systemic effects. We will also start with a dose-escalation study to identify the maximum tolerable dose in the murine model, to balance efficacy and safety. If the selected cytokines prove to have significant systemic effects, an alternative approach is to engineer cytokines with reduced receptor affinity or creating conditionally activated immunocytokine prodrugs to minimize off-target effects. We will also validate the tumor specificity of cytokine effects, utilizing scRNA-seq analysis and MRI monitoring.

#### Objective 3: Design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines

This objective focuses on the design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines, specifically antibody-cytokine fusion proteins, to enhance anti-tumor immune responses within the brain tumor microenvironment (TME). The working hypothesis is that by using identified lead cytokines from Objective 2 and coupling them with antibodies that target species-conserved tumor antigens, it is possible to achieve potent immune reactivation and eradication of melanoma brain metastases (BMs). The core goal is to move beyond a binary response/no-response outcome and into iterative cycles of optimization using murine models of melanoma BMs and advanced single-cell analysis technologies. This approach will ultimately identify the most effective immunocytokine constructs for Investigational New Drug (IND) stage development.

This objective's tasks are structured to systematically design, produce and validate the activity of novel immunocytokines. The initial task involves designing fusion proteins and cleavage sites. This will utilize advanced protein design techniques to determine optimal structures for mAb-cytokine combinations, which includes selecting suitable peptide linkers, blocking moieties, tumor-associated macrophages (TAM)-specific cleavage sites and computational optimization of protein structure and stability. The second task focuses on the production of 3-5 mAb-cytokine fusion proteins. Protein synthesis will be outsourced to a contract research organization (CRO) that has been selected based on prior experience. The production will include selection of stable molecules with high protein expression, avoiding aggregation. The final task involves the in-vitro validation of the activity of immunocytokines. This includes confirming binding via Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR), Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay (ELISA), cell-based binding and reporter assays. Furthermore, the impact on myeloid, NK and T cell activity will be validated using co-culture assays with human monocyte-derived macrophages (huMDMs). These assays will evaluate immunocytokine-mediated interactions, using data from Task 1.1 as a control.

The successful completion of this objective will lead to the identification of immunocytokine constructs with superior antitumor activity and safety compared to combined administration of antibodies and cytokines. This will be measured by assessing binding specificity, activation of intra-tumoral effector cells, and cytotoxicity levels. A key dependency is the identification of lead cytokines from Objective 2. The findings will establish a platform for further preclinical and clinical evaluation of immunocytokines and will provide valuable insights into their mechanisms of action, safety profiles and therapeutic effects. This includes understanding of the potential applications and limitations of these fusion proteins, guiding the design and development of next-generation immunocytokines.

##### Preliminary Results

Our preliminary data demonstrates the feasibility of designing and producing functional immunocytokines targeting the tumor microenvironment. We have successfully generated an anti-TREM2 antibody fused with IL-2, which serves as a model for our proposed immunocytokine constructs. This fusion protein has been produced and shown to induce strong pro-inflammatory macrophage activity in vitro, demonstrating the synergistic potential of combining antibody targeting with cytokine activation. We have also developed a masking strategy for the cytokine arm using blocking moieties responsive only to TAM-specific proteases, and we have demonstrated in vivo that this strategy can prevent systemic cytokine activation, which is consistent with findings described in the literature regarding conditionally activated immunocytokine prodrugs. These findings provide proof-of-concept for our approach to construct novel immunocytokines with enhanced tumor-targeting and reduced systemic toxicity. Specifically, we have designed a masking approach that is responsive to TAM-specific proteases, allowing for local activation of the cytokine within the tumor microenvironment. This will provide a basis for evaluating the in-vitro activity of our 3-5 mAb-cytokine fusion proteins, using methods such as SPR, ELISA and cell-based assays. In our assays we will validate the impact of the immunocytokines in co-cultures with huMDMs, NK, and T cells, and will evaluate their ability to stimulate cytotoxicity towards tumor cells. We will evaluate their specificity and potency by measuring intra-tumoral effector cell activation and cytotoxicity levels.

##### Research Tasks

###### Task 3.1: Design fusion proteins and cleavage site

Task 1 will focus on the design of optimal fusion protein structures, utilizing the top 3-5 mAb-cytokine combinations identified in Aim 2. Advanced protein design techniques will be employed to achieve this, with a focus on several key aspects. The design process will encompass the selection of peptide linkers to join the mAb and cytokine components, ensuring appropriate flexibility and minimal steric hindrance. Blocking moieties will be strategically incorporated to prevent premature activation or off-target effects. Tumor-associated macrophage (TAM)-specific cleavage sites will be identified and integrated to enable conditional activation of the cytokine within the tumor microenvironment, enhancing specificity and reducing systemic toxicity. Computational methods will be used to optimize the protein structure, assessing stability, folding, and potential for aggregation. The fusion protein design will also be optimized for high protein expression and will be manufactured by a contract research organization (CRO). This CRO will be selected based on prior experience, focusing on quality and timely production. The resulting fusion protein candidates will be assessed for their binding affinity using Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR), Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay (ELISA), and cell-based binding assays. The effects of the fusion proteins on myeloid, Natural Killer (NK), and T cell activity will be validated using in-vitro assays of co-cultured human monocyte-derived macrophages (huMDMs), NK, or T cells. These assays will measure cell activity, cytotoxicity, and interactions between myeloid and lymphoid cells. The data from Task 1 will be used as a control for these assays. Furthermore, NK and T cell killing assays, involving co-cultured huMDMs, NK or T cells, and tumor cells will be performed to assess the fusion proteins efficacy in inducing cytotoxic activity. Data collected in previous task will serve as control. The design process will consider the synergistic effects of different cytokines, as well as their pharmacological properties and half-lives, in order to optimize the fusion protein design. These designs will be built upon in subsequent tasks to produce the novel mAb-cytokine fusion molecules.

###### Task 3.2: Produce fusion proteins

Task 2.2 involves the production of 3-5 mAb-cytokine fusion proteins, building upon the designs developed in Task 2.1. The protein synthesis will be outsourced to a contract research organization (CRO), selected based on prior experience with leading CROs to ensure quality and punctual production. The selection process will prioritize CROs with a proven track record in producing high-quality fusion proteins with minimal aggregation. The production process will involve transient gene expression in mammalian cells, followed by purification using affinity chromatography and size exclusion chromatography. The purified fusion proteins will undergo rigorous quality control (QC) analysis, including SDS-PAGE, Western blotting, and dynamic light scattering (DLS) to confirm purity, molecular weight, and aggregation status. The selection of stable molecules exhibiting high protein expression will be achieved through a multi-step screening process. This process will include initial screening of protein expression levels via ELISA or similar binding assays, followed by stability assessment using accelerated degradation studies at various temperatures and pH values. Molecules demonstrating optimal stability and expression characteristics will be advanced for large-scale production. This selection process is critical to ensure that the fusion proteins used in subsequent validation steps are of high quality and suitable for in vitro and in vivo assays. This task depends on the designs made in Task 2.1, which outlines the structure of the fusion proteins, including peptide linkers, blocking moieties and TAM specific cleavage sites. The successful completion of this task is essential for the subsequent validation of the pro-inflammatory and antineoplastic activity of the fusion proteins.

###### Task 3.3: In-vitro validation of immunocytokines

Task 3 will focus on the in-vitro validation of immunocytokines. The binding of the produced immunocytokines will be confirmed using surface plasmon resonance (SPR), enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA), cell-based binding assays, and reporter assays. This task will also evaluate the immunocytokines' impact on interactions between myeloid and lymphoid cell activity through in-vitro co-culture assays involving human monocyte-derived macrophages (huMDMs), natural killer (NK) cells, or T cells. Furthermore, the efficacy of the fusion proteins in inducing cytotoxic NK and T cell activity will be assessed. This involves treating co-cultures of huMDMs, NK cells, or T cells with tumor cells using the various monoclonal antibody (mAb)-cytokine chimeras. The data collected in Task 1.2, where the ability of cytokines in combination with the E3C7 mAb to induce cytotoxic T and NK cell activity was assessed, will be used as a control. NK and T cell activity and cytotoxicity levels will be measured by quantifying the amount of tumor cell death using FACS. These experiments will provide a better understanding of the nature and role of tumor rejection antigens, and information on the dynamics and breadth of T cell specificities in response to therapy. The mechanistic investigation of NK cells is more difficult, but this task will help to clarify the key molecular events responsible for the anti-tumoral activity of activated NK cells, as depletion experiments in preclinical models and Killer Immunoglobulin-like Receptor (KIR) genotype analyses of clinical data demonstrate the potent contribution of this cell type to therapeutic action of immunocytokines. This task is dependent on the completion of Task 3.2, as the in vitro validation requires the produced fusion proteins.

##### Risks and Alternatives

The primary risks associated with this objective revolve around the design and production of functional immunocytokines. A key technical risk is the potential failure to generate stable mAb-cytokine fusion proteins with high expression levels and without aggregation, which could impede in vitro validation. To mitigate this, the CRO will be selected based on their prior experience and proven track record in producing similar molecules, with an emphasis on stable protein expression and aggregation prevention. Another risk lies in the selection of optimal peptide linkers, blocking moieties and cleavage sites. Suboptimal choices here can affect the immunocytokine's stability and activity. This will be addressed by employing advanced protein design techniques and computational optimization to ensure structural integrity and functionality. Furthermore, the in-vitro validation process may encounter issues, particularly in confirming specific binding via SPR and ELISA. As a mitigation, we will employ a suite of binding assays including cell-based reporter assays to validate the activity of each molecule. A methodological risk is the potential for immunocytokine constructs to exhibit off-target effects. To address this, we will rigorously evaluate the impact on myeloid, NK, and T cell activity in co-culture assays, including control assays of unmodified cytokines and antibodies. Finally, a resource dependency lies in the successful identification of lead cytokines from Objective 2, which if delayed would impede the development of fusion molecules. Contingency planning includes having backup cytokines in mind and utilizing a broader range of cytokines to begin with.
## Experimental Design and Methods
To achieve the project's aims, we will employ a multi-faceted experimental design, leveraging advanced single-cell technologies and AI-driven analysis. This will be achieved through a combination of in vitro and in vivo experiments. Our methodology includes the following steps: 1) We will establish robust orthotopic immunocompetent murine models of melanoma brain metastases and collect patient-derived clinical samples. These samples will be processed to create single-cell atlases of immune, stromal, and tumor cells from both human and murine BMs. This will involve utilizing our novel technologies for cell temporal tracking (ZMAN-seq, differentiation flows), cell-cell interaction analysis (PIC-seq), and advanced AI analysis of spatial transcriptomics using Stereo-seq. 2) In vitro functional screening will be performed using a system for bone-marrow-derived monocyte differentiation towards the M2 suppressor macrophage state. This system includes a control using monocytes from TREM2-/- mice. We will co-culture myeloid and NK-T cells and apply scRNA-seq analysis to understand the effects of perturbations created by selected cytokines in combination with our anti-TREM2 mAb. 3) In vivo screening will be conducted using a platform to over-express cytokines in an inducible fashion with barcoded tumor cell lines. The most promising cytokines from the in vitro screen will be screened in vivo with our anti-TREM2 mAbs. Again, we will use in depth scRNA-seq analysis to select 3-5 cytokines for further study. For all experiments, we will use appropriate controls, including isotype control antibodies, and vehicle treatments. Reproducibility will be ensured by performing experiments in replicates and using standardized protocols. Data validation will be performed using multiple statistical analyses to ensure scientific rigor. The data management plan involves storage of raw and processed data in a secure cloud environment and following FAIR principles. We will apply our in-depth single-cell and spatial analysis pipelines, and combine it with advanced machine learning tools, to identify novel biomarkers and therapeutic targets. This data will also inform masking strategies to enhance the specificity of immunocytokines. This comprehensive approach will enable the identification of novel therapeutic strategies, particularly by modulating multiple immune cell compartments simultaneously.
## Statistical Plan
The statistical analysis of single-cell RNA sequencing data will be performed using established bioinformatics pipelines, including SCANPY for data normalization, dimensionality reduction, and clustering. Differential gene expression analysis will be conducted using DESeq2 to identify statistically significant changes in gene expression between experimental groups. For cell temporal tracking data from ZMAN-seq, we will employ a generalized linear model to assign time bins to individual cells and assess the temporal dynamics of gene expression. MetaCell will be used for the analysis of single-cell data including construction of metacells, ensuring homogeneity within each metacell. For spatial transcriptomics data from Stereo-seq, we will use a spatial k-nearest neighbor approach to integrate spatial information into clustering and data interpretation. Sample size calculations will be based on power analysis to ensure sufficient statistical power to detect meaningful differences in the experimental groups, taking into consideration the expected effect sizes and variability from preliminary experiments. Statistical tests, such as t-tests, ANOVA, and non-parametric tests, will be employed based on the distribution of the data and specific research question. Data interpretation will be performed through visualization and pathway analysis, emphasizing the biological context of our findings. Statistical methods will be rigorously controlled and reported in detail to ensure reproducibility and data integrity. Reproducibility will also be ensured by performing experiments in replicates and using standardized protocols, in addition to using multiple statistical analyses to ensure rigor.
