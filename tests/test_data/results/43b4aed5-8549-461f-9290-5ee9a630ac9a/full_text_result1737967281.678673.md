# Developing AI tailored immunocytokines to target melanoma brain metastases

## Narrative

### Research Plan

### Objective 1: Developing BM TME models with holistic, multimodal AI-driven analysis

Objective 1 focuses on the comprehensive characterization of the Bone Marrow Tumor Microenvironment (BM TME) using advanced single-cell technologies and AI-driven analysis. This objective will employ Zman-seq to capture the temporal dynamics of immune activity within the BM TME, facilitating the identification of cell state transitions and trajectories over time. We will use the Zman-seq methodology to track transcriptional changes in immune cells in vivo after exposure to the tumor. The Zman-seq approach enables us to analyze in vivo transcriptomic cell states across time by assigning continuous tumor exposure time values, which elucidates the gradual shift of cell states across time, leading to the identification of genes that change their expression over time in response to tumor exposure. Specifically, we will apply this technology to track the temporal evolution of immune cell populations, including regulatory macrophages, T cells and NK cells. This temporal understanding is essential for identifying key time points at which therapeutic interventions might be most effective. Further enhancing our analysis, PIC-seq will be utilized to investigate specific cell-cell interactions within the BM TME. This will provide insights into the complex signaling networks that govern immune suppression and tumor progression, enabling us to identify critical interactions that might be targeted to restore anti-tumor immunity. Spatial transcriptomics, using stereo-seq, will map the immune cell distribution within the BM TME, providing a crucial spatial context to our single-cell data. Stereo-seq will allow for highly sensitive spatial transcriptomics at near-cellular resolution, enabling a detailed mapping of the BM TME's architecture. The spatial data will be used to identify areas of high immune cell infiltration or exclusion, as well as to uncover the spatial relationships between different cell types. This will facilitate the identification of potential therapeutic targets based on their localization within the BM TME. The analysis of the complex multimodal data generated from Zman-seq, PIC-seq, and stereo-seq will rely heavily on advanced AI-driven analysis. AI algorithms will be utilized to process the data, identify patterns, and generate hypotheses regarding the dynamics of the BM TME. This computational analysis will enable us to integrate the temporal, interactional, and spatial data into a cohesive understanding of the BM TME. The integration of the Zman-seq, PIC-seq and stereo-seq data with AI analysis will create a holistic and in depth understanding of the BM TME. The expected outcomes of this objective include a detailed characterization of the BM TME, including the temporal dynamics of immune activity, specific cell-cell interactions, and the spatial distribution of immune cells. Deliverables from this objective will comprise comprehensive datasets and models of the BM TME, as well as a set of potential therapeutic targets based on these analyses. This objective will form the basis for subsequent objectives by providing foundational data that can be used to drive the design and testing of novel immunocytokines in Objective 2, and also inform the design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines in Objective 3. The comprehensive understanding of the BM TME gained through this objective will be critical for the selection of cytokines for screening in Objective 2, and will also guide the selection of targets and design parameters for Objective 3. The findings from this objective will also be used to further investigate specific aspects of the TME that are found to be clinically relevant. The use of these cutting edge technologies coupled with AI-driven analysis will provide unprecedented insights into the mechanisms of immune suppression in the BM TME and will enable the rational design of novel therapeutic strategies.

#### 1.1: Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME

Task 1.1 will employ Zman-seq to track temporal changes in the BM TME, building upon previously established methodologies used in glioma research. This will provide critical temporal resolution to study the dynamics of immune cell infiltration and functional reprogramming. Specifically, Zman-seq will be used to label and track immune cells as they infiltrate the BM TME, measuring the time-dependent transcriptomic changes and cell state transitions. We will adapt the Zman-seq methodology previously employed in glioma models, where we identified the rapid transition of NK cells to a dysfunctional state within 24 hours, and monocytes to regulatory macrophages within 36-48 hours, focusing on the unique cellular and temporal dynamics within the BM. This will include the use of fluorescent antibody time stamps to track immune cells in vivo and the use of FACS to analyze the labeled cells and their transcriptomes. This approach will allow us to generate a detailed temporal map of the immune response within the BM TME, identifying key time points at which changes in immune activity occur. We will analyze early (12 hour), intermediate (24 hour), and late (36-48 hour) time points to capture the dynamic transitions of immune cell states. The expected output of Task 1.1 will be a detailed temporal map of immune cell populations, such as regulatory macrophages, T cells, and NK cells, their transcriptional changes, and trajectories over time. This will allow us to identify gene expression changes that occur in response to the BM TME. These temporal data will inform our understanding of how immune cell function and interactions are altered within the BM TME, revealing the timing of key events, such as the transition of NK cells from cytotoxic to dysfunctional states, and the differentiation of monocytes to immunosuppressive macrophages, mirroring the cell state transition in the glioma model. Furthermore, it will elucidate the temporal relationships between cell populations that may drive immune dysfunction. The results will guide the selection of time points for the cell-cell interaction analyses in Task 1.2 and will be integrated with spatial data from Task 1.3 to provide a comprehensive understanding of the BM TME.

#### 1.2: Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME

Task 1.2 will employ PIC-seq to dissect cell-cell interactions within the BM TME. This methodology will identify physically interacting immune cells, providing a direct measure of cellular communication. Specifically, we will focus on interactions between regulatory macrophages (TREM2+), T cells, and NK cells, as these populations are crucial in the immunosuppressive environment of the BM TME. PIC-seq will be performed on BM samples at specific time points informed by the Zman-seq data from Task 1.1, ensuring that cell-cell interaction analyses are conducted during periods of significant immune cell state transitions. The PIC-seq workflow will involve sorting physically interacting cells, followed by single-cell RNA sequencing to profile the transcriptomes of these interacting pairs. Computational modeling will then be used to identify ligand-receptor pairs and analyze the impact of cell-cell interactions on gene expression. This analysis will reveal specific signaling pathways that are activated or suppressed during these interactions, providing insights into the mechanisms of immune dysfunction. The data generated will be integrated with the Zman-seq data from Task 1.1 to correlate cell-cell interaction data with the temporal dynamics of immune cell state transitions. This will allow us to identify specific interactions that drive the development of dysfunctional phenotypes in the BM TME. Furthermore, these data will provide a crucial context for the spatial information generated in Task 1.3, enabling us to understand how cell-cell interactions affect the spatial organization of the BM TME. This comprehensive approach will enable the identification of novel therapeutic targets aimed at disrupting immunosuppressive cell interactions and promoting anti-tumor immunity.

#### 1.3: Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME

Task 1.3 will employ stereo-seq to map the spatial distribution of immune cells within the BM TME, focusing on regions of high tumor burden and areas of immune infiltration. This spatial transcriptomics approach will provide a high-resolution view of the BM TME architecture, enabling the identification of spatial niches occupied by different immune cell populations, including regulatory macrophages, T cells, and NK cells. Stereo-seq will allow for the simultaneous detection of gene expression across a large field of view at near-cellular resolution, ensuring a comprehensive capture of the spatial heterogeneity of the BM TME. The data generated by stereo-seq will be analyzed using a spatially constrained clustering algorithm to delineate distinct tissue domains based on their transcriptomic profiles. This will include analysis of spatial cell-type heterogeneity and the identification of genes associated with specific spatial domains. The spatial distribution data will then be integrated with the temporal data from Task 1.1 and the cell-cell interaction data from Task 1.2 to build a comprehensive model of the BM TME. Specifically, we will correlate the spatial localization of different immune cell types with their temporal dynamics and interactions, revealing how spatial relationships influence immune cell behavior and function. This will allow for the identification of spatial patterns that are time-dependent. The integration of spatial data will also allow for the evaluation of whether cell-cell interaction data can predict spatial relationships. Expected outcomes include detailed maps of immune cell distribution within the BM TME, which will be crucial for understanding the organization of the BM TME and the spatial constraints on immune cell function. The results of this task will inform the design of immunotherapies that can target specific spatial niches within the BM TME to enhance anti-tumor immunity. The detailed spatial information will be used to identify areas of high immune cell infiltration or exclusion, as well as spatial relationships between different cell types.

### Objective 2: Preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models

Objective 2 will focus on the preclinical screening of cytokines to identify promising therapeutic candidates for modulating the BM TME. This objective will directly leverage the comprehensive BM TME characterization from Objective 1, using the identified temporal, interactional, and spatial dynamics to inform cytokine selection and screening strategies. Initially, we will perform cytokine screening using our in-house library of cytokines, which includes a broad spectrum of potential BM immune re-activation candidates. These cytokines will be tested for their ability to modulate immune cell activity within the BM TME, focusing on myeloid cells, NK cells, and T cells. The initial screening will employ an in-vitro system, based on bone-marrow-derived monocyte diversification toward the M2 suppressor macrophage state, and will assess the impact of selected cytokines in combination with our E3C7 mAb. This system will utilize co-cultures of myeloid and NK/T cells, coupled with single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq), to provide a detailed understanding of the cytokine-induced perturbations. Control cultures of monocytes derived from TREM2-/- mice, which do not differentiate into M2 macrophages under the same conditions, will be included to establish the specificity of cytokine-mediated effects. The most promising cytokines from the in-vitro screening, typically 5-8 candidates, will be advanced for further validation in-vivo. This validation step will involve an orthotopic immunocompetent murine model of melanoma BM, where cytokine expression will be induced in a controlled fashion using our inducible barcoded (Pro-Code) tumor cell lines. These clones will be used to generate melanoma BMs by intracardial injections, and the in-vivo effect of cytokine expression will be activated with doxycycline. The tumor response will be monitored using MRI and survival measurements. In this in-vivo model, we will apply our in-depth scRNA-seq analysis pipeline to make a data-driven selection of the 3-5 cytokines that exhibit the most promising modulatory function, in combination with our anti-TREM2 mAb. This functional screening process will also identify trans-acting MiTEs, further refining our understanding of the BM TME and therapeutic targets. Furthermore, the selected cytokines will be validated in-vitro using tumor spheroids containing one or multiple cytokine cassettes, co-cultured with immune cells. This will allow us to test varied cytokine combinations by adding doxycycline, and assess their impact on immune cell modulation. These comprehensive in-vitro and in-vivo validation studies will allow for data-driven selection of cytokines with optimal activity against melanoma BM TME. These studies will allow us to identify cytokines with optimal activity against melanoma BM TME, as well as a selection of cytokine combinations that have synergistic effects on different immune cell subsets. The findings from this objective will directly inform the design of immunocytokines in Objective 3, by providing critical data on the most promising cytokines and their combinations, which can then be used in the construction and validation of fusion proteins. The expected deliverables from this objective include a refined list of therapeutic cytokines, validated for their efficacy in modulating myeloid, NK and T cell activity within the BM TME, along with detailed insights into the temporal and spatial effects of these cytokines. The functional screening of our in-house library of cytokines will also allow for identification of trans-acting MiTEs. The data generated from this objective will be essential for the rational design of effective immunocytokines aimed at disrupting the immunosuppressive environment of the BM TME.

#### 2.1: Screening of cytokines in BM TME

Task 2.1 will employ a high-throughput screening approach using our in-house cytokine library to identify cytokines that modulate immune activity within the BM TME. This screening process will begin with an in-vitro co-culture system of bone-marrow-derived monocytes and NK/T cells. Monocytes will be differentiated towards the M2 suppressor macrophage state to mimic the immunosuppressive BM TME. The in-house cytokine library will be screened by adding individual cytokines to these co-cultures. The modulatory effect of each cytokine on immune cell activity will be evaluated by measuring changes in cellular transcriptomes using scRNA-seq. Specifically, we will look for cytokines that shift M2 macrophages toward a pro-inflammatory phenotype, and increase activation markers in NK and T cells. Control cultures, utilizing monocytes derived from TREM2-/- mice, which are unable to differentiate into M2 macrophages, will be used to establish the specificity of the cytokine-mediated effects. The screening will prioritize cytokines that show a significant shift in immune cell profiles, indicating their potential to reverse the immunosuppressive environment. We will apply stringent statistical analysis to identify cytokines that have significant effects on the transcriptome of the immune cell subsets. The experimental design will control for potential confounding factors, such as batch effects and variability in cell source, by including multiple replicates and using randomized sample processing. The anticipated results of this screening task will be a prioritized list of cytokines with the most significant impact on immune cell modulation within the BM TME. The selection criteria will include the magnitude of the shift toward a pro-inflammatory phenotype in macrophages and the activation of NK and T cells. The screening data will also reveal potential trans-acting MiTEs that are regulated by cytokine activity. These screening results, including the scRNA-seq data, will be prepared for further in vitro validation using tumor spheroids in Task 2.2 and in vivo validation in Task 2.3. We aim to identify 5-8 cytokines in this task that will be selected for further validation.

#### 2.2: In-vitro validation of cytokines

Task 2.2 will focus on the in-vitro validation of the cytokines identified in Task 2.1 using a co-culture system of bone-marrow-derived monocytes and NK/T cells. This system, detailed in Task 1.4, allows for the assessment of cytokine-induced perturbations on myeloid cell differentiation towards the M2 suppressor macrophage state, and on the activity of co-cultured NK and T cells. The cytokines, selected from the in-house library and based on the findings of Task 2.1, will be tested individually and in combinations in this system. Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) will be employed to assess the impact of the selected cytokines, in combination with our E3C7 mAb, on gene expression profiles of both myeloid cells and NK/T cells. Control cultures will include monocytes derived from TREM2-/- mice, which do not differentiate into M2 macrophages under the same conditions, to establish the specificity of cytokine-mediated effects on monocyte diversification. This will ensure that the observed changes are specifically due to the cytokines and not due to off-target effects or general stimuli. The primary endpoints of this in-vitro validation will include changes in the expression of key genes associated with M2 macrophage polarization and markers of NK and T cell activation or suppression. We will also use computational methods to analyze the single-cell RNA-seq data to predict potential in-vivo efficacy of the cytokines based on their ability to modulate immune cell phenotype. These data will also provide insight on which cytokine combinations will have synergistic effects on different immune cell subsets. The in-vitro validation results will inform the selection of cytokines for further in-vivo validation in Task 2.3, by providing preliminary data on their efficacy and mechanisms of action. Discrepancies between in-vitro and in-vivo validations may lead to refinement of in-vitro validation methods and further inform the in-vivo screening. The cytokines will also be validated in-vitro using tumor spheroids containing one or multiple cytokine cassettes, co-cultured with immune cells, to assess their impact on immune cell modulation.

#### 2.3: In-vivo validation of cytokines

In Task 2.3, the 5-8 most promising cytokines identified from the in-vitro functional screening system in Task 2.2 will undergo in-vivo validation. These cytokines, cloned into inducible expression vectors, will be introduced into orthotopic immunocompetent murine models of melanoma BM via intracardial injection of barcoded tumor cell lines. Cytokine expression will be activated by doxycycline, and the tumor response will be assessed using MRI and survival measurements. In-vivo validation will use single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) to evaluate the impact of the selected cytokines on myeloid, NK, and T cell activity within the BM TME. This will enable a data-driven selection of the 3-5 cytokines demonstrating the most promising modulatory effects, in combination with our anti-TREM2 mAb. The scRNA-seq analysis will provide insights into the temporal and spatial dynamics of these immune cell populations in response to cytokine expression. Further, the functional screening system will allow for identification of trans-acting MiTEs, which will be used to refine our understanding of the BM TME and potential therapeutic targets. The in-vivo endpoints will include changes in immune cell infiltration, activation status, and functional reprogramming, along with tumor growth inhibition and increased survival. The data gathered from this task will be crucial for the design of immunocytokines in Objective 3, guiding the selection of the optimal cytokine combinations and therapeutic strategies. The in-vivo validation will also identify potential synergistic effects between selected cytokines in modulating immune cell subsets. The combination of MRI and survival endpoints with the single-cell analysis will allow us to define the most effective cytokine combinations for translation into immunocytokine design.

### Objective 3: Design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines

Objective 3 will focus on the design, production, and in-vitro validation of tumor-targeting immunocytokines. The immunocytokine design will incorporate the top mAb-cytokine combinations identified in Objective 2 and will be tailored to the specific characteristics of the BM TME identified in Objective 1. This objective will utilize a novel approach to fusion protein design, incorporating peptide linkers, blocking moieties, and TAM-specific cleavage sites to optimize targeting and activity. The design process will employ computational modeling to refine the structure of the fusion proteins and ensure optimal binding and functional activity. Specifically, the immunocytokines will be engineered to target tumor antigens highly expressed in melanoma BMs while carrying potent cytokines that reactivate the immune compartment. The selection of peptide linkers will be based on their flexibility and stability, ensuring the optimal spatial orientation of the mAb and cytokine domains. Blocking moieties will be designed to mask the cytokine activity until the immunocytokine reaches the tumor microenvironment. TAM-specific cleavage sites will be incorporated to allow for the conditional activation of the cytokine only within the BM TME. The design will be optimized using in silico modeling to predict protein structure, binding affinity, and stability. The production of the mAb-cytokine fusion proteins will be carried out using a robust recombinant protein expression system. This process will include rigorous quality control measures, such as purity analysis by SDS-PAGE and size-exclusion chromatography, and stability testing to ensure consistent and high-quality production. The proteins will be manufactured under GMP-compliant conditions and characterized for their physicochemical properties. The in-vitro validation of the immunocytokines will assess their binding and functional activity. Binding assays, including surface plasmon resonance (SPR) and ELISA, will be used to confirm the affinity of the fusion proteins for the target antigen. Cell-based binding assays will be used to validate the targeting specificity of the immunocytokines. Reporter assays will evaluate the cytokine activity of the immunocytokines upon cleavage. The efficacy of the fusion proteins on cytotoxic activity of NK and T cells will be assessed by co-culturing immune cells with tumor cells, and measuring the cytotoxic activity. The validation will include assays to assess the effect of the immunocytokine on the cytotoxic activity of NK cells and T cells when co-cultured with tumor cells. These assays will measure the impact of the mAb-cytokine chimeras on cell viability and the production of cytotoxic cytokines. Functional assays will assess the ability of the immunocytokines to activate the immune cells and induce cytokine production. The expected deliverables for this objective include the design and production of several immunocytokine constructs, optimized for tumor targeting and immune activation. Each construct will be fully characterized in vitro through SPR, ELISA, and cell-based assays, to validate their binding specificity and functional activity. The validated immunocytokines will be ready for in vivo testing in subsequent stages of the research. The milestones include the completion of fusion protein design, successful manufacturing of the immunocytokines, and the in-vitro validation of their binding and functional activity. The design of immunocytokines will be informed by the selection of the mAb-cytokine combination from Objective 2 and the in-depth characterization of the BM TME from Objective 1. The validation process will provide critical data on the binding, specificity, and activity of the engineered immunocytokines and will guide the selection of candidates for in vivo testing. Results from Objective 3 will inform refinements to the TME models developed in Objective 1 and may lead to improvements in cytokine selection for Objective 2.

#### 3.1: Design fusion proteins and cleavage site

Task 3.1 will focus on the detailed design of fusion proteins, incorporating the top mAb-cytokine combinations identified in Objective 2. This design process will employ advanced protein engineering techniques to optimize the immunocytokine structure for enhanced tumor targeting and conditional activation within the BM TME. The methodology will prioritize the selection of peptide linkers that provide optimal flexibility and stability, ensuring proper spatial orientation of the mAb and cytokine domains. Blocking moieties will be strategically designed to mask cytokine activity, preventing off-target effects until the immunocytokine reaches the tumor site. TAM-specific cleavage sites will be incorporated to enable conditional activation of the cytokine within the BM TME, where the high concentrations of TAM-associated proteases will trigger the cleavage of the blocking moieties, releasing the active cytokine. Computational optimization will be a critical component of the protein design, involving in silico modeling to predict protein structure, binding affinity, and stability. These computational methods will aid in refining the fusion protein structure, identifying potential issues related to protein aggregation or misfolding, and enhancing the overall stability of the immunocytokine. The design parameters, including the choice of peptide linkers, blocking moieties, and cleavage sites, will be selected to improve the tumor targeting and efficacy of the immunocytokines, minimizing off-target effects, and maximizing therapeutic potential. The selection of peptide linkers will be based on their known properties such as flexibility and stability, ensuring that they do not interfere with the binding and activity of the mAb or cytokine domains. Blocking moieties will be selected such that they can be cleaved by TAM specific proteases within the BM TME, but remain stable in circulation, ensuring targeted release of cytokine. The choice of TAM specific cleavage sites will also be based on the known specificity of different proteases, ensuring the cleavage takes place only in the tumor microenvironment.

#### 3.2: Produce fusion proteins

Task 3.2 focuses on the manufacturing of the mAb-cytokine fusion proteins. The protein synthesis will be outsourced to a contract research organization (CRO). The selection of the CRO will be based on a proven track record of quality and punctual production, leveraging our experience with leading CROs. The manufacturing process will prioritize the selection of stable molecules exhibiting high protein expression and minimal aggregation. To ensure stable molecules with high protein expression, the CRO will employ a robust recombinant protein expression system. This system will be optimized for the specific fusion protein designs developed in Task 3.1. The process will include stringent codon optimization of the constructs, and the use of high-expression cell lines. To minimize protein aggregation, the manufacturing process will incorporate techniques such as controlled-rate protein folding and purification using size-exclusion chromatography. Formulations containing stabilizing excipients will be tested, aiming to enhance long-term protein stability. The quality and purity of the manufactured fusion proteins will be assessed using sodium dodecyl-sulfate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) and size-exclusion chromatography. Furthermore, the fusion proteins will undergo rigorous physicochemical characterization, including assessments of their binding affinity, and stability under various storage conditions. The produced proteins will be characterized for their structural integrity and biological activity before proceeding to Task 3.3.

#### 3.3: In-vitro validation of immunocytokines

Task 3.3 will focus on the in-vitro validation of the immunocytokines, employing a range of assays to assess their binding, activity, and impact on immune cells and tumor cells. Surface plasmon resonance (SPR) will be used to quantify the binding kinetics of the immunocytokines to their target antigens, determining association and dissociation rates. ELISA assays will be performed to confirm the specificity of the immunocytokines for their intended targets. Cell-based binding assays will further validate the targeting of the immunocytokines to tumor cells and relevant immune cell subsets, confirming that the molecules bind to the intended cell types and not to off-target cells. Reporter assays will be employed to assess the functional activity of the cytokines upon cleavage, measuring the induction of downstream signaling pathways and cytokine production. Co-culture assays will be utilized to evaluate the impact of the immunocytokines on myeloid and lymphoid cell activity. These assays will involve co-culturing human monocyte-derived macrophages (huMDMs), NK cells, and T cells to assess the modulation of their function in response to immunocytokine treatment. Specifically, the impact on NK cell and T cell activation, proliferation, and cytokine production will be analyzed. Furthermore, co-culture assays of immune cells and tumor cells will assess the cytotoxic activity of the immunocytokines. These assays will measure tumor cell death, quantifying the cytotoxic effects of NK cells and T cells induced by the immunocytokines. The data from these assays will be compared to the data collected in Task 1.2, which assessed the cytotoxic effects of cytokines in combination with E3C7 mAb, to determine the effectiveness of the immunocytokine constructs. Malignant melanoma remains the most lethal form of skin cancer, with a high propensity for metastasis, particularly to the brain. Brain metastases (BMs) occur in approximately 50% of patients with advanced melanoma, significantly reducing survival rates and quality of life. Despite advancements in melanoma treatment, including immunotherapies and targeted therapies, the prognosis for patients with BMs remains poor, highlighting a critical unmet need for effective therapeutic strategies. A review of the literature underscores the challenges in managing melanoma BMs, revealing limited efficacy of current treatments and the need for novel approaches. While immunotherapies have demonstrated success in some melanoma patients, the immunosuppressive microenvironment of the brain often limits their effectiveness, leading to treatment resistance and disease progression. The unique characteristics of the BM tumor microenvironment (TME), including the presence of regulatory macrophages and the limited infiltration of cytotoxic immune cells, contribute to this immunosuppressive state. This complex interplay between tumor cells and the immune system within the BM TME represents a significant knowledge gap that hinders the development of effective immunotherapies. Recent studies emphasize the need for a deeper understanding of the cellular and molecular mechanisms that drive immune suppression in the BM TME. The limitations of current therapies, such as chemotherapies and targeted therapies, in addressing the intricacies of the BM TME underscore the urgent need for novel strategies. Single-cell technologies, coupled with advanced computational analysis, provide unprecedented opportunities to dissect the complexities of the BM TME and identify novel therapeutic targets. These technologies enable a deeper understanding of cellular heterogeneity, cell-cell interactions, and spatial organization within the TME, providing valuable insights for developing targeted interventions. Our preliminary data has identified a key component of the immunosuppressive TME, regulatory (TREM2+) macrophages, and demonstrated the synergistic potential of anti-TREM2 antibodies in combination with cytokines in vitro. This provides a strong scientific rationale for the proposed research, which seeks to leverage these findings to develop novel immunocytokine therapies. Our approach will employ cutting-edge single-cell technologies, including Zman-seq for temporal tracking of immune cell dynamics, PIC-seq for dissecting cell-cell interactions, and stereo-seq for mapping spatial distribution of cells within the BM TME. Coupled with advanced AI analysis, we will be able to integrate this multimodal data to generate a comprehensive understanding of the BM TME. This comprehensive analysis will enable the identification of specific cytokines capable of modulating the BM TME towards anti-tumor activity. Our rationale for this study stems from the need for novel immunotherapeutic approaches that can overcome the limitations of current therapies and induce robust anti-tumor immune responses in the challenging environment of the BM TME. We propose to develop immunocytokines that simultaneously target multiple immune cell compartments (macrophages, T cells, NK cells). This is based on the premise that modulation of different immune cell subsets can induce a synergistic anti-tumor immune response. The design of our immunocytokines will be informed by the deep characterization of the BM TME, leveraging identified temporal, interactional, and spatial dynamics. This informed approach will allow for the rational design of immunocytokines that exhibit enhanced tumor targeting and immune activation. The use of masking strategies will further increase the specificity of our immunocytokines to the BM TME, minimizing off-target effects and maximizing therapeutic efficacy. Our strategy also directly addresses the limitations identified in the literature by moving away from therapies that are limited by the blood-brain barrier. By leveraging insights from our single cell analysis, we will also aim to identify biomarkers to design masking strategies to increase the specificity of the immunocytokine to the BM TME. Our approach addresses several major unmet needs in melanoma BM treatment: (1) the development of novel therapies that can overcome the immunosuppressive environment of the BM TME, (2) the lack of targeted therapies that can induce robust anti-tumor immune responses, and (3) the need for more effective treatments that can improve survival and quality of life for patients with melanoma BMs. This proposal will address these needs by providing comprehensive insights into the BM TME and delivering novel immunocytokine therapies with the potential to revolutionize the treatment of melanoma BMs.### Preliminary Data

## Project Summary

This project aims to revolutionize the treatment of melanoma brain metastases (BM) by developing novel, tumor-targeting immunocytokines. Our research leverages cutting-edge single-cell technologies and AI-driven analysis to achieve a comprehensive understanding of the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME). Specifically, we will employ Zman-seq, PIC-seq, and stereo-seq to map the temporal dynamics, cell-cell interactions, and spatial distribution of immune cells within the BM TME. This detailed characterization will inform the selection of cytokines with the most potent immunomodulatory effects. We will then screen our in-house library of cytokines in preclinical orthotopic immunocompetent BM models to identify the most promising therapeutic candidates. The selected cytokines will be combined with our anti-TREM2 antibody and engineered into tumor-targeting immunocytokines. These immunocytokines will be designed with peptide linkers, blocking moieties, and TAM-specific cleavage sites to enhance specificity and conditional activation within the BM TME. The immunocytokines will then be produced and rigorously validated in-vitro, using a range of assays to assess their binding, activity, and impact on immune and tumor cells. The project's clinical impact lies in its potential to overcome the immunosuppressive nature of the BM TME, a significant barrier to successful melanoma treatment, by inducing a targeted and robust anti-tumor immune response. The innovative design of our immunocytokines, which combines tumor-targeting with conditional cytokine release, will maximize therapeutic efficacy while minimizing off-target effects. The translational potential of this project is significant, offering a novel therapeutic approach for melanoma BM patients with an urgent unmet clinical need. Our approach utilizes state-of-the-art technologies and advanced protein engineering and will contribute to our understanding of BM TME, leading to development of novel immunotherapies for melanoma brain metastasis.
