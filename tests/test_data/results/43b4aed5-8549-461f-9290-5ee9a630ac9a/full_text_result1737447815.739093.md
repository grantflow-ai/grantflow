# Project Summary
## General Audience Abstract
This research project focuses on improving outcomes for patients with melanoma that has spread to the brain. Melanoma brain metastases are particularly difficult to treat, and current therapies often fail. Using cutting-edge single-cell technologies and artificial intelligence, we will analyze the complex environment surrounding these tumors to identify new ways to stimulate the body's immune system to fight the cancer. We have discovered a specific type of immune cell that suppresses the body's anti-cancer response. Our goal is to develop a novel therapy that combines an antibody blocking this suppressive cell with immune-stimulating molecules, thus creating a more powerful and targeted treatment for melanoma brain metastases. This new approach has the potential to significantly increase the survival and improve the quality of life for individuals affected by this disease.
## Technical Abstract
Brain metastases (BMs) from melanoma exhibit a dismal prognosis due to an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). This project leverages advanced single-cell technologies, including ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq, coupled with AI, to dissect the melanoma BM TME. Specific aims include: 1) identifying cytokines that can remodel the TME towards anti-tumor activity by developing BM TME models with holistic, multimodal AI-driven analysis; 2) preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models, employing cytokine-secreting melanoma clones to assess in vitro and in vivo efficacy; and 3) designing tumor-targeting immunocytokines by incorporating potent cytokines into antibody-cytokine fusion proteins targeting melanoma BM antigens, with the goal of increasing specificity to the BM TME using masking techniques. The expected outcomes are an in-depth understanding of the BM TME, identification of novel immunocytokines, and biomarkers to refine treatment specificity. This translational research is expected to improve melanoma BM treatment, potentially increasing life expectancy and quality of life for patients by simultaneously modulating macrophages, T cells, and NK cells.

# Project Description
## Background and Specific Aims
Brain metastases (BMs) occur in almost 50% of patients with metastatic melanoma, resulting in a dismal prognosis and poor overall survival. Immunotherapy has revolutionized melanoma treatment, but many patients still experience relapse or do not respond, particularly in BMs where the brain microenvironment is highly immunosuppressive. Single-cell technologies and machine learning have emerged as powerful tools to decipher complex interactions within the tumor microenvironment (TME), enabling data-driven immunotherapy designs. Our research, utilizing advanced single-cell technologies, has identified regulatory (TREM2+) macrophages as a crucial component of the immunosuppressive TME in melanoma BMs. These cells suppress the body's anti-cancer response, especially in immunotherapy-resistant tumors. We have developed an antibody that blocks the suppressive action of these cells.

Our rationale is that advances in single cell technologies enable an in-depth understanding of the immune environment of BMs and identification of novel approaches for activating the immune system to eliminate the BMs, and that simultaneously modulating different immune cell compartments (macrophages, T cells, NK cells) can induce unprecedented anti-tumor immune response. Our hypothesis is that with our advanced single-cell technologies, including cell temporal tracking (ZMAN-seq, differentiation flows), cell-cell interactions (PIC-seq), and improved strategies for AI analysis of spatial transcriptomics with Stereo-seq, we can obtain a comprehensive understanding of the BM TME and identify cytokines capable of remodeling it towards anti-tumor activity. This will enable us to design novel immunocytokines combining the most promising cytokines with our anti-TREM2 antibody to modulate both myeloid and T/NK cell compartments. Our single-cell and AI analysis will also enable us to identify biomarkers for designing masking strategies to increase the specificity of the immunocytokine to the BM TME. Specific aims include: 1) Developing BM TME models with holistic, multimodal AI-driven analysis to create single-cell atlases of immune, stromal, and tumor cells, identifying pathways and cytokines with antitumor activity in melanoma BMs; 2) Preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models to identify the most effective cytokines and cytokine combinations in vitro and in vivo, by generating cytokine-secreting brain tropic melanoma cell lines; 3) Design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines, incorporating potent cytokines into antibody-cytokine fusion proteins targeting melanoma BM-specific antigens, breaking through the classical dichotomy of response/no-response and into cycles of model-driven optimization.
## Preliminary Data
Our preliminary data strongly support the feasibility of the proposed research. We have previously demonstrated the synergistic effect of combining an anti-TREM2 antibody with cytokines, observing potent pro-inflammatory macrophage activation in vitro. These findings are supported by our work using ZMAN-seq, which revealed that TREM2 blockade redirects the monocyte-to-macrophage trajectory, leading to a pro-inflammatory phenotype. Additionally, we have successfully produced and tested preliminary TREM2 immunocytokine molecules, including an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein, and validated a masking strategy for the cytokine arm using TAM-specific protease-responsive blocking moieties in vivo. These pilot studies provide solid supporting evidence that we can modulate the tumor microenvironment (TME) using immunocytokines and masking technologies. Specifically, our in vivo data with aTREM2 antibody showed downregulation of immunosuppressive genes such as Arg1, Pirb, Il18bp, Vegfa and Cd274, and upregulation of pro-inflammatory genes (Ccl3, Ccl4, Cd81 and Cd83). Moreover, in vitro studies of bone-marrow-derived macrophages treated with aTREM2 antibody showed increased expression of Sat1, indicating a metabolic switch that may be a direct consequence of TREM2 blockade. These results demonstrate the feasibility of our approach to modulate myeloid cell populations, which will be further investigated in this project using single-cell technologies (ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, Stereo-seq) to analyze the BM TME and design novel immunocytokines.
## Research Plan

### Research Objectives

#### Objective 1: Developing BM TME models with holistic, multimodal AI-driven analysis

This research objective aims to elucidate the complex dynamics of the brain metastasis (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME) using advanced single-cell technologies and artificial intelligence (AI)-driven analysis. The central hypothesis is that a comprehensive, multi-modal characterization of the BM TME will reveal critical targets for immunomodulatory interventions, specifically antibodies and cytokines. This objective is structured into three interconnected tasks. Task 1, "Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME," will employ Zman-seq, a technology that integrates fluorescent temporal tracking with single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq), to map the dynamic changes in immune cell states over time. This task will focus on identifying the trajectories of immune cell dysfunction within the BM TME, specifically looking at transitions of NK cells from cytotoxic to impaired states and monocytes into immunosuppressive tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs). The experimental setup includes using orthotopic immunocompetent murine models for melanoma BMs. Task 2, "Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME," will utilize PIC-seq to analyze cell-cell communication networks, identifying key ligand-receptor interactions that regulate immune responses within the BM TME. This approach is designed to pinpoint crucial signaling pathways and potential targets for therapeutic modulation. Task 3, "Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME," will employ Stereo-seq to spatially map immune and tumor cells and their interactions, creating a comprehensive spatial atlas of the BM TME and identifying the spatial context of immune cell dysfunction. By combining temporal, interactional, and spatial information, we aim to go beyond current static TME characterizations. These tasks are expected to produce single-cell atlases of human and murine BMs and integrated tissue models for understanding their physiology and drug response. The expected outcomes are specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) and directly related to validating the objective hypothesis. Data from these single-cell atlases will be used to identify shared processes and pathways between human and murine models. The outputs from this objective will inform the selection of cytokines for further screening in objective 2 and guide the design of immunocytokines in objective 3. The success of this objective is dependent on the successful establishment of the murine models for melanoma BMs and the collection of a large cohort of patient clinical samples. The single-cell sequencing data will be further processed with AI-based analysis to identify potentially effective cytokines that convert the BM TME from immunologically 'cold' to 'hot'.

##### Preliminary Results

Our preliminary data, derived from prior studies, strongly supports the feasibility of the proposed research. We have demonstrated that an anti-TREM2 antibody, when combined with specific cytokines, induces a robust pro-inflammatory macrophage response in vitro, suggesting a viable approach for modulating the tumor microenvironment (TME). Using Zman-seq, we previously characterized the dynamic changes in the glioblastoma (GBM) TME, observing that within 24 hours of tumor infiltration, cytotoxic NK cells transitioned to a dysfunctional state regulated by Tgfbl activation, while infiltrating monocytes differentiated into immunosuppressive tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) over 36 to 48 hours. Specifically, we found that monocyte differentiation into TAMs was associated with increased Trem2 expression, a key immunosuppressive signaling hub. We also showed that blocking TREM2 signaling using an antagonistic antibody redirects the monocyte-to-macrophage trajectory from immunosuppressive to pro-inflammatory macrophages. This shift was characterized by the downregulation of immunosuppressive genes (e.g., Arg1, Pirb, Il18bp, Vegfa, Cd274) and upregulation of pro-inflammatory genes (e.g., Ccl3, Ccl4, Cd81, Cd83). Furthermore, temporal analysis of ligand-receptor interactions revealed CCL3 and CCL4 as critical signaling hubs mediating TME reprogramming upon TREM2 blockade. We observed that TREM2 inhibition increased CCL3 expression, while decreasing its cognate receptor CCR5 on TAMs, suggesting that TREM2 blockade enhances and channels CCL3 signaling to promote inflammation. These findings validate the use of Zman-seq to map immune cell trajectories and identify key signaling pathways involved in myeloid reprogramming in the TME. Additionally, we have successfully generated preliminary TREM2 immunocytokine models, including an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein, and developed TAM-specific protease-responsive masking strategies, demonstrating our capability to design and test targeted immunotherapeutic molecules. These preliminary results directly support the proposed research objectives by validating the temporal analysis of immune cell dynamics using Zman-seq, characterizing key molecules involved in immune suppression, and the possibility of converting immunosuppressive TME to pro-inflammatory TME, and also by establishing proof-of-concept data for the design of immunocytokines. Moreover, the identified pathways, like the TREM2-CCL3 axis, provide specific targets for therapeutic intervention, guiding the selection of cytokines for further screening in objective 2 and the design of immunocytokines in objective 3. The Zman-seq data also highlights the importance of spatial-temporal relationships between different immune cell types in the TME which directly supports the rationale for Tasks 2 and 3 in the proposed objective, which will utilize PIC-seq and Stereo-seq technologies, respectively. **MISSING INFORMATION:** Statistical significance for differential gene expression upon aTREM2 treatment.**MISSING INFORMATION:** Details of the in vitro system for bone-marrow-derived monocyte diversification towards M2 macrophage states.

##### Research Tasks

###### Task 1.1: Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME

This research task will employ Zman-seq, a technology combining fluorescent temporal tracking of cells with scRNA-seq, to investigate immune cell dynamics within the bone marrow (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME). This will involve adapting the Zman-seq methodology, previously established in a glioma model, to the BM context. The experimental approach includes in vivo labeling of circulating immune cells with a temporal sequence of fluorophores. These labeled cells will then be tracked as they infiltrate the BM TME. Immunophenotyping will be performed using fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS), with index-sorting used to capture the fluorescent time stamps on individual cells, enabling inference of tumor exposure time. Single-cell transcriptomic profiling will be performed in conjunction with the tumor exposure time measurements to generate temporal maps of the BM TME. Intravascular injections of fluorescent anti-CD45 antibodies will be used to label peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs), with antibody concentrations ranging from 20 to 200 μg/kg body weight (bw) to achieve complete labeling. The kinetics of unbound antibody in the circulation will be assessed to validate that labeling is specific to circulating cells and not tumor-resident immune cells. Histological analysis will be performed on BM tumors following injection of fluorescent anti-CD45 antibodies at different time points before harvest. The temporal data from Zman-seq will be used to reconstruct the progression of immune cell states and to identify time-dependent gene modules and transcription factor (TF) circuits driving these trajectories. Receptor-ligand interaction events will be analyzed to understand tumor immune evasion mechanisms. These analyses will utilize cumulative distribution functions (CDF) of cell frequencies in each time bin to define continuous tumor exposure time (cTET) for each metacell. The study will establish a temporal baseline of immune activity in the BM TME, which will be a prerequisite to understanding cell-cell interaction. This data will provide insight into the temporal dynamics of immune cell infiltration and functional changes within the BM TME. **MISSING INFORMATION: Specific murine model to be used in this task is required.**

###### Task 1.2: Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME

Task 2 will employ PIC-seq to dissect immune cell interactions within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME). PIC-seq, which combines cell sorting of physically interacting cells with scRNA-seq, allows for the analysis of cell-cell interactions and their impact on gene expression. The methodology involves a mild tissue dissociation protocol to preserve in situ cellular structures, followed by fluorescence sorting using mutually exclusive cell type markers to isolate putative PICs and single cells of the same type. Massively parallel scRNA-seq will be used to generate gene expression profiles for the PICs. These profiles will then be computationally deconvolved to infer transcriptional states by contrasting PIC profiles with those of non-interacting single cells. This approach will enable the characterization of cellular crosstalk within the BM TME. The analysis will focus on immune cell types known to interact within the TME, and will identify co-stimulatory genes induced in antigen-presenting PICs. Computational deconvolution requires that the transcriptional profiles of the interacting cells be sufficiently distant. This will be considered when choosing cell type markers. The data analysis will identify cell-cell interactions relevant to TME function, and link these interactions to specific gene regulatory programs. The analysis of gene expression profiles and inferred interaction networks will be performed with the SCANPY framework. The analysis will be integrated with the temporal information generated in Task 1.1 as required by the dependencies of Task 1.2.

###### Task 1.3: Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME

This task will employ Stereo-seq to investigate the spatial distribution of immune cells within the bone marrow (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME). Stereo-seq, a spatial transcriptomics technology, uses DNA nanoball-patterned arrays for in situ RNA capture, enabling large field-of-view analysis at cellular resolution. This approach allows for the characterization of the transcriptomic organization and cell-type composition of complex tissues, which is essential for understanding the spatial heterogeneity of the BM TME. The methodology involves loading 10 um frozen tissue sections onto Stereo-seq chips, followed by fixation, permeabilization, and in situ RNA capture. The captured RNA will undergo reverse transcription and amplification, with the resulting cDNA used for library preparation and sequencing. Computational analysis of sequencing data will generate high-resolution, spatially resolved transcriptomic profiles. A spatially constrained clustering algorithm (SCC), optimized for large, complex tissues, will be used to analyze the Stereo-seq data and identify tissue domains. Image-based cell segmentation, using nucleic acid staining, will be performed to integrate transcriptomic data with cellular morphology. The resulting data will be used to analyze the spatial distribution of immune cell types within the BM TME. The spatial information provided by this task complements data generated in tasks 1.1 and 1.2, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the BM TME. This task depends on data generated in task 1.3. Integration of scATAC-seq data is also mentioned as a possible validation step. The high resolution of Stereo-seq enables image-based cell segmentation; however, it is noted that smaller cell types such as immune cells may be challenging to segment perfectly. Furthermore, some low expression genes may be missed due to capture limitations, which will be mitigated by the large field of view and number of cells profiled. **MISSING INFORMATION: Specific sample size and number of replicates need to be defined. ##### Risks and Alternatives

A primary risk is the potential failure to establish robust orthotopic murine models for melanoma BMs, which are crucial for Tasks 1, 2, and 3. This would severely impact the project timeline and the ability to generate single-cell atlases. Mitigation strategies include: 1) utilizing alternative, well-established melanoma BM models, if the primary model fails to develop appropriately, 2) increasing the number of animals per group to ensure sufficient sample sizes are achieved, 3) performing regular model validation through histological and molecular characterization. Another risk is the limited availability of human BM samples, which could hinder the validation of murine findings. To mitigate this, we will: 1) expand collaborations with clinical partners to increase sample availability, and 2) prioritize the samples to maximize the information obtained from each specimen. Technical challenges with Zman-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq, such as low signal-to-noise ratios or difficulties in data processing, pose a risk. Alternative approaches include: 1) optimizing experimental protocols, 2) employing computational methods to address limitations in data analysis. The AI-driven analysis of single-cell data might be affected by the complexity of the datasets. We will mitigate this by: 1) developing a robust, well-defined analysis pipeline, 2) incorporating external data to train the machine-learning models. A major dependency is the successful implementation of the AI-based analysis pipeline, which will be developed by the Amit lab, if the lab faces personnel or resource issues, this could cause considerable delays. Alternate plans involve securing collaboration with external experts or cloud based services to perform analysis. The failure to identify effective cytokines to convert the BM TME from 'cold' to 'hot' would affect downstream objectives. Alternative cytokines will be identified from literature and explored in Objective 2.

#### Objective 2: Preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models

This objective aims to preclinically screen cytokines identified in Aim 1, using orthotopic immunocompetent brain metastasis (BM) models, to modulate the tumor microenvironment (TME) from an immunosuppressive to an anti-tumor state. The working hypothesis is that select cytokines, identified through single-cell analysis in Aim 1, can synergize with anti-TREM2 mAb to enhance anti-tumor immune responses in the BM TME. This will be achieved through three interconnected tasks. Task 2.1 involves *Screening of cytokines in BM TME* using in vitro assays of co-cultured melanoma cell line-derived spheroids and immune cells, testing various cytokine combinations with and without TREM2 blockade. Cytokine-secreting clones of brain-tropic melanoma cell lines will be created via lentiviral transduction to conditionally express a panel of immune-modulating cytokines (identified in Aim 1), and tumor spheroids will be generated containing one or multiple cytokine cassettes. These will be co-cultured with immune cells and stimulated with doxycycline to induce cytokine expression and will assess for immune cell activity through flow cytometry, ELISA and qPCR. Task 2.2 is focused on *In-vitro validation of cytokines* where the most effective cytokine combinations identified in Task 2.1 will be validated in additional in vitro assays, focusing on T and NK cell cytotoxicity, quantified by tumor cell death using FACS. Task 2.3 focuses on *In-vivo validation of cytokines*. The B16 melanoma cell line clones expressing the validated cytokines, will be used to generate melanoma BMs through intracardial injections into mice. Cytokine expression will be induced by doxycycline, and tumor response will be assessed via MRI and survival measurements. Single cell RNA sequencing will be performed to dissect the in-vivo TME remodeling by cytokine treatment. These data will be essential for identifying the 3-5 most promising cytokines with the highest modulatory function in combination with anti-TREM2 mAb. Methodologies include lentiviral transduction, in vitro co-culture assays with melanoma cell lines and immune cells, flow cytometry, ELISA, qPCR, in vivo tumor models, MRI and survival studies. This work is dependent on Task 1.3 and Task 1.5 for identification of the optimal cytokines for screening, and the results will inform the selection of cytokines for immunocytokine design in Aim 3.

##### Risks and Alternatives

A primary risk is the potential failure of lentiviral transduction to generate stable cytokine-secreting melanoma cell lines, which would halt in vitro and in vivo experiments (Tasks 2.1 and 2.3). Mitigation involves utilizing multiple lentiviral constructs and optimizing transduction protocols; alternative approaches include using CRISPR-mediated knock-in strategies if transduction fails. Another risk is the variability in in vitro co-culture assays (Task 2.1), potentially leading to inconsistent data; mitigation includes rigorous standardization of co-culture conditions, utilizing positive and negative controls and increasing replicate numbers. A major challenge is the potential for poor engraftment or variable tumor growth in the orthotopic BM models, affecting in vivo cytokine validation (Task 2.3). Mitigation involves optimizing injection techniques and using mice with consistent immune backgrounds. If B16 cell engraftment is problematic, alternative brain-tropic melanoma cell lines or patient-derived xenografts will be tested. Moreover, variability in doxycycline-induced cytokine expression in vivo (Task 2.3) may lead to inconsistent responses; mitigation includes optimizing doxycycline dosage and delivery methods. Finally, the lack of overlap between murine and human cytokine activity is a concern, requiring careful interpretation of data and potentially cross-species validation experiments. This is mitigated by focusing on cytokines with conserved functions and testing the most promising cytokines in humanized mouse models in later stages. The success of Task 2 is critically dependent on the outputs from Task 1.3 and 1.5, and delays in Task 1.3 or Task 1.5 will directly affect the timeline of Task 2; therefore, close communication between the teams will be critical for on time completion of the project.

#### Objective 3: Design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines

This objective focuses on the design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines by incorporating potent cytokines, identified in Aim 2, that can reactivate the immune compartment and eradicate melanoma brain metastases (BMs). The core hypothesis is that targeted delivery of immunomodulatory cytokines to the tumor microenvironment (TME) via antibody-cytokine fusion proteins will elicit enhanced anti-tumor responses compared to systemic cytokine administration. This will be achieved by targeting species-conserved tumor antigens highly expressed in melanoma BMs. The work will leverage advanced technologies developed in previous objectives to optimize constructs, moving beyond binary response models. The goal is to identify the most effective immunocytokine candidates for Investigational New Drug (IND) stage development. Task 2.1, 'Design fusion proteins and cleavage site', will utilize protein design techniques to generate optimal fusion protein structures using the top 3-5 mAb-cytokine combinations identified in WP1. This includes the selection of peptide linkers, blocking moieties, tumor-associated macrophage (TAM)-specific cleavage sites, and computational optimization for protein structure and stability. Task 2.2, 'Produce fusion proteins', will involve manufacturing these 3-5 mAb-cytokine fusion proteins through a contract research organization (CRO), focusing on stable molecules with high expression and minimal aggregation. Task 2.3, 'In-vitro validation of immunocytokines,' will confirm binding via Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR), Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay (ELISA), cell-based binding and reporter assays. Furthermore, task 2.4 will validate the impact of the designed immunocytokines on myeloid, NK and T cell activity using co-culture assays with human monocyte-derived macrophages (huMDMs), NK and T cells, respectively. Data from Task 1.1 will serve as control. Lastly, Task 2.5 will assess the efficacy of fusion proteins in inducing cytotoxic NK and T cell activity using co-cultured huMDMs, NK or T cells and tumor cells. The data from Task 1.2 will be used as control. These experiments will measure NK and T cell activity and cytotoxicity levels as described in task 1.2. These steps will lead to the selection of potent, tumor-specific immunocytokine candidates for further in vivo validation. This objective depends on the cytokine selection from Aim 2 and builds on the single-cell analysis and in vitro screening from Aim 1.

##### Risks and Alternatives

Several risks are associated with the design and validation of tumor-targeting immunocytokines. A primary risk lies in the potential for off-target toxicity due to the chosen antibody targeting species-conserved tumor antigens also expressed on normal cells, which may lead to on-target off-tumor binding. Mitigation strategies include screening for tumor-specific antigens with minimal off-target expression and engineering the antibody Fc region to minimize effector functions, such as ADCC, using mutations like L234A/L235A or N297A. Another risk involves the 'cytokine sink' effect, where immunocytokines are trapped by cognate receptors in circulation, reducing tumor accumulation. This will be mitigated by using cytokine mutants with reduced receptor affinity, or by employing prodrug strategies that allow for conditional activation within the TME, utilizing tumor-specific proteases for activation. Furthermore, there's a risk that the selected mAb-cytokine combination may not exhibit superior antitumor activity or may induce unexpected side effects. This will be addressed by thorough in vitro validation of the top 3-5 combinations, including SPR, ELISA, and cell-based assays, and by utilizing co-culture assays with huMDMs, NK, and T cells to assess the impact of the designed immunocytokines on immune cell activity. Additionally, difficulties in producing stable fusion proteins with high expression and minimal aggregation may arise; this will be mitigated by contracting a CRO specialized in protein manufacturing. The in vitro assays may not fully predict in vivo efficacy; further in vivo validation studies may be needed. ## Experimental Design and Methods
The experimental design employs a multi-faceted approach integrating advanced single-cell technologies, in vitro and in vivo models, and AI-driven data analysis to dissect the melanoma brain metastasis (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME) and develop targeted immunocytokines. For Aim 1, we will establish comprehensive single-cell atlases of the BM TME using ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq. ZMAN-seq will track immune cell state transitions over time by introducing fluorescent antibody time stamps, enabling us to map cell trajectories in the TME and measure the exposure time of infiltrating cells to the tumor. This involves sequential in vivo injections of fluorescently labeled anti-CD45 antibodies to trace cellular and molecular kinetics of leukocytes. PIC-seq will be employed to resolve cell-cell interactions by capturing paired transcriptomes of interacting cells. This will reveal the specific ligand-receptor interactions driving immune suppression. Stereo-seq will provide spatial transcriptomic information, enabling us to map the spatial organization of different cell types within the TME and how this organization is altered upon therapeutic intervention. Data generated from these technologies will be integrated and analyzed using advanced AI algorithms to identify key pathways and cytokines driving anti-tumor activity. Controls will include isotype antibody controls, untreated cells, and previously characterized pro and anti-inflammatory controls. For Aim 2, we will screen a panel of cytokines, selected based on single-cell analysis from aim 1, in vitro using an established bone marrow derived monocyte (BMDM) diversification system. This system includes control cultures of TREM2-/- BMDMs, that do not differentiate toward the M2 macrophage state. These studies will be complemented by in vivo screening using orthotopic immunocompetent BM models. Cytokines will be overexpressed using inducible, barcoded tumor cell lines and screened in combination with our anti-TREM2 mAbs. Single-cell analysis using ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq and Stereo-seq will determine the impact on the TME, identifying 3-5 cytokines for further development. Controls will include tumor cells without cytokine overexpression, cells treated with isotype control, and previously characterized pro- and anti-inflammatory cytokines. For Aim 3, selected cytokines will be incorporated into antibody-cytokine fusion proteins, targeting melanoma BM-specific antigens. Masking strategies will be applied to increase the specificity of the immunocytokine to the BM TME. We will utilize TAM-specific protease-responsive blocking moieties, validated in our preliminary data, to ensure that the cytokine is only activated in the tumor microenvironment. In vivo validation will be conducted using orthotopic melanoma BM models, assessing the efficacy of the immunocytokines and the masking approach in modulating immune responses. Data management will include standardized protocols for data collection, annotation, storage, and analysis, ensuring reproducibility and scientific rigor. This methodology is built on our expertise in single-cell technologies, cancer immunology, and advanced data analysis. The use of in vitro and in vivo assays ensures both reproducibility and validation of results.
## Statistical Plan
The statistical analysis will be performed using biostatistics methods appropriate for single-cell RNA-seq, spatial transcriptomics, and cell-cell interaction data. For sample size calculations, we will aim to collect data from at least 5 biological replicates per group for in vivo experiments, and 3 replicates per group for in vitro experiments, based on previous studies and power calculations to detect a statistically significant difference with a power of 0.8 and alpha of 0.05. For single-cell RNA-seq data, we will use DESeq2 for differential gene expression analysis, with moderated estimation of fold change and dispersion. We will implement Scanpy for large-scale data analysis, including dimensionality reduction (e.g., PCA, UMAP), clustering, and cell type identification. For cell trajectory analysis using ZMAN-seq data, we will employ RNA velocity methods to reconstruct differentiation flows and cell state transitions, comparing tumor exposed versus non-exposed cells. For cell-cell interaction analysis using PIC-seq, we will use NicheNet to model ligand-receptor interactions. The analysis will include identifying significant changes in cell communication networks. Spatial transcriptomics data from Stereo-seq will be analyzed using Giotto, a tool for integrative analysis and visualization of spatial expression data, enabling mapping of cell types within the tumor microenvironment. For AI analysis, we will use deep generative models to quantify sample-level heterogeneity in single-cell omics data. Statistical tests will include t-tests or ANOVA for continuous data and chi-squared tests for categorical data, with adjustments for multiple comparisons (e.g., Benjamini-Hochberg correction) when necessary. Data interpretation will focus on identifying key cell populations, pathways, and cytokines associated with anti-tumor activity. We will also validate our findings using orthogonal methods. To ensure reproducibility, all statistical methods, parameters, and code will be documented in our data management plan, following FAIR data principles, and will be made available upon request.
