# Project Summary
## General Audience Abstract
This research project focuses on improving the treatment of melanoma that has spread to the brain, a condition with poor survival rates. We are employing advanced single-cell technologies and artificial intelligence to understand the complex environment of these brain tumors. Our goal is to identify new targets for therapy and to design immunotherapies that can effectively activate the body's immune system to fight the cancer. This includes developing a novel antibody that blocks immune-suppressing cells in the tumor and combining it with immune-stimulating molecules to enhance treatment efficacy. This research has the potential to significantly extend life expectancy and improve the quality of life for patients with metastatic melanoma.
## Technical Abstract
Brain metastases (BMs) in melanoma are associated with poor prognosis due to an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). We propose to leverage advanced single-cell technologies, including ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq, combined with AI analysis, to dissect the BM TME and identify cytokines capable of remodeling it towards anti-tumor activity. This will enable the design of novel immunocytokines combining selected cytokines with our anti-TREM2 antibody, modulating both myeloid and T/NK cell compartments. Our specific aims include: 1) Developing BM TME models using AI-driven analysis of single-cell data to identify cytokines with high anti-tumor potential. 2) Preclinical screening of these cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models. 3) Designing tumor-targeting immunocytokines incorporating potent cytokines to reactivate the immune compartment and eradicate melanoma BMs. This approach will be enhanced by a masking strategy to increase the molecule's specificity to the BM TME. The expected outcomes include the identification of novel immunocytokines and biomarkers for targeted melanoma BM therapy, leading to significant clinical impact by increasing life expectancy and quality for metastatic melanoma patients. Our translational research has the potential to overcome current limitations in melanoma BM treatment.

# Project Description
## Background and Specific Aims
Brain metastases (BMs) are a significant clinical challenge in melanoma, occurring in nearly 50% of patients with metastatic disease and resulting in poor overall survival. While immunotherapy has revolutionized melanoma treatment, many patients experience relapse or do not respond, particularly within the immunosuppressive brain tumor microenvironment (TME). Single-cell technologies, coupled with machine learning, offer powerful tools to dissect complex cellular interactions within the TME, enabling data-driven design of immunotherapies. Our prior research identified regulatory (TREM2+) macrophages as a critical component of the immunosuppressive TME in melanoma BMs, and we have developed an antibody that blocks their suppressive action. Our central hypothesis is that applying advanced single-cell technologies-including cell temporal tracking (ZMAN-seq), cell-cell interaction analysis (PIC-seq), and spatial transcriptomics with Stereo-seq, coupled with AI-will yield a deep understanding of the BM TME and identify cytokines capable of remodeling it towards anti-tumor activity. This understanding will inform the design of novel immunocytokines combining promising cytokines with our anti-TREM2 antibody to modulate myeloid, T, and NK cell compartments. We will also leverage this single-cell and AI analysis to identify biomarkers for designing masking strategies, increasing immunocytokine specificity to the BM TME. Our rationale is that the use of advanced single cell technologies enables the identification of novel targets for activating the immune system to eliminate BMs. Our rationale is also that simultaneous modulation of macrophages, T cells and NK cells is a promising approach for inducing unprecedented anti-tumor immune responses. We will use our advanced technologies to systematically analyze the BM TME and identify novel immunocytokines capable of eliminating melanoma BMs.
## Preliminary Data
Our preliminary data demonstrates the feasibility of our approach. We have shown that an anti-TREM2 antibody combined with cytokines induces strong pro-inflammatory macrophage activity in vitro, providing supporting evidence for our hypothesis. Furthermore, we have successfully produced TREM2 immunocytokines, including a preliminary IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein model molecule, demonstrating the feasibility of our therapeutic approach. Additionally, we designed and tested a masking strategy using TAM-specific protease-responsive blocking moieties in vivo, offering validation for our targeting approach. Our methodology involved in vitro assays for macrophage activation and in vivo testing of masked immunocytokines. These key findings support our research plan, providing a strong foundation for further investigation into novel immunocytokine development and their modulation of the BM TME. We have also developed an in-vitro system for monocyte differentiation towards M2 suppressor macrophages, and have demonstrated that TREM2-/- monocytes do not differentiate towards the M2 state under cytokine induction, which validates our approach to combine anti-TREM2 antibodies with cytokines. Zman-seq analysis further demonstrates that anti-TREM2 treatment redirects monocyte-to-macrophage differentiation towards a pro-inflammatory state, confirming the efficacy of TREM2 targeting. Our pilot studies establish a robust platform for the proposed research.
## Research Plan

### Research Objectives

#### Objective 1: Developing BM TME models with holistic, multimodal AI-driven analysis

This objective aims to elucidate the complex dynamics of the brain metastases (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME) using advanced single-cell technologies and AI-driven analysis. The working hypothesis is that a comprehensive, multimodal understanding of the BM TME will reveal novel therapeutic targets for modulating immune activity towards anti-tumor responses. The primary goals are to create single-cell atlases of immune, stromal, and tumor cells in both murine models and human BM samples, identify shared pathways, and prioritize cytokines with high anti-tumor potential. Task 1, 'Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME', will employ Zman-seq to track transcriptomic changes in infiltrating immune cells over time, establishing differentiation trajectories and identifying time-dependent gene expression patterns. This method utilizes fluorescent antibody time stamps to quantify the duration of immune cell exposure to the tumor. Task 2, 'Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME', will use PIC-seq to define cell-cell interactions and spatial relationships within the BM TME, revealing intercellular communication networks. Task 3, 'Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME', will apply Stereo-seq to assess the spatial organization of immune cells and their surrounding environment, using high-resolution spatial transcriptomics. These methodologies will be implemented on immunocompetent murine BM models and human patient samples, generating a dataset which will be analyzed using advanced AI methods to identify shared processes and pathways. Zman-seq utilizes fluorescently labeled cells to track the timing of immune cell infiltration into the tumor and, combined with scRNA-seq, provides a temporal map of the TME, which can be correlated with the cell state. PIC-seq measures cell-cell interactions through antibody-oligonucleotide conjugates. Stereo-seq will map spatial gene expression. Expected outcomes include the identification of specific time-dependent gene modules, transcription factor circuits, and receptor-ligand interactions driving immune dysfunction, as well as spatial maps of cellular interactions within the TME. Successful completion of this objective will yield a prioritized list of cytokines with potentially high anti-tumor activity in melanoma BMs, informing the cytokine selection strategy in Objective 2. This objective relies on the availability of orthotopic immunocompetent murine models for melanoma BMs and a large cohort of patient clinical samples, and uses the resources developed in the mentioned groups, such as Zman-seq, PIC-seq, and AI analysis pipelines. The results of this objective will define the design parameters for the subsequent studies and cytokine based immunotherapies.

##### Research Tasks

###### Task 1.1: Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME

This research task will investigate temporal dynamics of immune cell activity within the bone marrow (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME) using Zman-seq, adapting methodologies previously applied in glioma studies. The core approach involves in vivo pulse-chase labeling of circulating immune cells with sequential fluorophores, followed by single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) and fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) to determine tumor exposure time. This adaptation of Zman-seq to the BM TME will require careful titration of fluorescent anti-CD45 antibodies to ensure comprehensive labeling of peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) without compromising cell viability or function. Antibody concentrations and labeling durations will be rigorously validated using flow cytometry. Index-sorting during FACS will be essential for capturing fluorescent stamps on individual cells to correlate transcriptomic profiles with tumor exposure time, enabling the generation of temporal maps. Computational analysis will involve assigning continuous tumor exposure time (cTET) values to metacells by calculating the area under the curve (AUC) of cumulative distribution functions based on the frequency of cells in different time bins, facilitating the mapping of cell state transitions across time. This approach will also allow to define cell differentiation trajectories, and temporal ligand-receptor interaction events. This will involve the study of cell populations like NK cells, monocytes and macrophages. Specifically, this work aims to reveal how immune cells transition from functional to dysfunctional states over time within the BM TME. These results will be compared to the previous results obtained in the glioma context. Downstream analysis will identify time-dependent gene modules and TF circuits orchestrating immune cell trajectories, as well as key time-dependent receptor-ligand interactions driving tumor immune evasion. This study will require flow cytometers, cell sorters, and high-throughput sequencing technologies. ###### Task 1.2: Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME

This research task will employ PIC-seq to dissect immune cell interactions within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME). PIC-seq, a methodology that combines cell sorting of physically interacting cells with scRNA-seq, will be utilized to profile cellular interactions and their impact on gene expression. The protocol involves a mild tissue dissociation to preserve in situ cellular structures, followed by fluorescence sorting using mutually exclusive cell type markers to isolate cell aggregates, representing putative physically interacting cells (PICs), and single cells of the same types. Massively parallel scRNA-seq will be performed on the isolated PICs, generating gene expression profiles. These profiles will be computationally deconvolved to infer the transcriptional states of interacting cells by contrasting them with those of non-interacting single cells. The deconvolution process relies on the transcriptional profiles of interacting cells being sufficiently distinct. The data generated from this task will be complementary to the temporal data derived from task 1.1 and the cell-cell interaction data from task 1.2, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the dynamics of the BM TME. The analysis will utilize the SAW pipeline for processing raw sequencing data and generating a CID-containing expression profile matrix. Additionally, computational tools such as SCANPY will be employed to analyze the single-cell gene expression data. Downstream analysis may use NicheNet for modeling intercellular communication by linking ligands to target genes. The specific cell markers for sorting and identifying cell populations within the BM TME need to be defined, and optimized. Further, the computational parameters for deconvolution should be determined based on the specific cell types and interaction dynamics under investigation. ###### Task 1.3: Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME

Task 3 will employ Stereo-seq to characterize the spatial distribution of immune cells within the bone marrow (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME). This method combines DNA nanoball-patterned arrays and in situ RNA capture, enabling large field-of-view spatial transcriptomics at cellular resolution. This approach facilitates the dissection of spatial cell-type heterogeneity, a key aspect of the BM TME. Stereo-seq uses lithographically etched chips with spots of approximately 220 nm diameter and a center-to-center distance of 500 or 715 nm, providing up to 400 spots for tissue RNA capture per 100 um². Random barcode-labeled DNBs achieve a large spatial barcode pool, with unique molecular identifiers (UMI) and polyT sequence-containing oligonucleotides ligated onto each spot. Frozen tissue sections will be loaded onto the chip surface, followed by fixation, permeabilization, and in situ RNA capture. Amplified barcoded cDNA will then be sequenced along with the coordinate identity (CID). Computational analysis of the sequencing data enables high-resolution spatially resolved transcriptomics. The spatial data from this task is complementary to the temporal and cell-cell interaction data obtained in tasks 1.1 and 1.2, providing a comprehensive view of the BM TME dynamics. Analysis will involve spatially constrained clustering (SCC) algorithms, optimized for complex tissue analysis, to identify tissue domains. Image-based cell segmentation will be performed using nucleic acid staining to generate an image-guided gene-by-cell matrix. The data will be aligned with single-cell transcriptomic data using Tangram for cell-type mapping. This will allow for the identification of functional anatomic regions and cell types within the BM TME. Validation of the Stereo-seq data will be done by comparison with in situ hybridization (ISH) data. Further analysis will include diffusion analysis of transcripts, such as nuclear localized long non-coding RNAs like Malat1 and Neat1, mitochondrial RNAs, and neuropeptide Vip transcripts.

##### Risks and Alternatives

The primary risks associated with this objective involve technical and logistical challenges. A key risk is the potential limitation in the number of fluorescent time-stamps achievable with Zman-seq due to spectral overlap, which could restrict the temporal resolution of immune cell tracking; mitigation strategies include optimizing fluorophore combinations and employing full spectrum analyzers to expand detectable fluorophores. Additionally, signal fading over time and potential cell shedding of CD45 could affect the accuracy of long-term measurements; this can be mitigated by focusing analysis on the first 2-3 days post-infiltration and employing alternative markers. The spatial resolution of Stereo-seq could also be a limiting factor; this can be addressed by integrating with scATAC-seq datasets to enhance the spatial measurements of chromatin accessibility, as well as using advanced image analysis for cell segmentation. Furthermore, the dependence on orthotopic immunocompetent murine models and a large cohort of patient clinical samples represents a logistical risk; mitigation involves close collaboration with the Weiss group and other hospital partners, along with contingency plans for sample acquisition. Finally, the complexity of AI analysis could pose challenges; this is addressed by leveraging existing analysis pipelines and developing novel algorithms, as well as benchmarking analysis methods. Alternative approaches include using established scRNA-seq methods as a validation set, if any of the techniques show to be problematic.

#### Objective 2: Preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models

This objective aims to preclinically screen cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent brain metastasis (BM) models, building upon the cytokine identification from Aim 1. The central hypothesis is that specific cytokines, identified through single-cell analysis of the BM tumor microenvironment (TME), can modulate immune activity to promote anti-tumor responses. The goal is to identify effective cytokine and anti-TREM2 mAb combinations for therapeutic intervention. Task 2.1, "Screening of cytokines in BM TME", involves the generation of cytokine-secreting clones from brain tropic melanoma cell lines using conditional (dox-induced) expression of a range of promising cytokines. These clones will form tumor spheroids, which will be co-cultured with immune cells, and various cytokine combinations will be tested through doxycycline induction. The most effective cytokines will then be moved forward to in-vivo testing. Task 2.2, "In-vitro validation of cytokines", will use the results of Task 2.1. The in-vitro assays will measure the impact of selected cytokines, alone and in combination with our E3C7 mAb, on co-cultured huMDMs, NK, and T cells using flow cytometry, ELISA, and qPCR to assess cell proliferation and cytokine secretion. These assays, also performed with and without the anti-TREM2 mAb, will be compared to the results from Task 1.1. Task 2.3, "In-vivo validation of cytokines," will use the top cytokines and combinations identified from tasks 2.1 and 2.2. B16 melanoma clones expressing these cytokines will be used to generate BMs via intracardial injection. Cytokine expression will be induced with doxycycline, and tumor response will be monitored via MRI and survival measurements. These in vivo experiments will leverage Zman-seq technology to analyze the single-cell transcriptomic dynamics over time in response to the cytokine treatment. The methodologies will also include single cell isolation and sorting for RNA sequencing, FACS sorting, and histological analysis. This objective will assess whether the selected cytokines can redirect the monocyte-to-macrophage trajectory towards pro-inflammatory TAMs, by analyzing the TME with scRNA-seq and evaluating the functional status of NK cells and T cells. The combination of in vitro and in vivo assays, with the single-cell omics analysis, is expected to result in a data-driven list of 3-5 cytokines with the most promising modulatory function in combination with our anti-TREM2 mAb. These results will directly validate the efficacy of the cytokines identified in Aim 1 and feed into the immunocytokine design in Aim 3.

##### Risks and Alternatives

A primary risk is the potential for off-target effects or systemic toxicity from the selected cytokines, particularly given their potent immunomodulatory activities. This will be mitigated by using inducible expression systems and by carefully titrating cytokine concentrations in both in vitro and in vivo assays. Another risk is the variability in cytokine responses between different cell types and in vivo environments. This will be complemented by in vitro assays using co-cultures of human myeloid, NK, and T cells to validate the identified cytokine effects. ## Experimental Design and Methods
This project employs a multi-faceted experimental design, integrating advanced single-cell technologies, in vivo modeling, and in vitro assays to dissect the brain metastasis (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME) and develop targeted immunotherapies. Our approach includes the use of orthotopic, immunocompetent murine models for melanoma BMs and patient clinical samples to generate single-cell atlases of immune, stromal, and tumor cells. These atlases will be constructed using a combination of ZMAN-seq for cell temporal tracking, PIC-seq for cell-cell interaction analysis, and Stereo-seq for spatial transcriptomics. Data will be analyzed using advanced AI algorithms, and integrated to create holistic models of tissue physiology and drug response prediction. In vivo experiments include the use of syngeneic melanoma BM tumor models where immune cell dynamics will be tracked using ZMAN-seq, with pulse labeling of immune cells using fluorescently labeled antibodies. This method allows us to measure the temporal dynamics of immune cell infiltration and their transition to dysfunctional states within the TME. Control groups will include mice without tumors, mice treated with isotype control antibody, and mice receiving single-agent cytokines. For validation, we will utilize TREM2 knock-out (KO) mice to confirm the role of TREM2 in macrophage polarization. In vitro assays will involve the co-culture of myeloid, T and NK cells with or without TREM2 blockade using our antibody, and will evaluate the effects of selected cytokines. We will apply our single cell analysis pipeline to select the most promising cytokines in combination with our anti-TREM2 mAb. The masking strategies will be validated using in vivo experiments, where the activity of masked immunocytokines will be compared with non-masked controls. Reproducibility will be ensured through standardized protocols, rigorous data analysis, and the use of appropriate controls. Data management will follow a strict plan for storage, access, and sharing, ensuring data integrity and availability for the team. The research methods have been tested and validated in our preliminary studies, and are robust to ensure the scientific rigor of the project.
## Statistical Plan
The statistical analysis will be performed using R and Python, leveraging packages such as DESeq2 for differential gene expression analysis, Scanpy for single-cell data analysis, and custom-developed scripts for spatial transcriptomics data integration and analysis. Sample size calculations for in vivo experiments will be determined based on power analyses using data from our preliminary studies, aiming for a power of 0.8 with an alpha of 0.05, to detect meaningful differences in immune cell infiltration and tumor growth. We will use a two-way ANOVA or mixed-effects model for group comparisons depending on whether we have repeated measures. Post-hoc tests will be adjusted for multiple comparisons using Bonferroni correction. For single-cell data, statistical tests will include Wilcoxon rank-sum tests and Kruskal-Wallis tests to identify significant differences in gene expression between cell populations across different treatment conditions. For trajectory and cell-cell interaction analyses, we will apply methods developed for temporal single-cell data, including generalized additive models (GAMs) and network analysis using methods like NicheNet. The data interpretation will be data-driven, focusing on biological relevance, including the identification of novel biomarkers and therapeutic targets. We will utilize machine learning techniques to identify patterns in the high-dimensional data.
