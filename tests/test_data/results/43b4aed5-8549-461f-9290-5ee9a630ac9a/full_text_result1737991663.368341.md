# Developing AI tailored immunocytokines to target melanoma brain metastases

## Project Summary

### Project Abstract

This project aims to revolutionize the treatment of melanoma brain metastases (BMs) by developing a novel immunotherapeutic approach. We will utilize advanced single-cell technologies, including ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq, coupled with generative AI to dissect the complex tumor microenvironment (TME) of BMs. Our analysis will focus on identifying key cytokines capable of reprogramming the TME towards anti-tumor activity. This will allow us to rationally design a novel immunocytokine combining our anti-TREM2 antibody with these cytokines to simultaneously modulate myeloid, T, and NK cell compartments. We will also employ masking strategies to enhance the specificity of the immunocytokine to the BM TME, thereby minimizing off-target effects. Our preliminary data demonstrates the synergistic potential of anti-TREM2 antibody with cytokines in inducing pro-inflammatory macrophage activity. We have also established the feasibility of producing TREM2 immunocytokines, and have designed a masking strategy for the cytokine arm. The expected outcome of this project is to develop a first-in-class therapeutic modality for melanoma BMs that could significantly improve patient survival and quality of life.

**General Audience Abstract:** Melanoma that spreads to the brain is a devastating disease with limited treatment options. This research project will use advanced technologies to study the cells within the brain tumors and develop a new approach to help the body's own immune system fight the cancer. We will focus on identifying specific signals that can turn off the suppression of immune cells by the tumor, and we will design a new medicine combining those signals with an antibody that blocks immunosuppressive cells. This will help the immune system find and kill the tumor cells. We also will add a "mask" to this medicine, so it will only activate in the brain tumors, avoiding side effects in other parts of the body. This research will pave the way for better treatments for patients with brain tumors caused by melanoma.**Technical Abstract:** This project leverages single-cell multi-omics and AI-driven analysis to characterize the immunosuppressive TME of melanoma BMs. We hypothesize that modulation of both the myeloid and T/NK cell compartments can be achieved through a novel immunocytokine. Specifically, the anti-TREM2 antibody will be fused to identified cytokines that induce an anti-tumor immune response and masked with a TAM-specific protease substrate to enhance BM TME specificity. Single-cell technologies, including ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq will be utilized to map the TME. Machine learning will be implemented to identify optimal cytokine combinations, and to design masking moieties. This project will result in a first-in-class immunotherapeutic molecule targeting the BM TME and a pipeline for identifying novel immunomodulatory targets.

## Narrative

### Research Plan

### Objective 1: Developing BM TME models with holistic, multimodal AI-driven analysis

Objective 1 will employ an innovative, multi-faceted approach integrating Zman-seq for temporal analysis of transcriptomic cell states, PIC-seq to map cell-cell interactions, and stereo-seq for high-resolution spatial transcriptomics within the BM TME. This integrated methodology, leveraging single-cell technologies, aims to provide a holistic understanding of the complex dynamics of the BM TME. The temporal resolution afforded by Zman-seq will delineate the evolution of immune cell subsets and their interactions, while PIC-seq will identify critical signaling pathways between immune and tumor cells. Stereo-seq will provide the necessary spatial context, revealing the organization of the TME and the localization of specific cell types and their interactions. This approach directly addresses the knowledge gap regarding the dynamic and spatial heterogeneity of the BM TME, as identified in the background context. The data obtained will be analyzed using AI-driven algorithms to identify novel therapeutic targets and predict the response to immunotherapeutic interventions. By prioritizing the analysis of immune cell subsets, including macrophages, T cells, and NK cells, and their interactions with tumor cells, we aim to uncover the mechanisms of immunosuppression within the BM TME. The expected results include a comprehensive spatial-temporal map of the BM TME, pinpointing critical immune dysfunction trajectories and cell-cell interaction networks. The results will validate the research methodology through a correlation of single-cell temporal data, cell-cell interaction maps, and spatial transcriptomic profiles, thus demonstrating the robustness and reliability of the analytical pipeline.

#### 1.1: Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME

Task 1.1 will adapt the `Zman-seq` methodology, previously applied in glioma models, to the `BM TME`. This involves sequential in vivo injections of fluorescently labeled anti-CD45 antibodies at 12-hour intervals, up to 60 hours prior to tissue harvest. This approach will label circulating leukocytes and allow tracking of their infiltration into the BM. The cells will be sorted using FACS, and their fluorescent profiles will be recorded using index-sorting to determine the tumor exposure time. Single-cell RNA sequencing will then be performed, providing transcriptomic data for each cell, linked to its specific exposure time. This adaptation allows us to resolve temporal dynamics of `immune activity` in the `BM TME`, an extension of the work in the tumor context. We will analyze the changes in immune cell states, including macrophages, T cells, and NK cells, at 12, 24, 36, 48 and 60-hour time points, identifying key transition points and trajectories. This will provide a temporal map of `immune activity`, focusing on cell states. Data analysis will be performed by correlating temporal transcriptomic data with known `immune activity` signatures to determine the impact of the `BM TME` on `immune` cell function. This task also serves to test the robustness of the `Zman-seq` technology in the `BM TME` context. Results from this task will be used to contextualize the cell-cell interaction data obtained in Task 1.2. Also, results in Task 1.2 can also inform the optimal time points for the temporal analysis in this task.

#### 1.2: Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME

Task 1.2 will employ PIC-seq to dissect cell-cell interactions within the BM TME, focusing on interactions between immune cell subsets (macrophages, T cells, and NK cells) and tumor cells. This approach involves physically sorting interacting cells followed by scRNA-seq to profile their transcriptomes. Data analysis will focus on identifying interacting cell pairs and characterizing the signaling pathways involved. PIC-seq data will be integrated with the temporal data from Task 1.1, allowing for the identification of cell-cell interactions at specific timepoints during tumor development and response to therapy. This will enable us to pinpoint key interacting partners and understand their functional contributions to the TME, providing critical insights into the immunosuppressive mechanisms at play. The results will generate detailed cell-cell interaction maps, revealing the dynamic interplay of immune and tumor cells within the BM TME.

#### 1.3: Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME

Task 1.3 will employ stereo-seq to map the spatial distribution of immune cells within the BM TME at high resolution. This will involve analyzing tissue sections from murine BM TME models, prioritizing regions with high immune cell infiltration, including areas adjacent to tumor cells and those with high macrophage presence, as identified from the results of Task 1.1 and 1.2. The stereo-seq method will capture transcriptomic data with spatial coordinates, enabling the reconstruction of the cellular architecture of the BM TME. The spatial transcriptomics data will then be integrated with the single-cell temporal and cell-cell interaction data from Tasks 1.1 and 1.2, respectively, to create a comprehensive spatiotemporal model. We will use spatially constrained-clustering (SCC) algorithm optimized for analysis of large complex tissues. This combined analysis will identify spatial niches and cell distributions associated with specific immune cell states and interactions. This will be used to define the spatial context of immunosuppression and to identify specific targets for immunomodulation. The anticipated results include high-resolution spatial maps of immune cell subtypes within the BM TME, revealing specific microenvironments characterized by unique immune cell compositions and transcriptional states, and providing novel targets for therapeutic intervention.

### Objective 2: Preclinical screening of cytokines in orthotopic immunocompetent BM models

Objective 2 will conduct a preclinical screening of candidate cytokines, identified through literature review and preliminary data, using both in-vitro and in-vivo models. This objective aims to identify novel immunomodulatory targets within the BM TME, directly addressing the hypothesis that specific cytokines can remodel the TME towards anti-tumor activity. The experimental design involves generating cytokine-secreting clones of brain-tropic melanoma cell lines, which will be used to create tumor spheroids containing single or multiple cytokine cassettes. These spheroids will be co-cultured with immune cells, and the impact of various cytokine combinations on myeloid, NK, and T cell activity will be evaluated. Specifically, the in-vitro validation will use doxycycline induction of cytokine expression, allowing for precise control of cytokine combinations. The most effective cytokines and cytokine combinations, as determined by their impact on immune cell activation and tumor cell killing, will progress to in-vivo testing. For in-vivo validation, B16 melanoma cells expressing the shortlisted cytokines will be injected intracardially into immunocompetent mice to establish orthotopic BM models. Cytokine expression will be activated by doxycycline, and the tumor response will be monitored using MRI and survival measurements. This approach allows for a direct evaluation of the therapeutic potential of identified cytokines within the complex BM TME. The data generated will not only identify the most promising cytokines for use in the immunocytokine design in Objective 3 but will also serve to refine the experimental design in Objective 1, by potentially indicating time points for optimal analysis of immune response. This preclinical screening methodology will provide a robust framework for evaluating the immunomodulatory potential of various cytokines and their impact on the BM TME.

#### 2.1: Screening of cytokines in BM TME

Task 2.1 will execute a cytokine screening using our in-house cytokine library to identify molecules capable of modulating immune activity within the BM TME. This library encompasses a diverse range of cytokines, including interleukins, interferons, and chemokines. The initial screen will assess each cytokine's impact on immune cell subsets, specifically macrophages, T cells, and NK cells, focusing on changes in activation markers, cytokine production, and cytotoxic potential. Modulation of immune activity will be defined by significant changes in these parameters compared to baseline controls. The experimental design will involve co-culturing BM TME cell models with individual cytokines and measuring the cellular response using flow cytometry and ELISA. Cytokine selection will be based on their ability to elicit a strong immunomodulatory effect. The expected outcome is a ranked list of cytokines, with an emphasis on those exhibiting the greatest potential for enhancing anti-tumor immune activity. This `cytokine screening` is a critical step, as it will identify lead candidates for further in-vitro validation in Task 2.2, and later for in-vivo validation in Task 2.3. Specifically, this initial screening will provide an assessment of the range and diversity of our `in-house cytokine library` and establishes the criteria used to define 'modulation of immune activity' within the context of the `BM TME`, paving the way for the downstream in-vitro validation in Task 2.2.

#### 2.2: In-vitro validation of cytokines

Task 2.2 will focus on the in-vitro validation of the cytokines selected in Task 2.1. This will be achieved by utilizing an in-vitro co-culture system of tumor spheroids and immune cells (myeloid cells, NK cells, and T cells). The tumor spheroids will express single or multiple cytokines under doxycycline induction to allow for precise control of cytokine combinations. The impact of different cytokine combinations on immune cell activity will be measured by quantifying proliferation using flow cytometry, and cytokine secretion using ELISA and qPCR assays, thus providing a comprehensive analysis of the immune response. The validation criteria will primarily focus on assessing the capacity of the cytokines to enhance myeloid, NK, and T cell activation and cytotoxicity, and to induce changes in immune cell states that are associated with anti-tumor activity. This in-vitro validation is essential for selecting the most promising cytokines and cytokine combinations that will proceed to in-vivo testing in Task 2.3. This step also contributes to the overall experimental design by optimizing the selection of cytokines and their combinations, ensuring that the subsequent in-vivo studies are well-informed and efficient.

#### 2.3: In-vivo validation of cytokines

Task 2.3 will focus on in-vivo validation of the most promising cytokine candidates selected from Tasks 2.1 and 2.2, using orthotopic immunocompetent murine BM models. This task will employ B16 melanoma cells engineered to express the shortlisted cytokines or cytokine combinations. These cells will be injected intracardially into mice, and cytokine expression will be induced by doxycycline administration. Tumor response will be assessed through MRI and survival analysis, providing a robust measure of therapeutic efficacy. To assess the impact of the cytokines on the immune landscape, we will employ single-cell analysis on the BM tissue. This will allow us to evaluate changes in myeloid, NK, and T cell activity, including alterations in cell states, activation markers, and effector functions. The in-vivo validation also includes the use of the functional screening system described in Tasks 1.4 and 1.5 to evaluate the effects of the cytokines and their combinations in vivo and identify trans-acting MiTEs. This is crucial to refine the immunocytokine design in Objective 3 by providing empirical data on cytokine efficacy within the BM TME. The experimental design aims at identifying the optimal cytokine combination for immunocytokine design, focusing on efficacy and modulation of myeloid, NK and T cell activity within the BM TME.

### Objective 3: Design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines

Objective 3 focuses on the innovative design of immunocytokines, leveraging insights from Objectives 1 and 2 to create targeted cancer therapies. This objective will synthesize fusion proteins composed of the anti-TREM2 antibody, selected from our previous work, and the most effective cytokine candidates identified in Objective 2. The immunocytokine design will incorporate peptide linkers optimized for flexibility and stability, and blocking moieties to enhance tumor specificity. TAM-specific cleavage sites will be introduced to ensure conditional activation of the cytokine payload within the tumor microenvironment, addressing potential systemic toxicities. The design will incorporate data from Objective 1, particularly the spatial and temporal aspects of cell interactions, to optimize immunocytokine targeting and activity. The binding affinity of the designed immunocytokines will be assessed using SPR and ELISA. Functional activity will be evaluated through cell-based binding assays and reporter assays to confirm the specificity and potency of the fusion proteins. Data from Objective 2 will validate the efficacy of the immunocytokine in vitro, by confirming that the selected cytokine enhances immune cell activation and cytotoxicity. The anticipated impact of this objective is the creation of highly effective immunocytokines with enhanced tumor specificity, with the potential to significantly improve the treatment of melanoma BMs, addressing the rationale of modulating multiple immune cell compartments simultaneously. The selected anti-TREM2 antibody and cytokines were developed in our lab and have proven preclinically efficacy, ensuring that this is a feasible approach.

#### 3.1: Design fusion proteins and cleavage site

Task 3.1 will design optimal fusion protein structures using advanced protein design techniques, focusing on the top 3-5 mAb-cytokine combinations identified in Objective 2. The design will incorporate peptide linkers selected for optimal flexibility and stability, along with blocking moieties to enhance tumor specificity. TAM-specific cleavage sites will be engineered to ensure conditional activation of the cytokine payload within the tumor microenvironment, leveraging the spatial and temporal data from Objective 1. Computational optimization will be employed to ensure protein stability. This task will use the identified cytokines from Objective 2, with the aim to optimize the design of the fusion proteins, paving the way for production in Task 3.2. The selection of peptide linkers, blocking moieties, and TAM-specific cleavage sites will be based on the results from Objective 1 and 2, and will further be computationally optimized to improve stability and minimize off-target effects. The fusion protein design and cleavage sites will directly impact the in-vitro validation in Task 3.3.

#### 3.2: Produce fusion proteins

Task 3.2 will focus on the production of the designed fusion proteins, leveraging a contract research organization (CRO) for protein synthesis. The selection of the CRO will be based on our prior experience with leading providers, prioritizing quality and punctual delivery. The CRO will manufacture the 3-5 fusion proteins designed in Task 3.1. Key criteria for protein selection include high protein expression and stability, as well as the absence of aggregation. Quality control measures will be implemented throughout the protein synthesis process to ensure that the final product meets the stringent requirements for downstream validation in Task 3.3. This includes monitoring protein expression levels, aggregation propensity, and structural integrity. The use of a CRO with expertise in fusion protein production will ensure the generation of high-quality immunocytokines for subsequent validation.

#### 3.3: In-vitro validation of immunocytokines

Task 3.3 will focus on the in-vitro validation of the designed immunocytokines. Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR) will be used to measure the binding affinity of the fusion proteins to TREM2 and determine the impact of the fusion on binding kinetics. ELISA assays will be performed to confirm the binding specificity of the immunocytokines to TREM2, assessing both direct binding and blocking of TREM2 ligand binding. Furthermore, cell-based binding assays will be used to confirm the immunocytokine's binding to TREM2 expressing cells, evaluating the binding on the surface of relevant cells. To assess the functionality of the immunocytokines, reporter assays will be employed to measure the activation of immune cells via the cytokine component. These reporter assays will quantify the downstream signaling events following immunocytokine binding. Additionally, co-culture assays involving myeloid cells, lymphoid cells, and tumor cells will be performed to validate the impact of the immunocytokines on cell interactions. These co-culture systems will enable the assessment of how the immunocytokine influences cell activation, cytokine production, and cytotoxic responses. Results from these assays will be analyzed using multivariate statistical methods to identify the most effective fusion proteins and to correlate binding affinity and cellular response data. The goal of this task is to validate the functional activity of the fusion proteins produced in Task 3.2, demonstrating their binding and modulation of the immune cells.### Clinical Relevance and Impact
