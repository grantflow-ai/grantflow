# Project Summary
## General Audience Abstract
This research project aims to address the critical challenge of melanoma brain metastases, which currently have limited treatment options and poor patient outcomes. Using cutting-edge single-cell technologies and artificial intelligence, we will investigate the complex environment surrounding these tumors to identify new therapeutic targets. Specifically, we will focus on a subtype of immune cell that suppresses the body's natural ability to fight cancer. Our goal is to develop a novel immunotherapy that combines an antibody we've created with immune-boosting proteins to specifically attack these suppressive cells, promoting an anti-tumor response and extending the lives of patients. This project will not only advance our understanding of melanoma brain metastases but also provide a novel approach to treatment with the potential to significantly impact patient survival and quality of life.
## Technical Abstract
Brain metastases (BMs) in melanoma are a significant clinical challenge, with nearly 50% of patients developing them and experiencing poor survival outcomes. While immunotherapy has improved outcomes for some, many patients still do not respond or relapse. This is largely due to the highly immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) of BMs. Our research aims to overcome this challenge by using advanced single-cell technologies including ZMAN-seq for cell temporal tracking, PIC-seq for cell-cell interactions, and Stereo-seq for spatial transcriptomics, in combination with AI analysis to identify key components of the BM TME and develop novel immunocytokines. We hypothesize that a comprehensive understanding of the BM TME, including the role of regulatory (TREM2+) macrophages, will allow us to design immunocytokines that remodel the TME towards anti-tumor activity by simultaneously modulating multiple immune cell compartments, including macrophages, T cells, and NK cells. Specifically, we will use our advanced single cell technologies to get an in-depth understanding of the BM TME and identify cytokines capable of remodulating the TME towards anti-tumor activity. We will combine these promising cytokines with our anti-TREM2 antibody to modulate both the myeloid and T and NK cell compartments. Additionally, we will identify biomarkers for targeted delivery via masking strategies to increase specificity of the immunocytokine to the BM TME. This translational research project will provide a significant clinical impact by developing effective therapies to treat melanoma BMs and greatly improve patient life expectancy and quality of life. Our outcomes will include identification of novel therapeutic targets and the development of targeted immunocytokines, as well as biomarkers to guide treatment.

# Project Description
## Background and Specific Aims
Brain metastases (BMs) occur in almost 50% of patients with metastatic melanoma, leading to a poor prognosis and reduced overall survival. While immunotherapy has significantly improved outcomes for melanoma patients, many still experience relapse or do not respond to treatment, particularly in the immunosuppressive environment of brain metastases (BMs). The brain's tumor microenvironment (TME) is known to suppress the body's anti-tumor response. Using advanced single-cell technologies and machine learning, we have identified a subtype of immune cell, regulatory (TREM2+) macrophages, as a key suppressor of anti-tumor immunity in melanoma BMs. We have developed an antibody that blocks the suppressive action of these cells. Our rationale is that in-depth understanding of the immune environment of BMs enabled by advanced single cell technologies, will uncover novel approaches for activating the immune system to eliminate the BMs. We also hypothesize that simultaneously modulating different immune cell compartments (macrophages, T cells, NK cells) can induce unprecedented anti-tumor immune responses. Our hypothesis is that using our advanced single cell technologies, including cell temporal tracking (ZMAN-seq, differentiation flows), cell-cell interactions (PIC-seq), and improved strategies for AI analysis of spatial transcriptomics with Stereo-seq, we can achieve an in-depth understanding of the BM TME and identify cytokines capable of remodulating the TME towards anti-tumor activity. This will enable us to design novel immunocytokines combining the most promising cytokines with our anti-TREM2 antibody to modulate both the myeloid and T and NK cell compartments. Furthermore, our single cell and AI analysis will enable us to identify biomarkers to design masking strategies to increase the specificity of the immunocytokine to the BM TME.
## Preliminary Data
Our preliminary data strongly supports the feasibility of our proposed research. We have previously demonstrated the synergistic potential of combining an anti-TREM2 antibody with cytokines, observing a robust pro-inflammatory macrophage activation in vitro. This is crucial, as TREM2+ macrophages are a key immunosuppressive component of the brain tumor microenvironment (TME) in melanoma brain metastases (BMs). Furthermore, we have successfully produced preliminary TREM2 immunocytokine models, including an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein, indicating that our molecule design is feasible. We have also designed and tested in vivo a masking strategy for the cytokine arm using blocking moieties responsive specifically to tumor-associated macrophage (TAM) proteases. These results demonstrate that the technology we plan to use is already working in our hands, thus setting a solid foundation for the proposed project. These pilot studies provide critical supporting evidence that our approach is viable and that we can achieve our ambitious research goals.
## Research Plan

### Research Objectives

#### Objective 1: Developing BM TME models with holistic, multimodal AI-driven analysis

The primary objective of this research aim is to develop comprehensive models of the brain metastasis tumor microenvironment (BM TME) using advanced single-cell technologies and AI-driven analysis. This will involve employing orthotopic immunocompetent murine models of melanoma BMs, alongside a large cohort of patient clinical samples, to create single-cell atlases of immune, stromal, and tumor cells from both human and murine BMs. The overarching goal is to understand the complex immunosuppressive nature of the BM TME and identify novel cytokine-based approaches that can shift the environment from immunologically "cold" to "hot". This will be achieved through three interconnected tasks. First, to gain a temporal understanding of immune activity within the BM TME, we will employ ZMAN-seq, a single-cell technology that tracks transcriptomic dynamics across time using fluorescent antibody time stamps. This method will allow us to monitor cell state transitions and differentiation pathways of immune cells in the brain metastasis environment, elucidating the kinetics of immune dysfunction. Second, we will investigate immune cell-cell interactions within the BM TME using PIC-seq, allowing for a thorough examination of cell-cell communication networks. Lastly, we will apply improved AI strategies to analyze spatial transcriptomics data obtained using Stereo-seq, to understand the spatial distribution of immune cell subsets and their interactions within the tumor microenvironment. All these advanced techniques will be applied to create integrated tissue models from single cells, holistically inferring their physiology and potential drug responses. The expectation is to identify shared processes and pathways between the murine models and human patient TMEs. This combined approach will lead to a refined list of cytokines that possess high antitumor activity in melanoma BMs, which will then be further examined in subsequent tasks using both in-vitro and in-vivo functional screening systems. This multi-faceted approach allows for comprehensive understanding of the BM TME and provides a strong data driven platform for the development of more effective immunotherapies for patients with melanoma brain metastases.

##### Preliminary Results

Our preliminary results build upon previous research demonstrating the synergistic potential of an anti-TREM2 antibody combined with cytokines to induce pro-inflammatory macrophage activity in vitro. We have successfully produced TREM2 immunocytokines, including an IL-2-anti-TREM2 fusion protein, and have developed a masking strategy for the cytokine arm using blocking moieties that are responsive to TAM-specific proteases and successfully tested them in vivo. These preliminary findings directly support our research objective to develop novel cytokine-based approaches to shift the brain metastasis (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME) from immunologically "cold" to "hot." Our previous work using Zman-seq, a single-cell technology for tracking transcriptomic dynamics across time, has shown that immune cells infiltrating the tumor microenvironment undergo rapid transitions to dysfunctional states, particularly NK cells, which transition to a low cytotoxic state within 24 hours due to TGF-β imprinting and monocytes differentiating to immunosuppressive TAMs characterized by upregulation of key suppressive myeloid checkpoints such as Trem2, Il18bp and Arg1, over 36 to 48 hours. Crucially, we have demonstrated that blocking TREM2 using an antagonistic mAb can redirect monocyte differentiation towards pro-inflammatory TAMs. These results were observed in a murine glioblastoma model using temporal analysis with Zman-seq. Using this method, we have previously measured single-cell RNA profiles of 10,583 leukocytes and grouped them into 139 metacells. We saw a correlation of tumor exposure time with immune cell functional states, showing chemotactic and cytotoxic NK cells being enriched early (12hr), while dysfunctional NK cells (Itgal+, Ctla2a+, Prfl/Gzma/Gzmb-low) were enriched later. These experiments provide a solid proof of concept for using single-cell temporal analysis to understand tumor microenvironment dynamics and identify key targets for therapeutic intervention. Building on our preliminary work, we will apply ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq to our melanoma BM TME model. The goal is to create single-cell atlases of immune, stromal, and tumor cells from both human and murine BMs, and to understand the kinetics of immune dysfunction and cell-cell communication networks and their spatial distribution within the tumor microenvironment, ultimately leading to the identification of cytokines with high anti-tumor activity.

###### Task 1.1: Temporal understanding of immune activity in BM TME

This research task aims to investigate the temporal dynamics of immune cell activity within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME) using Zman-seq. The study will adapt the Zman-seq technology, previously applied to glioma, to the BM setting. Zman-seq, an in vivo single-cell transcriptomic method, will be employed to track immune cell state transitions over time by introducing fluorescent antibody time stamps into circulating immune cells and then tracking these cells in the BM tissue. This method allows for the empirical measurement of tissue exposure time of immune cells. The experimental design involves a pulse-chase approach where circulating immune cells are fluorescently labeled and their infiltration into the BM TME is monitored. The labeled cells will be profiled using fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS), and the fluorescent stamps will be captured using index-sorting to determine the time of tumor infiltration and tumor exposure time. The single-cell transcriptomic data will be analyzed in conjunction with the temporal information to generate dynamic maps of the BM TME. Data analysis will include the construction of cellular trajectories and differentiation pathways, identification of time-dependent gene modules, transcription factor circuits, and receptor-ligand interaction events. ###### Task 1.2: Immune cell-cell interaction in the BM TME

This research task aims to investigate immune cell-cell interactions within the Bone Marrow Tumor Microenvironment (BM TME) using PIC-seq technology. PIC-seq combines cell sorting of physically interacting cells with single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) to profile cellular interactions and their impact on gene expression. This approach will allow us to characterize the transcriptional states of interacting cells and contrast them with non-interacting single cells, offering insights into the molecular mechanisms driving cell-cell communication in the BM TME. The methodology involves a mild tissue dissociation protocol to retain in situ cellular structures followed by fluorescence sorting using mutually exclusive cell type markers to isolate putative PICs. Subsequently, massively parallel scRNA-seq will be performed, generating gene expression profiles for PICs which are then computationally deconvolved. This allows for the inference of transcriptional states and facilitates comparison with non-interacting single cells. While PIC-seq is effective in characterizing interactions, it is crucial to ensure that the transcriptional profiles of interacting cells are sufficiently 'distant' for optimal deconvolution. This analysis will require substantial computational resources for data processing, including specialized algorithms for deconvolution and statistical analysis to identify significant gene expression changes associated with cell interactions. Furthermore, this task is highly dependent on the results from Task 1.2 which provides cell-cell interaction data, and feeds into screening in Objective 2, highlighting a temporal relationship in the research objectives. The lack of details on specific markers for immune cell types in the BM TME is a critical gap in the task description. Specific markers for cell sorting would need to be identified to accurately isolate and analyze desired cell populations. Additionally, the downstream analysis and specific tools/algorithms needed for PIC-seq data processing were not clearly defined and need to be identified, as well as the method for validation of these interactions.

###### Task 1.3: Immune spatial distribution in the BM TME

This research task focuses on characterizing the spatial distribution of immune cells within the bone marrow tumor microenvironment (BM TME) using Stereo-seq technology. This approach will provide a high-resolution, large field-of-view perspective on the immune landscape, complementing temporal data from Task 1.1 and cell-cell interaction data from Task 1.2. The Stereo-seq methodology involves DNA nanoball-patterned arrays and in situ RNA capture, enabling spatial transcriptomics at cellular resolution. Specifically, the process begins with the design of a DNB patterned array chip, followed by in situ sequencing to determine spatial coordinates of uniquely barcoded oligonucleotides. Capture probes are then prepared, and in situ RNA capture is performed, with cDNA amplification and sequencing used for data acquisition. This will allow for the generation of spatially resolved transcriptomic profiles, with a reported resolution of 2um to 100um spot sizes, achieving high sensitivity and large capture areas (up to 13.2cm x 13.2cm). Data analysis will incorporate a spatially constrained-clustering algorithm (SCC), optimized for complex tissues, to identify distinct tissue domains and gene expression patterns. The analysis will use algorithms to compare with scRNA-seq datasets and in situ hybridization data for validation. The resulting spatial maps will allow for identification of immune cell locations and assessment of their local environment. These spatial maps will also inform screening strategies in Objective 2. This approach provides superior transcript capture compared to methods like Seq-Scope, and avoids the problem of spatial data interpretation from conventional cell taxonomy approaches. The integration of data from this task with that of Tasks 1.1 and 1.2 is crucial for a holistic understanding of the BM TME. Further analysis may involve spatial RNA velocity calculations to study cell fate transitions and identification of spatially auto-correlated gene or regulon modules. **MISSING INFORMATION** includes specific antibody panel for immune cell identification via Stereo-seq, specific sample sizes, and the method for integration with the mentioned scRNA-seq datasets.

##### Risks and Alternatives

The primary risk associated with this research is the potential failure to establish robust, reproducible murine models that accurately reflect human melanoma brain metastasis (BM) tumor microenvironments (TME). Specifically, the orthotopic immunocompetent models may not fully recapitulate the complex immunosuppressive landscape of human BMs, limiting the translatability of findings. To mitigate this, we will rigorously validate murine TME models by cross-comparing single-cell atlases with human clinical samples, identifying conserved pathways and processes. A second major risk is the high technical complexity and cost associated with implementing ZMAN-seq, PIC-seq, and Stereo-seq simultaneously. This may lead to delays or challenges in data acquisition and analysis. Mitigation will include establishing clear protocols and pipelines for each technology, ensuring adequate training for personnel, and seeking external expertise for troubleshooting. Furthermore, the AI-driven analysis of large-scale multi-omics data introduces a risk of bias or error in interpretation. To address this, we will employ rigorous quality control metrics, validate AI algorithms using publicly available datasets, and ensure transparent reporting of analysis methods. Potential challenges in procuring high-quality clinical samples and patient data are also a risk; this will be addressed by having established collaborations with hospitals in Israel. Alternative approaches include focusing on in vitro platforms if in vivo modeling proves challenging.

#### Objective 3: Design of tumor-targeting immunocytokines

The core hypothesis of this objective is that tumor-targeting immunocytokines, designed with specific cytokines and antibodies, can effectively modulate the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) of melanoma brain metastases (BMs) towards a more anti-tumor state. To achieve this, we will utilize the insights gained from Objective 2 to inform the design of immunocytokines, focusing on species-conserved tumor antigens highly expressed in melanoma BMs. The overarching goal is to move beyond a binary response/no-response paradigm by incorporating iterative, model-driven optimization. This will be accomplished through three primary tasks: (1) Design fusion proteins and cleavage sites: Employing our knowledge of validated cytokines (from prior objectives), we will engineer antibody-cytokine fusion proteins (immunocytokines). This design will incorporate both full IgG and antibody fragments, carefully selecting payloads (e.g., IL2, IL10, IL12, IL15) based on their immunomodulatory properties and tumor targeting potential. We will also engineer specific cleavage sites within the linker region to ensure efficient release of the active cytokine within the TME. (2) Produce fusion proteins: Utilizing well-established recombinant protein production techniques, we will express and purify the designed immunocytokine constructs. This will involve optimizing expression systems and purification protocols to ensure high yields and protein quality for in vitro and in vivo validation. (3) In-vitro validation of immunocytokines: Utilizing the TME models developed in objective 1, we will perform a comprehensive assessment of the in vitro activity and efficacy of our designed immunocytokines. We will measure key parameters, such as cytokine release, receptor binding affinity, and the activation of effector immune cells (T cells and NK cells) at various concentrations and timing. This task is crucial in confirming the functionality and assessing the optimal doses. The expected results will be a panel of validated immunocytokine constructs that exhibit high tumor targeting specificity, efficient cytokine release, and potent anti-tumor activity in vitro. This will allow us to select lead immunocytokine candidates for further in vivo assessment. ##### Risks and Alternatives

The primary risks associated with this objective involve the successful design, production, and validation of functional immunocytokines. A significant technical risk lies in the potential for the engineered fusion proteins to exhibit poor stability or low binding affinity to the target antigens, thereby compromising their therapeutic efficacy. Mitigation strategies include generating diverse immunocytokine constructs with various linkers and payloads, systematically screening them for binding affinity and stability, and optimizing production protocols to ensure high protein yields. ## Experimental Design and Methods
This project will employ a multi-faceted experimental design to achieve our specific aims, focusing on the comprehensive characterization of the brain metastasis (BM) tumor microenvironment (TME) and the development of targeted immunotherapies. Our methodology is grounded in scientific rigor and emphasizes reproducibility and validation at every stage. Firstly, we will utilize orthotopic immunocompetent murine models for melanoma BMs, developed by the Weiss group, alongside a large cohort of patient clinical samples to generate single-cell atlases of immune, stromal, and tumor cells from human and murine BMs. This approach ensures a translational relevance of our findings. We will go beyond the current state of the art by building integrated tissue models from single cells and holistically inferring their physiology and potential drug response. We will use novel technologies developed in our groups for cell temporal tracking (ZMAN-seq, differentiation flows), cell-cell interactions (PIC-seq), and improved strategies for AI analysis of spatial transcriptomics with Stereo-seq. These techniques allow us to track the dynamic changes in the TME over time, understand cell-cell interactions, and map the spatial organization of cells in the tumor. Specifically, ZMAN-seq will be crucial to dissect how cells are reprogrammed in vivo upon exposure to the BM environment, by measuring the exposure time of infiltrating immune cells to the tumor microenvironment and defining the cellular trajectory and differentiation pathways underlying immune incompetence. PIC-seq will provide an in-depth understanding of cell-cell interactions within the TME, identifying crucial communication networks. Finally, Stereo-seq will give us unprecedented detail of spatial transcriptomics at cellular resolution, revealing the spatial cell-type heterogeneity of BMs. Our single-cell analysis will also include an in-vitro functional screening system for bone-marrow-derived monocyte diversification toward the M2 suppressor macrophage state, incorporating a control culture of monocytes derived from TREM2-/- mice that do not differentiate toward the M2 macrophage state. We will then co-culture myeloid and NK-T cells and apply scRNA-seq analysis to understand the effects of cytokines in combination with our anti-TREM2 mAb. For in-vivo screening, we have developed a platform to over-express cytokines in an inducible fashion by barcoded (Pro-Code) tumor cell lines, and screen the most promising cytokines in combination with our anti-TREM2 mAbs in vivo. We will rely on our in-depth scRNA-seq analysis pipeline to make a data-driven selection of the 3-5 cytokines with the most promising modulatory function in combination with our anti-TREM2 mAb. Our data management plan emphasizes the use of cloud-based storage and analysis tools to ensure data security and accessibility. We will also implement rigorous quality control measures at every stage of data acquisition and processing, adhering to the FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) data principles. Our experimental design includes appropriate controls at each step. For instance, in our in vivo studies, we will include control groups treated with vehicle alone or irrelevant antibodies. In the single cell studies we will compare cells from tumor bearing vs naive animals and cells expressing different TREM2 levels. We are confident that this comprehensive approach, combining state-of-the-art technologies, detailed analysis, and rigorous controls, will enable us to achieve our ambitious research goals. Our prior experience and preliminary data provide compelling evidence that our approach is feasible and will yield significant advancements in the development of effective therapies for melanoma brain metastases. ## Statistical Plan
Our statistical analysis will be rigorous and designed to ensure the reproducibility of our findings. For single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data, we will employ established pipelines using Scanpy and Seurat for quality control, normalization, and dimensionality reduction. We will perform differential gene expression analysis using DESeq2 to identify significant changes in gene expression between different experimental groups. For trajectory analysis, we will use methods like Palantir and Monocle to infer cell differentiation paths and identify key regulators of cellular reprogramming in the BM microenvironment. Cell-cell interaction analysis will be performed using NicheNet, which allows us to predict potential ligand-receptor interactions between cell types based on scRNA-seq data. Spatial transcriptomics data from Stereo-seq will be analyzed using established pipelines, integrating it with scRNA-seq data to map gene expression patterns onto spatial locations. We will use statistical tests appropriate for our data, including t-tests or ANOVA for comparisons between groups and non-parametric tests (e.g., Mann-Whitney U test, Kruskal-Wallis test) where the assumptions of normality are violated. The sample size for our in vivo experiments will be determined based on power calculations to ensure adequate statistical power to detect meaningful differences between treatment groups. Specifically, we plan to use a sample size of 8 mice per group for in vivo studies, which was shown to be sufficient to observe significant differences in our previous experiments. We will use effect size estimates derived from previous publications and our own preliminary data to calculate the appropriate sample size. For in vitro studies we will use three biological replicates per condition and at least 3000 cells per sample for scRNA-seq. For the analysis of the functional screening assays, we will use appropriate statistical methods to identify cytokines with modulatory effects on macrophage differentiation and T cell activation. For the cell-cell interaction analysis, we will use machine learning methods to find patterns and generate hypothesis about cell interactions. Data interpretation will be performed based on statistical significance and biological relevance, and we will use biostatistical techniques to model multi-omics data. Data management will be performed by storing data in our cloud-based servers, which will ensure accessibility and security. The data will be made available after publication in an appropriate repository. The complete data analysis workflow, including code and metadata, will be publicly available to ensure the reproducibility of our results.
