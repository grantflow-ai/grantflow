#### Aim 1: Discovery campaign of cytokine candidates with optimal synergistic effects with anti-TREM2 antibody
The primary objective of this research aim is to identify cytokine candidates that exhibit optimal synergistic effects when combined with the anti-TREM2 monoclonal antibody (mAb), E3C7. The working hypothesis posits that specific combinations of pro-inflammatory cytokines will enhance the therapeutic efficacy of the anti-TREM2 mAb, thereby improving immune modulation in relevant cellular contexts. To achieve this, we will conduct a comprehensive screening of approximately 30 cytokines, including but not limited to CCL3, IL-6, IL-12, and CXCL10, assessing their effects on myeloid, natural killer (NK), and T cell activity. The general goals encompass the identification of the most effective cytokine combinations that can potentiate the immune response against TREM2-expressing targets, ultimately leading to improved therapeutic strategies for conditions associated with TREM2 dysregulation.

  The methodology will involve five distinct tasks: first, we will measure the effects of the selected cytokines in conjunction with the anti-TREM2 mAb on the activation and proliferation of co-cultured human monocyte-derived macrophages (huMDMs), NK cells, and T cells, utilizing in vitro assays to quantify cytokine secretion and cell activation markers. Second, we will perform killing assays to evaluate the cytotoxic efficacy of these combinations against human-derived cancer cells. Third, we will select the most effective cytokines based on their performance in the initial assays. Fourth, we will employ a single-cell analysis approach using an in vitro functional screening system to dissect the cellular responses to the cytokine-mAb combinations at a granular level. Finally, we will implement a single-cell analysis of an in vivo screening system to validate the findings in a more physiologically relevant context.

  The expected results include the identification of specific cytokine combinations that significantly enhance the activity of the anti-TREM2 mAb, providing a molecular basis for their synergistic effects. This research aims to elucidate the underlying mechanisms of action and establish a foundation for the development of novel immunotherapeutic strategies targeting TREM2-related pathologies. By integrating advanced multi-omics and machine learning analytics, we anticipate generating a robust dataset that will inform future therapeutic designs and clinical applications.

#### Aim 2: Design, development and production of novel mAb-cytokine fusion molecules
The primary objective of this research aim is the design, development, and production of novel mAb-cytokine fusion molecules, with a specific focus on validating their pro-inflammatory and antineoplastic activities. The working hypothesis posits that these fusion proteins will exhibit enhanced therapeutic efficacy through synergistic interactions between monoclonal antibodies and cytokines, thereby improving immune responses against tumors. The overarching goal is to create a new class of immunotherapeutics that can effectively modulate the tumor microenvironment (TME) and promote anti-tumor immunity while minimizing systemic toxicity.

 To achieve this, we will employ a multi-faceted methodology encompassing the design of fusion proteins and cleavage sites, followed by their production and rigorous validation. The design phase will utilize advanced computational modeling to optimize the structural and functional properties of the fusion proteins. Subsequent production will involve recombinant DNA technology to express the fusion proteins in suitable host systems. Validation will be conducted through a series of in silico and in vitro assays, including co-culture experiments with human monocyte-derived macrophages (huMDMs), natural killer (NK) cells, and T cells to assess the immunomodulatory effects of the fusion proteins. Specific assays will include measuring T-NK cell activation, cytokine secretion, and proliferation, as well as conducting killing assays against human-derived cancer cells.

 Expected results include the identification of lead fusion molecules that demonstrate significant pro-inflammatory activity and enhanced cytotoxicity against tumor cells. We anticipate that these molecules will selectively activate immune cells in a TREM2-positive niche, as confirmed through specificity testing using genetically modified huMDMs. Furthermore, in-depth analysis of the in vivo efficacy and toxicity profiles of the lead candidates will be performed using humanized TREM2 animal models inoculated with various tumors. This comprehensive approach aims to elucidate the mechanisms of action (MoA) of the fusion proteins and establish their potential as viable therapeutic agents in oncology. Ultimately, the successful completion of this aim will contribute to the advancement of targeted immunotherapies, paving the way for clinical applications in cancer treatment.
