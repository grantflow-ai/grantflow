## Section I. Notice of Funding Opportunity Description

#### Background

Authorized by the U.S. Congress in 1993, the National Institutes of Health (NIH) established the Institutional Development Award (IDeA) program to build biomedical research capacity in the [23 U.S. states and Puerto Rico](https://www.nigms.nih.gov/Research/DRCB/IDeA/Pages/default.aspx) that had historically low NIH grant funding success rates. IDeA funding programs collectively support biomedical research in basic, clinical, behavioral, and translational science in IDeA-eligible states by developing faculty investigators, providing research opportunities to students, and enhancing research infrastructure.

The [IDeA Clinical & Translational Research programs](https://www.nigms.nih.gov/Research/DRCB/IDeA/Pages/idea-ctrp.aspx) build clinical and translational (C&T) research capacity to enable research on diseases and health conditions prevalent in IDeA states. This Notice of Funding Opportunity (NOFO) supports IDeA Clinical and Translational Research Networks (CTR-N) awards, which fund statewide or multi-state networks of health research organizations and community clinics involved in health research to: expand existing infrastructure for C&T research; develop the research workforce to enhance the network organizations' abilities to design and implement competitive C&T research programs; and coordinate clinical research activities state-wide and/or across IDeA states. Parallel to this NOFO, [PAR-23-257](https://grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/pa-files/PAR-23-257.html) supports IDeA Clinical & Translational Research Development (CTR-D) awards that focus on C&T research workforce development and infrastructure enhancement. An IDeA-Clinical Research Resource Center ([I-CRRC](https://www.nigms.nih.gov/Research/DRCB/IDeA/Pages/idea-ctrp.aspx)) was funded to develop clinical research coordinators for IDeA states and to improve access to clinical research and clinical trials across health research organizations in IDeA states, including those affiliated with CTR-Ns and CTR-Ds.

To maximize the impact of the capacity building investment, an organization may participate as a lead or partner organization in one CTR-N (or its predecessor IDeA-CTR) or CTR-D award at a time. Applications that include an organization supported or proposed to be supported by another CTR-N, IDeA-CTR, CTR-D award, or by a Clinical and Translational Science Award (CTSA) Hub as a partner will not be accepted for review; see Section III. Eligibility Information for details. In keeping with the goal of the CTR-N program, applications with a narrow disease or population focus are not responsive to this NOFO and will not be accepted for review.

#### Program Description and Requirements

**Network Leadership and Organization**

A CTR-N must be led by a health research organization with a track record of externally funded C&T research activities on a broad spectrum of health challenges faced by the populations it serves. The applicant/lead organization is also expected to have a well-established research administration infrastructure that can effectively manage C&T research projects including clinical trials and sub-awards to other organizations in the network. The applicant organization must assemble a statewide or multi-state network of health research organizations that conduct externally funded C&T research. These organizations are designated as the network's partner organizations. The CTR-N must also include a nexus of community clinics interested in health research, such as clinics of a Practice-Based Research Network ([PBRN](https://pbrn.ahrq.gov/)), with the goal of engaging their clinical staff and patients in research led by the CTR-N.

If a CTR-N includes organizations from multiple IDeA states, the states must work together as equal partners to build synergistic C&T research capacity across the region.

An established clinical investigator, or investigators for multiple PD/PI (multi-PI) applications, with a track record of leading externally funded C&T research projects or programs is expected to lead the CTR-N as the Program Director/Principal Investigator (PD/PI). The PD/PI should also have the leadership experience and skills to work across institutional boundaries to coalesce the participating organizations into a cohesive network and to integrate the CTR-N's components to maximize efficiency and effectiveness.

There are three required committees to assist the PD/PI in administering a CTR-N:

1. **Steering Committee (SC):** The SC is composed of the PD(s)/PI(s) and one senior institutional leader (President, Vice President, Provost, Dean or equivalent) from each partner organization, and is charged with ensuring the network’s adherence to the CTR-N’s mission to build C&T research capacity statewide or regionally and maintaining each network organization’s support for participation of their workforce in CTR-N activities.
1. **Executive Committee (EC):** The EC is composed of the PD(s)/PI(s), a representative from each partner organization, and other key personnel appointed by the PD(s)/PI(s), and assists in administering the award.
1. **External Advisory Committee (EAC):** The EAC is composed of 3 - 5 experts outside the network and provides advice on critical scientific activities and administrative decisions.

See Section IV for Composition and Responsibilities of each Committee.

**CTR-N Components**

Each CTR-N application **must** include an Overall Plan and the following 5 cores:

- Administrative Core (AC)
- Research Design, Compliance, and Data Management (RDCD) Core
- Community Engagement and Outreach (CEO) Core
- Professional Development (PD) Core
- Health Research (HR) Core

Additional cores may be requested to support functions essential to a proposed network that are not provided by the required cores. All components are expected to interact with each other and leverage relevant existing resources at the network's organizations to foster a collaborative research environment.

**Overall Plan**: The applicant should describe the major healthcare concerns faced by the state(s)' populations and the goals of the CTR-N. The plan should summarize the current C&T research activities, external funding, resources and investigators of the lead and each partner organization and describe how each organization will contribute to the CTR-N's goals. The plan should describe how all proposed components and participating organizations will work together as a cohesive program that promotes workforce development, facilitates synergistic collaborations, and accelerates the pace of C&T research in the state(s).

**Administrative Core (AC)**: The AC administers the CTR-N award. In addition to coordinating the activities of all network organizations, cores, and committees, the AC manages the network's budget, prepares accurate and timely program and financial reports, conducts annual performance evaluations of the CTR-N, as well as carries out other functions required to implement the network's overall plan. The PD/PI (or the contact PD/PI for a multi-PI application) is expected to lead the AC.

**Research Design, Compliance and Data Management (RDCD) Core**: The RDCD Core provides CTR-N investigators with expertise needed to design research protocols with sufficient statistical power to ensure rigor and avoid methodological bias. The RDCD Core also provides guidance on human subjects and clinical trial research, including risk assessment, safety, intervention models, and federal regulatory and reporting requirements. Furthermore, the RDCD Core manages Electronic Health Records (EHR) and other large clinical datasets for the network. It is responsible for dataset curation and integration across the network, and for providing data analytic and biostatistical assistance to investigators in developing database queries, validating research results, and preventing methodological bias. The RDCD Core is also responsible for ensuring compliance with NIH and institutional data management policies to safeguard protected health information.

**Community Engagement and Outreach (CEO) Core**: The CEO Core leads the engagement of clinical staff and patients from community clinics and the PBRN in CTR-N research. It promotes multi-directional engagement among researchers and community groups to build trust and ensure that health concerns of the community are among the network's top research priorities. The CEO Core assists C&T researchers in developing cultural respect and knowledge regarding assessment, measurement, and research design, and offers strategies to increase the recruitment and retention of a robust participant cohort in clinical research.

**Professional Development (PD) Core**: The PD Core organizes and sponsors network-wide professional development activities such as research seminar series, grant-writing workshops, skills training, and mentoring guidance. These activities should be tailored to meet the specific needs of the CTR-N's C&T research workforce, which includes investigators who are eligible for funding through the CTR-N's Health Research Core and members of their research teams. The PD Core also works with other CTR-N Cores to ensure that training and mentoring needs in the service areas of the various cores are provided as well. For example, the PD Core is expected to offer training on federal and institutional policies for research involving human subjects, vertebrate animals, and/or biohazards.

**Health Research (HR) Core**: The HR Core manages all research supported by the CTR-N. It supports three types of projects with the goal of developing a C&T workforce, strengthening investigator-initiated C&T research, and increasing the number and impact of clinical trials and observational studies conducted at network organizations:

- **Pilot Projects:** These are one-year feasibility pilots to allow investigators to test ideas suitable for development into independent applications such as NIH career development (K-series) or small/developmental research project grant (RPG) awards. A feasibility pilot may be led either by an independent investigator or by a clinician fellow working with a research mentor. The budget for an individual Pilot Project is $50,000-$100,000 in annual direct costs.
- **Developmental Projects:** These are 2-year projects, suitable for developing into investigator-initiated applications such as NIH R01-equivalent RPGs, with additional data. A Developmental Project should be led by an independent investigator with the background and scientific expertise necessary to lead the proposed research. The maximum budget for a Developmental Project is $100,000 in annual direct costs.
- **Multi-Site Collaborative Projects:** These are projects intended to advance C&T research outcomes and address important health concerns. A Multi-Site Collaborative Project may involve network-wide participation and/or collaborations with other CTR-Ns or IDeA state institutions outside the CTR-N network. An established investigator(s) with suitable scientific and administrative expertise, including prior experience in leading clinical studies, if applicable, is expected to lead the project. At least one Multi-Site Collaborative Project, with a maximum budget of $400,000 in annual direct costs, is expected to be supported during the CTR-N's project period. As appropriate, a phased approach is encouraged to provide early funding to meet specific benchmarks that warrant the release of additional funding to complete the study.

See Section VI. Award Administration Information for details on submitting HR research projects for NIH approval.

The HR Core is responsible for establishing appropriate mechanisms to administer these research projects, including soliciting applications, arranging for their review, and selecting meritorious applications for funding; with priority given to investigations of diseases that affect the network's populations or involve collaborations of basic, clinical, and translational researchers. A PD/PI of the CTR-N is expected to lead the HR Core and to lead collaborations with I-CRRC for participating in large-scale clinical studies and training the CTR-N's clinical research workforce.

**Other Cores** (optional): Additional cores may be proposed to provide infrastructure and resources needed by network investigators to conduct C&T research. The Core should be made available to all investigators from a network organization and is expected to provide facilities and/or services that are not duplicative of existing services or facilities already available in the network. Upgrading existing research facilities, including those supported by other IDeA programs (e.g., INBRE, COBRE) is encouraged.

See [Section VIII. Other Information](#) for award authorities and regulations.

Investigators proposing NIH-defined clinical trials may refer to the Research Methods Resources website for information about developing statistical methods and study designs.

#### Additional Eligibility Criteria for Lead Organizations

To be eligible to submit a CTR-N application as the lead/applicant organization, an organization must meet each of the following criteria:

- Is located in an IDeA-eligible state, commonwealth, or jurisdiction, which are Alaska, Arkansas, Delaware, Hawaii, Idaho, Kansas, Kentucky, Louisiana, Maine, Mississippi, Montana, Nebraska, Nevada, New Hampshire, New Mexico, North Dakota, Oklahoma, Puerto Rico, Rhode Island, South Carolina, South Dakota, Vermont, West Virginia, and Wyoming; and
- Holds externally funded C&T research grants; and
- For a new application, at the time of submission:
  - Is not a partner organization of an active IDeA-CTR award; and
  - Is not a lead or partner organization of an active CTR-N award, CTR-D award, or Clinical and Translational Science Award ([CTSA Hub](https://ncats.nih.gov/ctsa/about/hubs)); and
  - Is not located in a state or a multi-state region that has an active CTR-N, CTR-D, or [CTSA Hub](https://ncats.nih.gov/ctsa/about/hubs) award; or
- For a renewal application, at the time of submission:
  - Holds an active IDeA-CTR award, which includes awards in no-cost extension

An applicant organization that leads an active IDeA-CTR award, which includes awards in no-cost extension, may opt to submit a new application if the composition of the network has substantially changed. In these cases, the active IDeA-CTR award will be administratively ended prior to the start date of an award made to this NOFO.

An organization may only be the lead of one IDeA-CTR, CTR-N, or CTR-D award or application at a time, and a state may hold only one active IDeA-CTR, CTR-N, or CTR-D award.

#### Additional Eligibility Criteria for Partner Organizations

To be eligible to participate in a CTR-N as a partner organization, the organization must meet each of the following criteria:

- Is located in the same state or multi-state region as the lead organization of the CTR-N application; and
- At the time of submission:
  - Is not a lead or partner organization of an active CTR-N, CTR-D, or [CTSA Hub](https://ncats.nih.gov/ctsa/about/hubs) award; and
  - Is not located in a state or a multi-state region that has an active CTR-N award.

An organization may only be a partner of one IDeA-CTR, CTR-N, or CTR-D award or application at a time.

*A. Goals of the CTR-N:* Describe the goals of the CTR-N and the roles of lead and partner organizations in meeting those goals. Key elements to include are:

- The overall vision and goals of the CTR-N to address the major healthcare concerns faced by the state(s)' populations through support for research, infrastructure, and workforce development.

- A description of the lead and partner organizations and how each will contribute to accomplishing the CTR-N's goals.

- Innovative approaches to improve or strengthen C&T research across the CTR-N, including

  - Plans to increase interest among investigators in conducting C&T research on health concerns that impact the population in the state(s).
  - Plans for developing and/or strengthening collaborations across the network of participating organizations and community clinics (PBRN), to ensure the CTR-N performs effectively as one cohesive and coherent program across organizational boundaries.
  - Plans to collaborate with and leverage resources and infrastructure supported by other IDeA awards to the network organizations (e.g., INBRE and COBRE).
  - Plans to collaborate with IDeA state organizations outside the network, including those funded by other NIH-funded C&T programs (e.g., other CTR-N, CTR-D, CTSA, or I-CRRC).
  - Plans or initiatives to address barriers and improve or strengthen C&T research across the CTR-N.

  *B. Capacity for C&T Research:* To establish that there is sufficient C&T research capacity in the network to merit a CTR-N, describe the current C&T research capacity of lead and partner organizations (use tables where appropriate). Key elements to include are:

- Investigator research support and productivity:

  - The current number and annual total dollar amount of C&T research grants held by investigators at network organizations.
  - A summary of C&T-related publications authored by investigators at network organizations over the past five years.
  - A summary of the health conditions that are currently being studied.
  - For renewals, provide information on the progress and accomplishments from the current IDeA-CTR funding period, including:
    - The annual number of pilot projects supported and the number of publications, research grant applications and awards resulting from pilot project support.
    - The annual number of pilot projects that were collaborative, multidisciplinary, multi-site, and/or clinical trial projects.
    - A summary of non-pilot project C&T research supported by supplement awards and/or IDeA-CTR cores.

- Network Strength:

  - A summary of existing research collaborations among network investigators and other IDeA-supported programs including other CTR-N/IDeA-CTR programs, COBRE awards, and INBRE awards.
  - A synopsis of existing connections with community clinics/PBRN sites in the state(s).
  - For renewals, provide a summary of research collaborations initiated, community clinics/PBRNs engaged, and improvements in C&T infrastructure made during the current IDeA-CTR funding period.

- Professional Development:

  - A summary of programs, seminars, workshops, and mentoring activities provided to network participants.
  - For renewals, indicate activities that were developed during the current IDeA-CTR funding period.

C. Organizational Structure and Governance Model: The applicant should provide a clear organizational structure for the network that illustrates the relationships among the organizations, components, and committees. Key elements to include are:

- The lead organization's research administration infrastructure, its suitability for administering the CTR-N award and its subcontracts, and its experience in managing research involving human subjects and vertebrate animals.
- The externally funded C&T research base, including participation in clinical trials, for each lead and partner organization. Describe areas of C&T research expertise of faculty investigators and how their expertise will contribute to the goals of the network.
- A description of the three required CTR-N committees that will assist the PD(s)/PI(s) in administering the award:
  - Steering Committee (SC): The SC is composed of the PD(s)/PI(s) and one senior institutional leader (President, Vice President, Provost, Dean or equivalent) from each partner organization. The PD/PI (or the contact PD/PI) of the CTR-N award serves as the Chair. The SC should be consulted for decisions with network-wide impact, such as a change in network organization membership, appointment of the EAC Chair, and alterations of program priorities and key policies to ensure the network’s adherence to the CTR-N’s mission in building C&T research capacity statewide. As senior leaders of network organizations, the SC members also assure their organizations' commitments to supporting their workforces' participation in CTR-N activities and through sharing resources and sharing C&T research resources. An operating plan for the SC, including the names and positions of its members, should be provided.
  - Executive Committee (EC): The EC is composed of the PD(s)/PI(s), a representative from each partner organization, and other key personnel appointed by the PD(s)/PI(s). The PD/PI (the contact PD/PI in case of multi-PIs) should serve as Chair of the EC. The EC assists the PD(s)/PI(s) in establishing policies, developing initiatives, overseeing components of the CTR-N, making funding recommendations, and coordinating network-wide activities, and must submit annual progress reports to the EAC. An operating plan for the EC, including the names and positions of its members, should be provided.
  - External Advisory Committee (EAC): The EAC is composed of 3-5 experts in C&T research from outside the network. The EAC chair is appointed by the PD(s)/PI(s) in consultation with the SC. Other EAC members are appointed by the PD(s)/PI(s). The EAC reviews the annual CTR-N progress report provided by EC and provides feedback for improvement to the PD(s)/PI(s) and the SC through its annual assessment report. The EAC also assesses all research projects of the HR Core prior to their submission to the NIH for approval. An operating plan for the EAC should be provided that includes a description of the process for filling EAC vacancies and the name, position, and scientific expertise of each continuing EAC member (for existing EACs from previous CTRs). For new EAC members, only provide a description of the qualities and scientific expertise desired without naming specific individuals or contacting them prior to receiving the Notice of Award for the application.
- A governance plan for the CTR-N, which should include a description of:
  - How each member organization will have input and participate in decision making.
  - How the network organizations will maintain ongoing communication.
  - How the committees (SC, EC, and EAC) will work with each other and with the CTR-N components to accomplish the goals of the CTR-N.
  - Interactions between the PD(s)/PI(s) and Core Leads, who must be experienced personnel with the requisite stature, knowledge, authority, leadership, administrative skills and capabilities to direct the resources, which includes leading a team that provides services to investigators and being responsible for:
    - Developing or improving standard operating procedures governing the utilization and management of core expertise and resources;
    - Developing or improving plans for disseminating information, training, and serving as subject-matter-experts to investigators;
    - Maintaining state-of-the-art technologies and knowledge capabilities; and
    - Recommending to the CTR-N PD(s)/PI(s) and the EC any technology updates and/or replacement of equipment.
  - For renewal applications, a succession plan for PD(s)/PI(s) who have been leading a CTR program for more than 10 years.

**Letters of Support:** A letter of support from a senior official of each of the network organizations is expected to demonstrate institutional commitment for the CTR-N. Each letter should:

- Commit the organization to the CTR-N's goals and describe how the CTR-N's goals align with the organization's strategic priorities.
- Describe the commitment of specific resources, which may include support for hiring new investigators, clinical and core support staff, and/or investment in the infrastructure needed to conduct C&T research. Specific commitments to cores or other components should be summarized in a table.

Letters from the PD(s)/PI(s) of other NIH IDeA awards describing how their programs will support the CTR-N and its goals may be included. Additional letters addressing substantive and specific needs of the CTR-N may also be included.

## Section V. Application Review Information

**1. Criteria**

Only the review criteria described below will be considered in the review process. Applications submitted to the NIH in support of the [NIH mission](//grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/url_redirect.php?id=11149) are evaluated for scientific and technical merit through the NIH peer review system.

Overall Impact - Overall Reviewers will provide an overall impact score to reflect their assessment of the likelihood for the network to exert a sustained, powerful influence on the research field(s) involved, in consideration of the following review criteria and additional review criteria (as applicable for the network proposed).

Scored Review Criteria - Overall Reviewers will consider each of the review criteria below in the determination of scientific merit and give a separate score for each. An application does not need to be strong in all categories to be judged likely to have major scientific impact. For example, a network that by its nature is not innovative may be essential to advance a field.

#### Significance

- Does the proposed network have existing C&T research capacity and strengthen and evidence of collaborations among participating organizations at the level expected for a CTR-N?
- What important problems or barriers to conducting C&T research have been identified? How will the plans to address these strengthen C&T research across the network?
- How will each network organization contribute to the CTR-N's goals?
- If the application is a renewal, to what extent did the IDeA-CTR award strengthen the capacity of network organizations to conduct C&T research?
- How likely is the network to advance research on important health concerns for the population of the state(s)?

#### Investigator(s)

- Are the PD(s)/PI(s) well suited to lead the CTR-N? How well does prior scientific and administrative leadership experience prepare the PD(s)/PI(s) to lead and integrate the components that comprise the CTR-N and to work across organizational boundaries?
- For multi-PI applications, to what extent are the leadership approach and arrangements for sharing responsibility appropriate for the CTR-N?

#### Innovation

- How innovative are the overall approaches to enhancing C&T research capacity in the network?
- How innovative are the approaches for engaging more investigators in conducting C&T research at the participating organizations?
- In what ways are the proposed plans to enhance communication, coordination, and/or collaboration among investigators, health research organizations, cores, and targeted populations innovative?

#### Approach

- How likely is it that the Overall Plan for the CTR-N will significantly strengthen C&T research activities at the participating organizations?
- To what extent do the CTR-N's plans for networking, sharing of resources, and collaborations leverage the resources of other NIH-supported programs such as COBREs and INBREs?
- Are the plans to collaborate with IDeA organizations outside the network feasible, and to what extent will they strengthen the ability of CTR-N investigators to conduct collaborative C&T research studies?
- Are the roles of the SC, EC, and EAC clearly described and appropriate? How appropriate is the proposed or existing expertise of the members of the SC, EC, and EAC for their respective functions?
- To what extent will the network's organizational structure, governance model and governance plan, including communications with and among the SC, EC, and EAC, and, as applicable, the succession plan, enable synergistic collaboration and coordination across organizations to maximize the network’s success?

#### Environment

- How strong is the C&T research base of the lead organization? How broad is the C&T research expertise of its faculty investigators? To what extent is the lead organization's research administration infrastructure appropriate for leading the CTR-N?
- How adequate are the institutional commitments of the network organizations for supporting the goals of the CTR-N?
- To what extent do the network organizations have C&T research investigators and/or workforce who can benefit from the CTR-N's resources?
- To what extent will the network organizations be able to contribute to and benefit from the overall C&T infrastructure of the network?
- If the network was previously supported by an IDeA-CTR, how successful was that program and does it create a strong foundation on which to build additional C&T research capacity?

#### Review Criteria for Cores

Each of the following components of the application will be assessed and given a score; the component scores will also be considered in assigning an Overall Impact score to the overall application.

#### Administrative Core

- How well developed are plans for managing, coordinating, integrating and overseeing the cores and committees of the CTR-N, over the entire range of its activities?
- To what extent are effective strategies proposed for facilitating communication with core leads, institutional leaders, health research organizations, and investigators?
- How appropriate are plans to manage the CTR-N's budget, and prepare and submit accurate and timely financial reports?
- How strong are the proposed evaluation strategies that will measure the effectiveness of each component and the success of investigators supported by the CTR-N? How suitable are the proposed benchmarks for success and methods for tracking the use of each core? How strong are the plans for implementing recommendations of the EAC?
- How suitable are the proposed strategies to assess, prioritize and address the health needs of the state's population?

#### RDCD Core

- Is the core appropriately designed to assist investigators in developing suitable research protocols, and how well-suited is the RDCD Core for providing advice on the assessment and mitigation of risk to human subjects and the selection of appropriate intervention models?
- How strong are the proposed processes for the curation of clinical database(s) for research, their management and use, and the protection of personal health information?

#### CEO Core

- To what extent will the proposed communication strategies engage and connect communities, clinics of PBRNs, and investigators for their mutual benefit?
- How strong are the plans to engage community advisory boards to identify and prioritize the health issues and concerns of the network's communities and populations?
- To what extent will the CEO core assist investigators in developing culturally appropriate community-engaged research projects and facilitate the recruitment and retention of research participants?

#### PD Core

- How appropriate are the plans to interface with other CTR-N Cores to select topics for workshops, seminars, and training, to enhance the knowledge base and competency of investigators, research and clinical staff, and community members:
  - For conducting C&T research?
  - To prepare them to carry out community-engaged research?
  - To prepare them to carry out human subjects and clinical trial research?
  - To access and use clinical databases?
  - To prepare mentors to provide suitable scientific and career development advice ?
- To what extent will the PD Core provide staff from other cores with opportunities to maintain and enhance their expertise?

#### HR Core

- How strong are the communication strategies to engage investigators at different career stages from a variety of disciplines across the network?
- How sound are the plans to solicit, review and select:
  - Pilot Projects that support feasibility studies?
  - Developmental Projects that support data collection for the future submission of R01-equivalent RPGs or similar research grants?
  - Multi-Site Collaborative Projects that lead to more extensive collaborations and address important health concerns?
- How suitable is the plan for distributing support among the three types of projects for meeting the collective needs of network organizations and for establishing a pipeline of future investigators? How likely is it that the plan will lead to an increase in external funding for C&T research?
- To what extent do the plans for the HR Core interface with other CTR-N Cores and ensure that all HR project leads will receive the guidance needed for conducting their research?
- How available are the support, equipment, technical expertise and other physical resources to CTR-N investigators to meet the needs of the anticipated research activities?
- How well-designed are the plans to ensure study participant safety and that all federal, state, local and organizational laws, regulations and policies are followed?

#### Other Cores (if included)

- How appropriate are the resources and expertise provided by the Core for accomplishing its objectives?

Protections for Human Subjects

For research that involves human subjects but does not involve one of the categories of research that are exempt under 45 CFR Part 46, the committee will evaluate the justification for involvement of human subjects and the proposed protections from research risk relating to their participation according to the following five review criteria: 1) risk to subjects, 2) adequacy of protection against risks, 3) potential benefits to the subjects and others, 4) importance of the knowledge to be gained, and 5) data and safety monitoring for clinical trials.

For research that involves human subjects and meets the criteria for one or more of the categories of research that are exempt under 45 CFR Part 46, the committee will evaluate: 1) the justification for the exemption, 2) human subjects involvement and characteristics, and 3) sources of materials. For additional information on review of the Human Subjects section, please refer to the [Guidelines for the Review of Human Subjects](//grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/url_redirect.php?id=11175).

Inclusion of Women, Racial and Ethnic Minorities, and Individuals Across the Lifespan

When the proposed project involves human subjects and/or NIH-defined clinical research, the committee will evaluate the proposed plans for the inclusion (or exclusion) of individuals on the basis of sex, race, and ethnicity, as well as the inclusion (or exclusion) of individuals of all ages (including children and older adults) to determine if it is justified in terms of the scientific goals and research strategy proposed. For additional information on review of the Inclusion section, please refer to the [Guidelines for the Review of Inclusion in Clinical Research](//grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/url_redirect.php?id=11174).

Vertebrate Animals

The committee will evaluate the involvement of live vertebrate animals as part of the scientific assessment according to the following criteria: (1) description of proposed procedures involving animals, including species, strains, ages, sex, and total number to be used; (2) justifications for the use of animals versus alternative models and for the appropriateness of the species proposed; (3) interventions to minimize discomfort, distress, pain and injury; and (4) justification for euthanasia method if NOT consistent with the AVMA Guidelines for the Euthanasia of Animals. Reviewers will assess the use of chimpanzees as they would any other application proposing the use of vertebrate animals. For additional information on review of the Vertebrate Animals section, please refer to the [Worksheet for Review of the Vertebrate Animals Section](//grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/url_redirect.php?id=11150).

2\. Review and Selection Process

Applications will be evaluated for scientific and technical merit by (an) appropriate Scientific Review Group(s) convened by the National Institute of General Medical Sciences (NIGMS), in accordance with [NIH peer review policy and procedures](//grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/url_redirect.php?id=11154), using the stated review criteria. As part of the scientific peer review, all applications will receive a written critique.

Applications may undergo a selection process in which only those applications deemed to have the highest scientific and technical merit (generally the top half of applications under review) will be discussed and assigned an overall impact score.

Applications will be assigned on the basis of established PHS referral guidelines to the appropriate NIH Institute or Center. Applications will compete for available funds with all other recommended applications. Following initial peer review, recommended applications will receive a second level of review by the appropriate national Advisory Council or Board. The following will be considered in making funding decisions:

- Scientific and technical merit of the proposed project as determined by scientific peer review.
- Availability of funds.
- Relevance of the proposed project to program priorities.
- Geographic distribution among IDeA-eligible states.
- Potential to serve the population of the state(s) and address their most urgent health concerns.

#### 5. Evaluation

In carrying out the stewardship of grant programs, NIGMS will periodically evaluate the CTR-N program employing the representative measures identified below. In assessing the effectiveness of research capacity building investments, NIGMS may use information from progress reports and public databases, PDs/PIs, and from participants themselves. Where necessary, PDs/PIs and participants may be appropriately contacted after the completion of the grant period for updates on participants subsequent outcomes. The overall evaluation of the program will be based on metrics that may include, but are not limited to, the following:

- Indicators of network, institutional and investigator capacity building, such as:
  - Development of investigators and clinical fellows
  - Improved access to scientific research infrastructure
  - Subsequent submission of peer-reviewed grant proposals
  - Subsequent external independent funding obtained by participants in the program
- Characteristics of the applicant and awardee pool for the program, such as:
  - Geographic distribution
  - Investigator demographics
  - Institution types
- Indicators of scientific productivity and impact among network institutions, such as:
  - Peer-reviewed research publications and citations
  - Conference presentations
- Indicators of research network development and collaboration, such as:
  - Number and characteristics of partner organizations
  - Resources shared among partner organizations
  - Research collaborations established across network institutions and with non-network IDeA institutions
