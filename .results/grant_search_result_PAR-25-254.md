## Section I. Notice of Funding Opportunity Description

#### **Purpose**

This Notice of Funding Opportunity (NOFO) is intended to support mechanistic research that aims to understand how and why expectancy effects occur in a cancer context, elucidate their role in cancer symptom management, and identify patients, symptoms, cancer sites, and contexts in which expectancy effects can be leveraged to improve cancer outcomes. Expectancies are defined in this context as beliefs about future outcomes, including ones response to cancer or cancer treatment. Expectancies can be evoked by social, psychological, environmental, and systemic factors. Expectancy effects are the cognitive, behavioral, and biological outcomes caused by expectancies. Expectancy effects can be generated by expectancies held by patients, clinicians, family members, caregivers, and/or dyadic/social networks. NCI is particularly interested in applications that enroll individuals and groups from populations historically underrepresented or excluded from biomedical and behavioral research.

#### **Background**

The number of cancer survivors in the United States is expected to continue to rise. Cancer detection and treatment advances are accompanied by an increased population of individuals experiencing cancer symptom burden and short- and long-term side effects from cancer treatment. Despite evidence-based protocols for managing cancer symptoms (e.g., pain, fatigue, nausea, anorexia, anxiety, depression, emotional distress, cognitive effects, sleep disturbance), many patients with cancer continue to have unmet symptom management needs. Moreover, cancer symptom management is not experienced equally among members of the US population. Disparities exist by race, ethnicity, income, sex, and disability status. Individuals that are disadvantaged and medically underserved in health care, in part due to interpersonal and structural discrimination, have poorer outcomes when compared to those in non-minoritized groups.

Expectancies are one target that may be harnessed to help improve cancer symptom management and reduce disparities in cancer care. Although the placebo effect (i.e., response to inert medical treatments) is a well-known example of an expectancy effect, patients may also have an expectancy response to social, behavioral, and environmental cues in the clinical context. For example, expectancy effects for symptom management can be generated not only by a placebo, but also by whether the clinician delivering such treatments voices confidence about the treatment or expresses empathy. Negative expectancies can also contribute to cancer symptom outcomes. For example, although informed consent for treatment is critically important, there may be ways of detailing potential side effects can create or attenuate expectancies that lead patients to experience these negative outcomes.

Despite the ubiquity of expectancy effects for cancer symptoms, effects of expectancy-generating factors in this context are variable. Understanding the degree to which treatment efficacy depends on expectancy is crucial for closing the symptom management gap and reducing disparities in these outcomes. Expectancy-generating factors have not been systematically evaluated, intentionally leveraged, or strategically considered in the context of cancer care delivery or outcomes. Understanding the mechanisms and moderators of various expectancy effects and identifying effective and ethical interventions that can harness these effects to improve patient outcomes are behavioral research priorities for optimal cancer care and quality of life. Importantly, translation of expectancy effects to clinical settings is unlikely to take the form of inert treatments or deceptive paradigms. Therefore, translational expectancy-leveraging efforts should be developed for implementation alongside active treatments rather than with deceptive placebo effect paradigms.

This NOFO also seeks to explore expectancy effects among medically underserved populations, and how these may contribute to health disparities. Although there are several reasons for unmet symptom management needs among medically underserved populations, including inadequate access to high-quality care and insufficient care coordination due to discrimination, expectancy effects may contribute to or compound these factors. Understanding expectancy effect in underserved groups and/ or the unequal distribution of expectancy effects (e.g., by race, ethnicity, income, sex, or disability status) is an important step in promoting equity in cancer care and outcomes, including quality of life.

#### **Research Objectives & Requirements**

This NOFO supports research on expectancy-generating factors and measures of their effects on expectancies and subsequent cancer symptom management outcomes, as well as research to identify moderators of such expectancy effects.

Projects will be required to employ the experimental medicine approach (for more information on this approach, see the [NIH Science of Behavior Change Initiative](https://commonfund.nih.gov/behaviorchange) ), which involves: 1) identifying target mechanisms (here, a relevant expectancy or expectancies) that can be engaged by manipulating one or more factors (i.e., experiment or intervention components); 2) identifying appropriate and validated measures that can reliably capture change in the target mechanism; and 3) identifying a particular outcome (i.e., cancer symptom(s)) that should change as a function of the change in the target expectancy. Planned analyses should evaluate whether the expectancy target(s) can be leveraged to change cancer symptoms, by demonstrating that the factors can influence a measure of the expectancies, that the expectancy measures are associated with symptom(s), and that the change produced in the expectancy measures is associated with changes in the symptom(s).

Projects should draw on the literature on expectancy effects to identify potential expectancy-generating factors (including those at the biobehavioral, psychological, social, environmental, and systemic levels) that can be manipulated to engage target expectancies in the context of cancer symptom management. Projects should also specify and test variables that may moderate the magnitude of expectancy effects. Projects should specify the cancer care context for the study, including the cancer site and treatment type(s), care setting(s), symptom(s), and patient population. Projects will also be required to include validated, multilevel measurements to assess target expectancies, as well as those that assess changes in cancer symptoms (including validated Patient-Reported Outcomes assessments).

Projects will be required to identify a particular clinical use case (e.g., specific symptoms matched with specific treatment), carefully select the population (including consideration of the role and limitation of variability in cancer type, severity, symptoms, etc., and of what groups experience the most unmet symptom needs), identify expectancy-generating factors and targets, and test this combination using the experimental medicine approach. Program is particularly interested in applications that enroll individuals and groups from populations historically underrepresented or excluded from biomedical and behavioral research/are medically underserved, as well as applications that provide fundamental knowledge about expectancy effects that can be translated to attenuate cancer symptom management disparities.

**Specific research questions of interest include, but are not limited to:**

*Mechanisms*

- What neurobiological, psychological, interpersonal, sociocultural, systemic, and environmental factors independently or interactively contribute to expectancy effects in cancer symptom management? Which combinations of factors produce the strongest expectancy effects in this context?
- What are the critical active ingredients (e.g., clinical rapport, empathy, communication styles) of patient-clinician encounters that produce beneficial cancer symptom management expectancy effects? What factors (e.g., verbal and nonverbal communication, explicit and implicit expressions of bias and other types of prejudice) contribute to disparities in such expectancies? To what extent can these factors be altered and harnessed to improve cancer symptom management?
- How do cancer symptom management expectancies change over time and with regard to clinical context?
- To what extent and how might expectancy effects be harnessed to improve cancer symptom management and reduce disparities in cancer care? What potential harms or other ethical issues are raised by interventions that aim to alter such expectancies?

*Moderators*

- Under what conditions (e.g., clinical or social circumstances) do expectancy effects produce physiological changes associated with cancer symptom management? What biological responses might be harnessed as biomarkers for susceptibility to such expectancy effects?
- How does the influence of the patient-clinician encounter on cancer symptom management expectancies differ depending on a patients race and culture, and other individual-level variables? How are such expectancy-related processes and outcomes influenced by racial concordance of patient-clinician dyads, cultural values and expectations, and interpersonal processes (e.g., prejudice and stigma)?
- What neurobiological, psychological, interpersonal, sociocultural, systemic, and environmental variables moderate cancer symptom management expectancy effects, and how can these be identified, measured, and harnessed to individualize the management of symptoms? Can we identify a phenotype for expectancy susceptibility in this context?

Projects submitted to this NOFO can examine any type of expectancy with potential implications for cancer symptom management, including expectancies among patients, clinicians, family members, caregivers, dyadic/social networks, or any combinations of these.

*Annual Meeting: I*nvestigators should budget for annual meetings of grantees on or near the NIH Campus in Bethesda, Maryland, to share research methods and findings. Awardees will be encouraged to consider incorporating common data elements and to collaborate on shared scientific opportunities at the annual meeting, as appropriate.

The following types of studies are not responsive to this NOFO and will not be reviewed:

- Projects that consider expectancy/expectancy effects only as an endpoint;
- Projects that propose an RCT that does not take an experimental medicine approach;
- Studies that involve interventions without examining mechanisms and/or moderators; or
- Studies that include a deceptive placebo condition.

## Section V. Application Review Information

#### 1. Criteria

Only the review criteria described below will be considered in the review process. Applications submitted to the NIH in support of the [NIH mission](//grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/url_redirect.php?id=11149) are evaluated for scientific and technical merit through the NIH peer review system.

Overall Impact Reviewers will provide an overall impact score to reflect their assessment of the likelihood for the project to exert a sustained, powerful influence on the research field(s) involved, in consideration of the following scored review criteria and additional review criteria (as applicable for the project proposed). An application does not need to be strong in all categories to be judged likely to have a major scientific impact.

Scored Review Criteria Reviewers will consider Factors 1, 2 and 3 in the determination of scientific merit, and in providing an overall impact score. In addition, Factors 1 and 2 will each receive a separate factor score.

<label>Factor 1. Importance of the Research (Significance and Innovation)</label>

***Significance***

- Evaluate the importance of the proposed research in the context of current scientific challenges and opportunities, either for advancing knowledge within the field, or more broadly. Assess whether the application addresses an important gap in knowledge in the field, would solve a critical problem, or create a valuable conceptual or technical advance.
- Evaluate the rationale for undertaking the study, the rigor of the scientific background for the work (e.g., prior literature and/or preliminary data) and whether the scientific background justifies the proposed study.

***Innovation***

- Evaluate the extent to which innovation influences the importance of undertaking the proposed research. Note that while technical or conceptual innovation can influence the importance of the proposed research, a project that is not applying novel concepts or approaches may be of critical importance for the field.

- Evaluate whether the proposed work applies novel concepts, methods or technologies or uses existing concepts, methods, technologies in novel ways, to enhance the overall impact of the project.
  ***Significance - Specific to this NOFO:***

- Evaluate whether the application describes a mechanistic project, as well as whether the study framework and design consider future translational potential for improving cancer pain and symptom management outcomes in clinical settings.

- Evaluate whether the trial includes at least one non-deceptive study condition and adequately consider pathways for ethical translation to clinical settings.

***Innovation - Specific to this NOFO:***

- Evaluate the extent to which the project will generate mechanistic knowledge about expectancy processes that affect cancer symptoms and their management.
- Evaluate whether the project leverages novel and emerging theoretical concepts and methodologies from the study of expectancies and related psychological processes.
  <label>Factor 2. Rigor and Feasibility (Approach)</label>

***Approach***

- Evaluate the scientific quality of the proposed work. Evaluate the likelihood that compelling, reproducible findings will result (rigor) and assess whether the proposed studies can be done well and within the timeframes proposed (feasibility).

***Rigor:***

- Evaluate the potential to produce unbiased, reproducible, robust data.
- Evaluate the rigor of experimental design and whether appropriate controls are in place.
- Evaluate whether the sample size is sufficient and well-justified.
- Assess the quality of the plans for analysis, interpretation, and reporting of results.
- Evaluate whether the investigators presented adequate plans to address relevant biological variables, such as sex or age, in the design, analysis, and reporting.
- For applications involving human subjects or vertebrate animals, also evaluate:
  - the rigor of the intervention or study manipulation (if applicable to the study design).
  - whether outcome variables are justified.
  - whether the results will be generalizable or, in the case of a rare disease/special group, relevant to the particular subgroup.
  - whether the sample is appropriate and sufficiently diverse to address the proposed question(s).
- For applications involving human subjects, including clinical trials, assess the adequacy of inclusion plans as appropriate for the scientific goals of the research. Considerations of appropriateness may include disease/condition/behavior incidence, prevalence, or population burden, population representation, and/or current state of the science.

***Feasibility:***

- Evaluate whether the proposed approach is sound and achievable, including plans to address problems or new challenges that emerge in the work. For proposed studies in which feasibility may be less certain, evaluate whether the uncertainty is balanced by the potential for major advances.

- For applications involving human subjects, including clinical trials, evaluate the adequacy and feasibility of the plan to recruit and retain a study population that appropriately models the target population. Additionally, evaluate the likelihood of successfully achieving the proposed enrollment based on age, race, ethnicity, and sex.

- For clinical trial applications, evaluate whether the study timeline and milestones are feasible.
  ***Approach - Specific to this NOFO***

- Evaluate whether the study is conducted in a clinical setting

- Evaluate whether the application provides a clear justification for the selection of a specific patient population(s), cancer and treatment type(s), care setting(s), and symptom(s).

- Evaluate the extent ot which the application considers which populations experience the largest symptom burden, and oversample or exclusively sample individuals from that group or otherwise consider disparities in symptom management.

- Evaluate whether the application take an experimental medicine approach (i.e., specifies at least one expectancy-generating factor, and provides preliminary evidence that the factor may be efficacious in generating the targeted expectancies)
  <label>Factor 3. Expertise and Resources (Investigator(s) and Environment)</label>

***Investigator(s)***

Evaluate whether the investigator(s) have demonstrated background, training, and expertise, as appropriate for their career stage, to conduct the proposed work. For Multiple Principal Investigator (MPI) applications, assess the quality of the leadership plan to facilitate coordination and collaboration.

***Environment***

Evaluate whether the institutional resources are appropriate to ensure the successful execution of the proposed work.

***Investigator - Specific to this NOFO:***

- Evaluate whether the study team includes medical staff (i.e., MD or specialized oncology nursing staff), as well as expertise in cancer symptom management, biobehavioral processes, psychological science, and health disparities (as appropriate).
  Additional Review Criteria As applicable for the project proposed, reviewers will consider the following additional items while determining scientific and technical merit, but will not give criterion scores for these items, and should consider them in providing an overall impact score.

  <label>Protections for Human Subjects</label>

For research that involves human subjects but does not involve one of the categories of research that are exempt under 45 CFR Part 46, evaluate the justification for involvement of human subjects and the proposed protections from research risk relating to their participation according to the following five review criteria: 1) risk to subjects; 2) adequacy of protection against risks; 3) potential benefits to the subjects and others; 4) importance of the knowledge to be gained; and 5) data and safety monitoring for clinical trials.

For research that involves human subjects and meets the criteria for one or more of the categories of research that are exempt under 45 CFR Part 46, evaluate: 1) the justification for the exemption; 2) human subjects involvement and characteristics; and 3) sources of materials. For additional information on review of the Human Subjects section, please refer to the[Guidelines for the Review of Human Subjects](https://grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/url_redirect.php?id=11175).

<label>Vertebrate Animals</label>

When the proposed research includes Vertebrate Animals, evaluate the involvement of live vertebrate animals according to the following criteria: (1) description of proposed procedures involving animals, including species, strains, ages, sex, and total number to be used; (2) justifications for the use of animals versus alternative models and for the appropriateness of the species proposed; (3) interventions to minimize discomfort, distress, pain and injury; and (4) justification for euthanasia method if NOT consistent with the AVMA Guidelines for the Euthanasia of Animals. For additional information on review of the Vertebrate Animals section, please refer to the[Worksheet for Review of the Vertebrate Animals Section](//grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/url_redirect.php?id=11150).

<label>Biohazards</label>

When the proposed research includes Biohazards, evaluate whether specific materials or procedures that will be used are significantly hazardous to research personnel and/or the environment, and whether adequate protection is proposed.

<label>Resubmissions</label>

As applicable, evaluate the full application as now presented.

<label>Renewals</label>

As applicable, evaluate the progress made in the last funding period.

<label>Revisions</label>

As applicable, evaluate the appropriateness of the proposed expansion of the scope of the project.

Additional Review Considerations As applicable for the project proposed, reviewers will consider each of the following items, but will not give scores for these items, and should not consider them in providing an overall impact score.

<label>Authentication of Key Biological and/or Chemical Resources</label>

For projects involving key biological and/or chemical resources, evaluate the brief plans proposed for identifying and ensuring the validity of those resources.

<label>Budget and Period of Support</label>

Evaluate whether the budget and the requested period of support are fully justified and reasonable in relation to the proposed research.

#### 2. Review and Selection Process

Applications will be evaluated for scientific and technical merit by (an) appropriate Scientific Review Group(s) convened by Center for Scientific Review (CSR) in accordance with [NIH peer review policy and procedures](//grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/url_redirect.php?id=11154), using the stated review criteria. Assignment to a Scientific Review Group will be shown in the eRA Commons.

As part of the scientific peer review, all applications will receive a written critique.

Applications may undergo a selection process in which only those applications deemed to have the highest scientific and technical merit (generally the top half of applications under review) will be discussed and assigned an overall impact score.

Applications will be assigned on the basis of established PHS referral guidelines to the appropriate NIH Institute or Center. Applications will compete for available funds with all other recommended applications submitted in response to this NOFO. Following initial peer review, recommended applications will receive a second level of review by the appropriate national Advisory Council or Board. The following will be considered in making funding decisions:

- Scientific and technical merit of the proposed project as determined by scientific peer review.
- Availability of funds.
- Relevance of the proposed project to program priorities.

If the application is under consideration for funding, NIH will request "just-in-time" information from the applicant as described in the[NIH Grants Policy Statement Section 2.5.1. Just-in-Time Procedures](//grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/url_redirect.php?id=82418). This request is not a Notice of Award nor should it be construed to be an indicator of possible funding.

Prior to making an award, NIH reviews an applicants federal award history in SAM.gov to ensure sound business practices. An applicant can review and comment on any information in the Responsibility/Qualification records available in SAM.gov. NIH will consider any comments by the applicant in the Responsibility/Qualification records in SAM.gov to ascertain the applicants integrity, business ethics, and performance record of managing Federal awards per 2 CFR Part 200.206 Federal awarding agency review of risk posed by applicants. This provision will apply to all NIH grants and cooperative agreements except fellowships.
