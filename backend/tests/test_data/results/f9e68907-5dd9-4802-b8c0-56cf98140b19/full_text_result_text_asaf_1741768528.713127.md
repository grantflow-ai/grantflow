# In-Vermis Ultra-Long DNA Synthesis

## Abstract

This Proof of Concept project aims to establish a novel in-vermis platform utilizing _C. elegans_ for cost-effective, accurate synthesis of ultra-long DNA sequences (1-3 Mb). Current DNA synthesis methods are expensive and inefficient, limiting advances in synthetic biology and biotechnology. Our innovation lies in harnessing _C. elegans_' natural ability to form extrachromosomal arrays (ECAs) from injected DNA, creating a "cloning machine" for synthesizing and amplifying error-free DNA. We will decipher the rules governing ECA formation, understand the role of DNA repair mechanisms, and develop software for injection mix prediction, integrated with automated microinjection robotics. Long-read sequencing will characterize array composition and junction fidelity. The expected outcomes include a functional in-vermis DNA synthesis platform, a validated software tool for array design, and optimized protocols for ECA capture and sequencing. This project addresses a critical bottleneck in DNA synthesis, offering a single-step, scalable method for constructing entire genomes and synthetic pathways. ## Section 1a: The idea – Breakthrough Innovation potential

### 1a.i. Brief description of the idea to be taken to proof of concept

This PoC project addresses the critical bottleneck in biotechnology caused by limitations in current DNA synthesis methods, which are cumbersome, labor-intensive, and expensive, particularly for long and ultra-long DNA molecules. Our solution leverages the natural DNA concatemerization process in _C. elegans_ to create a one-step, cost-effective "cloning machine" for synthesizing and amplifying error-free ultra-long DNA sequences (1-3 Mb). This in-vermis approach offers a novel alternative to traditional chemical and enzymatic methods, potentially transforming synthetic biology, genomics, gene therapy, and bioengineering. The value proposition lies in its potential to significantly reduce the cost and time associated with synthesizing long DNA sequences, making it more accessible for research and development.

The core technology harnesses the worm's ability to form gigantic, repetitive, extrachromosomal arrays (ECAs) from injected DNA. Our innovation lies in utilizing this in vivo process for DNA synthesis, offering a unique approach compared to existing in vitro methods. The scientific basis is rooted in our understanding of _C. elegans_ genomics, molecular biology, and heredity, combined with advances in DNA sequencing and robotic microinjection. Applications include the construction of entire genomes, synthetic pathways, and long DNA sequences for various fields. Feasibility is supported by preliminary data demonstrating ECA formation from diverse DNA sequences and the influence of DNA repair pathways on the process. The high-risk/high-gain aspect is the potential to revolutionize DNA synthesis, although challenges remain in controlling and optimizing the ECA formation process.### 1a.ii. Demonstration of Breakthrough Innovation Potential

Our in-vermis DNA synthesis platform represents a breakthrough innovation with the potential to disrupt the current DNA synthesis market. The value proposition is clear: a significant reduction in cost and time for synthesizing ultra-long DNA sequences (1-3 Mb) compared to existing methods. Current chemical and enzymatic methods are cumbersome, labor-intensive, and expensive, especially for sequences exceeding 150-200 base pairs. Assembly techniques like Gibson assembly and polymerase cycling assembly, while useful, still require high-quality short fragments and can be time-consuming. Microarray-based parallel synthesis offers cost-effectiveness for large numbers of unique sequences but yields small quantities of each. Our approach bypasses these limitations by leveraging the natural DNA concatemerization process in _C. elegans_, creating a one-step "cloning machine."

Compared to the reported cost of $0.10 per base pair for synthesizing yeast chromosomes using traditional methods, which translates to $1.2 million per genome, our in-vermis approach aims to drastically reduce this cost. The synthesis of longer segments in one step using existing technologies can increase costs to over $10 million per yeast genome. Our technology offers a competitive advantage by potentially synthesizing ultra-long DNA at a fraction of the cost, making it more accessible for research and development. The scalability of our approach is supported by the feasibility of automated microinjection systems for _C. elegans_, which have already been developed.

The disruptive potential of our technology lies in its ability to transform synthetic biology, genomics, gene therapy, and bioengineering. By providing a cost-effective and scalable method for constructing entire genomes and synthetic pathways, we can accelerate synthetic genome engineering, enable personalized medicine applications, and open new frontiers in DNA-based data storage.
