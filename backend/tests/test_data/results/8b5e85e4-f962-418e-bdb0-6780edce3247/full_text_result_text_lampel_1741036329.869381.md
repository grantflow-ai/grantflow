## Section 1a: The idea – Breakthrough Innovation potential

### 1a.i. Brief description of the idea to be taken to proof of concept

The core concept centers on a novel approach to enhance the efficiency and stability of perovskite solar cells (PSCs) through surface passivation using a unique class of organic molecules. These molecules, designed with specific functional groups, interact with surface defects on the perovskite material, mitigating non-radiative recombination losses and improving overall device performance. The innovation lies in the molecular design, which allows for targeted passivation of specific defect sites, leading to enhanced charge carrier lifetimes and improved long-term stability, a critical factor for commercial viability. This surface modification strategy aims to address the key limitations currently hindering the widespread adoption of PSCs, namely their susceptibility to environmental degradation and relatively short operational lifetimes. The scientific basis for this approach is rooted in the understanding of defect chemistry and surface physics of perovskite materials, combined with principles of organic chemistry to tailor the passivating molecules.

The technical description involves synthesizing organic molecules with tailored functional groups capable of selectively binding to perovskite surface defects. These molecules are designed to passivate both positively and negatively charged defects, thereby reducing the density of trap states within the perovskite film. The passivation process involves applying a solution of the organic molecules onto the perovskite surface, followed by a controlled annealing step to facilitate molecular binding and defect passivation. The resulting modified perovskite films are then characterized using a range of techniques, including time-resolved photoluminescence (TRPL) spectroscopy, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), and atomic force microscopy (AFM), to assess the effectiveness of the passivation strategy. Device fabrication involves incorporating the passivated perovskite film into a standard solar cell architecture, followed by performance testing under simulated sunlight conditions. The novelty of this approach stems from the rational design of the organic molecules, enabling targeted passivation of specific defect types, as opposed to non-selective passivation methods. This targeted approach allows for a more efficient and effective reduction of non-radiative recombination losses, leading to improved device performance and stability.

The potential applications of this innovation extend to the broader field of renewable energy, offering a pathway to develop high-performance, stable, and cost-effective solar cells. The enhanced efficiency and stability of the passivated PSCs could lead to significant improvements in energy conversion rates, making solar energy a more competitive and sustainable energy source. Furthermore, the surface passivation strategy can be adapted to other perovskite-based optoelectronic devices, such as light-emitting diodes (LEDs) and photodetectors, expanding the potential impact of this research. #### Description of the Action and timescale – Work Plan

### Objective 1: Develop and optimize conditions for antibody-drug conjugate click reactions as model systems

Objective 1 aims to develop optimized click reaction conditions within LLPS condensates for antibody-drug conjugation, building on our 8-year peptide materials expertise. We will investigate CuAAC and SPAAC reactions, analyzing kinetics via UV-Vis, fluorescence spectroscopy, HPLC, and mass spectrometry. Condensate properties will be tuned to enhance reaction rates, guided by photoprotection system data (Science 2017, Angew. Chem. 2021). Optimization metrics include monomer conversion, product yield, and reduced solvent usage. Contingency plans utilize chemical crosslinkers, with dedicated NMR and peptide synthesizer access.

#### 1.1: Develop and optimize reaction conditions of two click reaction models involving formation of antibody-drug conjugates.

For CuAAC, we will monitor triazole formation via HPLC-MS, optimizing copper ligand concentration to minimize oxidation. SPAAC reactions will be monitored by fluorescence quenching using DBCO-modified antibodies. Reaction kinetics will be determined by UV-Vis spectroscopy with pseudo-first-order kinetic models. Optimal conditions require >95% conversion, <5% aggregation, with retained binding affinity. Stability assessment uses accelerated degradation studies and SEC-HPLC. Specificity evaluation employs LC-MS/MS of trypsin-digested conjugates and ELISA assays. Alternative catalysts or strain-promoted reagents will be explored if needed.

#### 1.2: Develop and optimize conditions for LLPS and condensate formation under reaction conditions.

Task 1.1 focuses on developing peptide-based condensate systems optimized for CuAAC and SPAAC click chemistry reactions. We will characterize LLPS via turbidity assays, DLS, and fluorescence microscopy to quantify droplet properties and protein partitioning. Stability assessments under varying NaCl (0-500 mM), temperature (4-37°C), and pH (5-9) will use time-resolved turbidity and microscopy. CD and FTIR will probe secondary structure. Sequence modification and PEG-based crosslinking will address instability issues.

### Objective 2: Characterization of reactant and product encapsulation

Objective 2 focuses on characterizing reactant and product encapsulation within peptide-based condensates for green chemistry applications, leveraging our 8-year expertise (Science 2017, Angew. Chem. 2021). We will quantify partitioning coefficients of hydrophobic organic molecules (e.g., drug molecules, linkers) using HPLC and fluorescence correlation spectroscopy, informed by the optimized conditions from Objective 1. Condensate architecture, using polyalanine-based peptides, will be optimized through sequence and crosslinking variation for selective product exclusion. Molecular partitioning parameters will be assessed via ITC and SPR, while condensate stability monitoring uses turbidity and microscopy. Technical challenges in maintaining condensate integrity during reactions will be addressed through crosslinking optimization, informing Objective 3's kinetics studies.

#### 2.1: Analysing the reactant and product encapsulation efficiency (EE) in condensates.

Encapsulation efficiency will be quantified using HPLC-MS (Agilent 1260 Infinity II) to measure reactant/product concentrations inside/outside condensates. Samples will be separated via centrifugation through 0.22 μm filters, with filtrate and resuspended condensate fractions analyzed by HPLC-MS using a C18 column and gradient elution (Science 2017). Partitioning coefficients will be calculated from concentration ratios, validated by fluorescence correlation spectroscopy (ISS Vista FCS system, 488 nm laser) using labeled species. Controls will include measurements without condensates to address background and matrix effects. Temporal stability will be assessed by monitoring encapsulation efficiency over 24 hours at 4°C and 25°C, with statistical analysis (ANOVA) to validate reproducibility across peptide compositions.

#### 2.2: Optimization of reactant EE in the condensate by tuning the peptide composition and reaction conditions.

Task 2.1 will optimize reactant encapsulation by varying peptide sequence, exploring hydrophobic/charged residue ratios from 1:3 to 3:1, and crosslinking density, informed by preliminary data on polyalanine-based materials. We will correlate sequence characteristics with condensate properties (size, stability, partitioning) using DLS, TEM, and confocal microscopy. Key parameters include salt concentration (0-500 mM), temperature (4-37°C), and pH (5-9), assessing their impact on condensate integrity during reaction progression. ITC and SPR will quantify molecular interactions driving reactant partitioning, targeting >80% encapsulation efficiency to ensure efficient reaction kinetics. Optimization protocols will be scaled using automated liquid handling, ensuring reproducibility across replicates (n=3).

#### 2.3: Optimization of product EE in the condensate by tuning the peptide composition and reaction conditions aiming at product exclusion following separation of the phases (dilute and dense) by centrifugation.

Task 2.3 will focus on selective product exclusion strategies through phase separation studies. We will optimize protocols for phase separation during product formation using polyalanine-based peptides, adjusting salt concentrations (0-500 mM) and temperature (4-37°C). Condensate stability during product accumulation will be addressed through PEG-based crosslinking. Product recovery involves centrifugation at 10,000 RCF with Tris buffer resuspension (50 mM, pH 7.4). Condensate integrity assessment uses DLS for droplet size distribution and microscopy for morphology.

### Objective 3: Condensate Reaction Characterization

Objective 3 examines reaction characterization within LLPS condensates for green chemistry and pharmaceutical applications. #### 3.1: Quantitative analysis of reaction kinetics, conversion and initial rate in condensates.

Kinetic analysis will employ UV-Vis spectroscopy (Agilent Cary 60) to monitor reaction progress, with rate constants determined via non-linear regression. Initial rates will be measured at substrate concentrations below Km, using Michaelis-Menten kinetics. HPLC-MS/MS (Agilent 1260 Infinity II, Zorbax Eclipse Plus C18) will quantify conversion rates and yields. Phase-specific effects will be compared between bulk solution and condensates, using confocal microscopy for intra-condensate concentrations. Reproducibility validated through triplicates (p < 0.05).

#### 3.2: Microscopy analysis of reaction kinetics to elucidate product localization in the two phases (dilute vs. dense phases).

For microscopy-based analysis, time-lapse confocal microscopy will track reaction dynamics, distinguishing between dense and dilute phases using fluorescently labeled reactants/products. We will use a Zeiss LSM 980 with Airyscan 2 for high-resolution imaging. Sample preparation involves encapsulating reactants within condensates formed on #1.5 glass coverslips. Image analysis will quantify product distribution using ImageJ, with co-localization analysis (Pearson's correlation coefficient, Manders' coefficients) to assess spatial relationships. Reaction progression will be tracked by measuring mean fluorescence intensity changes over time, correlating with HPLC data. FRAP, using a circular bleach region (5 μm diameter), will measure internal mobility on a 10-second timescale. Controls will include reactions without condensates.
